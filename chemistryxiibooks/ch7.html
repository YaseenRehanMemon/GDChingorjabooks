1. Alcohols (R-OH)

Alcohols are organic compounds containing a hydroxyl (-OH) functional group attached to a saturated (\(sp^3\)-hybridized) carbon atom.

Properties and Structure

The O-H bond in alcohols is highly polar, which allows them to form hydrogen bonds. This results in significantly higher boiling points and better water solubility compared to hydrocarbons of similar molecular weight.

Alcohols are weakly acidic and can react with active metals like sodium to release hydrogen gas.

Preparation of Alcohols

  • Hydration of Alkenes: Addition of water across a double bond in the presence of an acid catalyst.
  • Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds: Aldehydes are reduced to primary alcohols, and ketones are reduced to secondary alcohols, using reducing agents like \( \text{NaBH}_4 \) or \( \text{LiAlH}_4 \).
  • From Grignard Reagents: Reaction of a Grignard reagent with an aldehyde or ketone produces an alcohol.

Reactions of Alcohols

  • Dehydration: Elimination of water in the presence of a strong acid (like \(H_2SO_4\)) to form an alkene.
  • Oxidation: Primary alcohols oxidize to aldehydes, then to carboxylic acids. Secondary alcohols oxidize to ketones. Tertiary alcohols do not typically oxidize.

2. Phenols (Ar-OH)

Phenols are compounds where a hydroxyl (-OH) group is attached directly to an aromatic ring (like benzene).

Acidity of Phenols

Phenols are significantly more acidic than alcohols. This is because the negative charge on the resulting phenoxide ion (\(\text{C}_6\text{H}_5\text{O}^-\)) is stabilized by resonance, delocalizing the charge over the aromatic ring. Alcohols lack this resonance stabilization.

Preparation and Reactions

  • Preparation: Can be prepared from chlorobenzene (Dow's Process) or by hydrolysis of diazonium salts.
  • Reactions: The -OH group is an activating, ortho-para director for electrophilic aromatic substitution reactions like nitration and halogenation.

3. Ethers (R-O-R')

Ethers are compounds with two alkyl or aryl groups bonded to a single oxygen atom.

Properties and Structure

Ethers have a bent structure similar to water and alcohols. However, they cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves, resulting in much lower boiling points than alcohols of similar molecular weight. They are relatively unreactive and are often used as excellent nonpolar solvents.

Preparation of Ethers

Williamson Ether Synthesis

This is the most important method for preparing ethers. It is an SN2 reaction between a metal alkoxide (\(\text{RO}^-\text{Na}^+\)) and a primary alkyl halide (\(\text{R'X}\)).

\( \text{R}-\text{O}^-\text{Na}^+ + \text{R'}-\text{X} \rightarrow \text{R}-\text{O}-\text{R'} + \text{NaX} \)

Chapter Summary

  • Alcohols (R-OH): Contain a hydroxyl group on an \(sp^3\) carbon. Hydrogen bonding gives them high boiling points. They can be prepared from alkenes or carbonyls and undergo reactions like dehydration and oxidation.
  • Phenols (Ar-OH): Contain a hydroxyl group on an aromatic ring. They are more acidic than alcohols due to resonance stabilization of the phenoxide ion.
  • Ethers (R-O-R'): Contain an oxygen atom bonded to two carbon groups. They are relatively unreactive, have low boiling points, and are excellent solvents. The Williamson synthesis is a key method for their preparation.
