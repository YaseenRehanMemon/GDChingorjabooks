1. Carboxylic Acids (R-COOH)

Carboxylic acids are organic compounds that contain the carboxyl functional group (-COOH), which consists of a carbonyl group (\(C=O\)) and a hydroxyl group (-OH) attached to the same carbon atom.

Acidity and Physical Properties

Carboxylic acids are weak acids, but they are significantly more acidic than alcohols and phenols. The carboxyl group is highly polar and can form strong hydrogen bonds, leading to very high boiling points (often existing as dimers in the liquid phase) and good water solubility for smaller molecules.

Acidity Comparison

The strength of a carboxylic acid depends on the stability of its conjugate base, the carboxylate ion (\(\text{RCOO}^-\)).

  • The carboxylate ion is stabilized by resonance, which delocalizes the negative charge over both oxygen atoms.
  • Electron-withdrawing groups attached to the R group increase acidity, while electron-donating groups decrease it.

2. Preparation of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids can be synthesized through various oxidation and hydrolysis reactions.

Key Preparation Methods

  • Oxidation of Primary Alcohols and Aldehydes: Primary alcohols and aldehydes can be oxidized to carboxylic acids using strong oxidizing agents like potassium permanganate (\( \text{KMnO}_4 \)) or potassium dichromate (\( \text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7 \)).
  • Hydrolysis of Nitriles: Nitriles (\(R-C\equiv N\)) can be hydrolyzed in acidic or basic conditions to produce carboxylic acids.
  • Carbonation of Grignard Reagents: The reaction of a Grignard reagent with carbon dioxide (\(\text{CO}_2\)), followed by acidification, produces a carboxylic acid with one more carbon than the original Grignard reagent.

3. Reactions of Carboxylic Acids

Carboxylic acids can react at either the acidic proton of the -OH group or at the electrophilic carbonyl carbon.

  • As Acids: They react with bases to form carboxylate salts.
  • Reduction: They can be reduced to primary alcohols using a strong reducing agent like lithium aluminum hydride (\( \text{LiAlH}_4 \)).
  • Conversion to Derivatives: The -OH group can be replaced by other nucleophiles, leading to the formation of functional derivatives.

4. Carboxylic Acid Functional Derivatives

These compounds are formed by replacing the -OH group of a carboxylic acid with another group (-Z). The central reaction for interconverting them is nucleophilic acyl substitution.

Common Derivatives

  • Acyl Halides (R-CO-X): The most reactive derivatives. Formed by reacting a carboxylic acid with thionyl chloride (\(\text{SOCl}_2\)) or other halogenating agents.
  • Acid Anhydrides (R-CO-O-CO-R): Formed by the dehydration (removal of water) from two carboxylic acid molecules.
  • Esters (R-CO-OR'): Formed by the reaction of a carboxylic acid and an alcohol (Fischer Esterification). They often have pleasant, fruity odors.
  • Amides (R-CO-NH‚ÇÇ): The least reactive derivatives. Formed by the reaction of a carboxylic acid derivative (like an acyl chloride) with ammonia or an amine.

Chapter Summary

  • Carboxylic Acids (-COOH): Are weak acids with high boiling points due to hydrogen bonding.
  • Preparation: Can be made by oxidizing primary alcohols/aldehydes or by reacting Grignard reagents with \(CO_2\).
  • Reactions: They act as acids and can be reduced to primary alcohols. Their most important reaction is conversion into functional derivatives.
  • Functional Derivatives: Include acyl halides, anhydrides, esters, and amides, all of which can be interconverted via nucleophilic acyl substitution.
  • Reactivity of Derivatives: Acyl Halide > Anhydride > Ester > Amide.
