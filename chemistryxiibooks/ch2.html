1. Introduction to Transition Elements

Transition elements, also known as d-block elements, are located in the central block of the periodic table. They are essential in chemistry and industry due to their unique properties.

Key Definition: Transition Element

An element that has a partially filled d-orbital in its atomic state or in any of its common ionic states.

These elements exhibit properties that are intermediate between the highly reactive s-block metals and the mostly non-metallic p-block elements. They are grouped into four series based on which d-orbital is being filled: 3d, 4d, 5d, and 6d series.

2. Electronic Configuration

The general valence electronic configuration for d-block elements is \( (n-1)d^{1-10} ns^{1-2} \), where 'n' is the outermost shell. Electrons typically fill the \(ns\) orbital before the \( (n-1)d \) orbital.

Anomalous Configurations of Cr and Cu

Chromium (Cr) and Copper (Cu) are important exceptions to the standard filling order. This occurs because half-filled (\(d^5\)) and completely-filled (\(d^{10}\)) d-orbitals have extra stability.

  • Chromium (Cr, Z=24): Expected configuration is \([Ar] 4s^2 3d^4\). However, the actual configuration is \([Ar] 4s^1 3d^5\). One electron moves from the 4s orbital to the 3d orbital to achieve a stable, half-filled d-subshell.
  • Copper (Cu, Z=29): Expected configuration is \([Ar] 4s^2 3d^9\). The actual configuration is \([Ar] 4s^1 3d^{10}\). One electron moves from the 4s to the 3d orbital to achieve a very stable, completely filled d-subshell.

3. General Properties of Transition Elements

The partially filled d-orbitals give transition elements several characteristic properties.

Variable Oxidation States

Transition elements can show multiple oxidation states because the energy difference between the \(ns\) and \((n-1)d\) orbitals is very small. This allows electrons from both subshells to participate in bonding.

  • Manganese (Mn) shows the widest range of oxidation states, from +2 to +7.
  • The most common oxidation state is +2 (from losing two 4s electrons).

Formation of Colored Ions

Most transition metal compounds are colored. This is due to a phenomenon called d-d transition. When light passes through a solution containing a transition metal ion, the d-electrons absorb light of a specific wavelength to jump to a higher energy d-orbital. The color we see is the complementary color of the light that was absorbed.

Example: \([Cu(H_2O)_6]^{2+}\) solutions are blue because they absorb orange-red light, transmitting the complementary blue light.

Ions with empty (\(Sc^{3+}\)) or completely full (\(Zn^{2+}\)) d-orbitals are colorless because d-d transitions are not possible.

Catalytic Activity

Many transition metals and their compounds are excellent catalysts. This is due to their ability to:

  • Exhibit variable oxidation states, allowing them to act as electron carriers.
  • Provide a large surface area for reactants to adsorb onto.

Examples: Iron (Fe) in the Haber process for ammonia synthesis, and Nickel (Ni) for hydrogenation of alkenes.

Magnetic Properties

Substances are classified based on their behavior in a magnetic field:

  • Paramagnetic: Weakly attracted by a magnetic field. This occurs in substances with unpaired electrons.
  • Diamagnetic: Weakly repelled by a magnetic field. This occurs in substances where all electrons are paired.

Since most transition metal ions have unpaired d-electrons, they are paramagnetic.

4. Alloy Formation

What is an Alloy?

An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more elements, where at least one is a metal.

Transition metals readily form alloys because their atoms are similar in size. This allows atoms of one metal to easily replace atoms of another in the metallic crystal lattice.

Alloy Composition Key Uses
Stainless Steel Iron (Fe), Chromium (Cr), Nickel (Ni) Cutlery, surgical instruments, construction
Brass Copper (Cu), Zinc (Zn) Plumbing, musical instruments, decoration
Bronze Copper (Cu), Tin (Sn) Medals, statues, bearings

5. Coordination Compounds

Transition metals have a strong tendency to form coordination compounds (or complexes). These consist of a central metal atom or ion bonded to a group of surrounding molecules or ions, called ligands.

Basic Nomenclature Rules

  1. Cation before Anion: The positive ion (cation) is named first, followed by the negative ion (anion).
  2. Ligands First: Within the complex ion, name the ligands first in alphabetical order, then the metal.
  3. Ligand Naming:
    • Negative ligands end in "-o" (e.g., chloro, hydroxo).

  5. Metal Naming:
    • If the complex is a cation or neutral, the metal keeps its name.
    • If the complex is an anion, the metal's name ends in "-ate" (e.g., ferrate, cuprate).
  6. Oxidation State: The oxidation state of the central metal is written in Roman numerals in parentheses after the metal's name.

6. Chemistry of Key Transition Elements

Potassium Dichromate (\(K_2Cr_2O_7\))

A powerful oxidizing agent, especially in acidic solutions. The chromium is in its +6 oxidation state. In redox reactions, the orange dichromate ion (\(Cr_2O_7^{2-}\)) is reduced to the green chromium(III) ion (\(Cr^{3+}\)).

\( Cr_2O_7^{2-} + 14H^+ + 6e^- \rightarrow 2Cr^{3+} + 7H_2O \)

Potassium Permanganate (\(KMnO_4\))

An even stronger oxidizing agent. The manganese is in its +7 oxidation state. Its reaction product depends on the pH of the solution:

  • In acidic solution: The purple permanganate ion (\(MnO_4^-\)) is reduced to the colorless manganese(II) ion (\(Mn^{2+}\)).
  • In neutral/basic solution: It is reduced to form a brown solid, manganese(IV) oxide (\(MnO_2\)).

\( MnO_4^- + 8H^+ + 5e^- \rightarrow Mn^{2+} + 4H_2O \)

Chapter Summary

  • Transition Elements: d-block elements with partially filled d-orbitals.
  • Electronic Configuration: General form is \( (n-1)d^{1-10} ns^{1-2} \). Cr and Cu are exceptions due to the stability of half-filled and fully-filled d-orbitals.
  • Key Properties: They exhibit variable oxidation states, form colored compounds (due to d-d transitions), have catalytic activity, and are often paramagnetic (due to unpaired electrons).
  • Alloys: Formed easily due to similar atomic sizes (e.g., steel, brass).
  • Coordination Compounds: Formed between a central metal ion and surrounding ligands. Their names follow specific IUPAC rules.
  • Important Compounds: \(K_2Cr_2O_7\) and \(KMnO_4\) are powerful oxidizing agents used widely in chemistry.
