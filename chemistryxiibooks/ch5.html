1. Introduction to Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds composed entirely of carbon and hydrogen atoms. They are the simplest organic compounds and form the backbone of many complex organic molecules.

Classification of Hydrocarbons

  • Aliphatic: Compounds with carbon atoms joined in straight chains, branched chains, or non-aromatic rings.
    • Alkanes: Single C-C bonds (saturated).
    • Alkenes: At least one C=C double bond (unsaturated).
    • Alkynes: At least one C‚â°C triple bond (unsaturated).
  • Aromatic: Compounds that contain one or more benzene rings or other ring systems with delocalized pi electrons.

2. Alkanes (Saturated Hydrocarbons)

Alkanes contain only single carbon-carbon bonds. They are known as "saturated" because they have the maximum possible number of hydrogen atoms for their carbon skeleton. The general formula is \( C_nH_{2n+2} \).

Structure and Reactivity

Alkanes have a tetrahedral geometry around each carbon atom due to \(sp^3\) hybridization. They are generally unreactive towards polar reagents because C-C and C-H bonds are nonpolar. Their primary reaction is free-radical substitution, typically initiated by UV light.

Free-Radical Substitution of Methane

This reaction occurs in three steps:

  1. Initiation: UV light splits a chlorine molecule into two reactive chlorine free radicals. \( \text{Cl}_2 \xrightarrow{UV} 2\text{Cl}\cdot \)
  2. Propagation: A chlorine radical reacts with methane, creating a methyl radical, which then reacts with another chlorine molecule. This is a chain reaction.
  3. Termination: Two free radicals combine to end the chain.

3. Alkenes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

Alkenes contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond (\( C=C \)). The general formula for an alkene with one double bond is \( C_nH_{2n} \).

Structure and Reactivity

The carbon atoms in a double bond are \(sp^2\) hybridized, resulting in a trigonal planar geometry. The double bond consists of one strong sigma (\(\sigma\)) bond and one weaker pi (\(\pi\)) bond. The exposed electrons of the pi bond make alkenes much more reactive than alkanes. Their characteristic reaction is electrophilic addition.

Key Reactions of Alkenes

  • Hydrogenation: Addition of \(H_2\) across the double bond to form an alkane.
  • Halogenation: Addition of a halogen (e.g., \(Br_2\)) to form a dihaloalkane.
  • Hydrohalogenation: Addition of HX (e.g., HBr) following Markovnikov's rule.
  • Hydration: Addition of water (\(H_2O\)) to form an alcohol.

Geometric Isomerism (Cis-Trans)

Due to the restricted rotation around the C=C double bond, alkenes can exhibit cis-trans isomerism.

  • Cis isomer: Similar groups are on the same side of the double bond.
  • Trans isomer: Similar groups are on opposite sides of the double bond.

4. Alkynes (Unsaturated Hydrocarbons)

Alkynes contain at least one carbon-carbon triple bond (\( C\equiv C \)). The general formula for an alkyne with one triple bond is \( C_nH_{2n-2} \).

Structure and Acidity

The carbon atoms in a triple bond are \(sp\) hybridized, leading to a linear geometry. The C-H bond in terminal alkynes (where the triple bond is at the end of a chain) is unusually acidic because the \(sp\) hybridized carbon is more electronegative, making it easier to remove the proton (\(H^+\)).

5. Aromatic Hydrocarbons (Benzene)

The simplest aromatic hydrocarbon is benzene, \(C_6H_6\). Its structure is a planar hexagonal ring of six carbon atoms, with a delocalized system of six pi electrons shared across the entire ring.

Structure and Stability

This delocalization of electrons, known as resonance, makes benzene exceptionally stable. Because of this stability, benzene does not undergo addition reactions like alkenes. Its characteristic reaction is electrophilic aromatic substitution, where a hydrogen atom on the ring is replaced by an electrophile, preserving the stable aromatic system.

Key Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution Reactions

  • Halogenation: Substitution of H with a halogen (e.g., Br), catalyzed by a Lewis acid.
  • Nitration: Substitution of H with a nitro group (\(-\text{NO}_2\)).
  • Friedel-Crafts Alkylation: Substitution of H with an alkyl group (\(-R\)).

Chapter Summary

  • Hydrocarbons: Compounds of only carbon and hydrogen, classified as alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, and aromatics.
  • Alkanes: Saturated (\(sp^3\)), unreactive, undergo free-radical substitution.
  • Alkenes: Unsaturated (\(sp^2\)), contain C=C bonds, undergo electrophilic addition, and can show cis-trans isomerism.
  • Alkynes: Unsaturated (\(sp\)), contain C‚â°C bonds, are linear, and terminal alkynes are weakly acidic.
  • Aromatic (Benzene): A stable planar ring with delocalized pi electrons. Undergoes electrophilic substitution, not addition.
