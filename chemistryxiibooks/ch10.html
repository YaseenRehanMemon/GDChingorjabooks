1. Introduction to Biochemistry

Biochemistry is the study of the chemical processes within and relating to living organisms. It focuses on the structure, function, and interaction of the four major classes of biological macromolecules.

2. Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or substances that yield these compounds upon hydrolysis. They are the primary source of energy for living organisms and also serve structural roles.

Classification of Carbohydrates

  • Monosaccharides: The simplest sugars (e.g., glucose, fructose). They are the monomers for larger carbohydrates.
  • Disaccharides: Two monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic bond (e.g., sucrose, lactose).
  • Polysaccharides: Large polymers of monosaccharides (e.g., starch, glycogen, cellulose).

Role in Health and Disease

Glucose is the main fuel for our cells. Its level in the blood is tightly regulated. An inability to regulate blood glucose leads to diabetes. Dietary fiber (like cellulose) is a carbohydrate that is important for digestive health, even though we cannot digest it.

3. Proteins

Proteins are large, complex polymers made from amino acid monomers linked by peptide bonds. They perform a vast array of functions in the cell.

Protein Structure

A protein's function is determined by its unique three-dimensional shape, which is described at four levels:

  1. Primary: The linear sequence of amino acids.
  2. Secondary: Local folding into structures like the \(\alpha\)-helix and \(\beta\)-pleated sheet, stabilized by hydrogen bonds.
  3. Tertiary: The overall 3D shape of a single polypeptide chain, determined by interactions between R-groups.
  4. Quaternary: The arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits to form a single functional protein (e.g., hemoglobin).

Functions of Proteins

  • Enzymes: Catalyze biochemical reactions.
  • Structural: Provide support (e.g., collagen, keratin).
  • Transport: Carry substances (e.g., hemoglobin transports oxygen).
  • Regulatory: Act as hormones to regulate body functions (e.g., insulin).

4. Lipids

Lipids are a diverse group of naturally occurring molecules that are defined by their insolubility in water but solubility in nonpolar organic solvents. They include fats, oils, waxes, and steroids.

Classification of Lipids

  • Triglycerides (Fats and Oils): Esters of glycerol and three fatty acids. They are the main form of long-term energy storage. Saturated fats have no C=C double bonds, while unsaturated fats have one or more.
  • Phospholipids: A major component of cell membranes, with a hydrophilic head and two hydrophobic tails.
  • Steroids: Lipids characterized by a specific four-ring carbon structure (e.g., cholesterol, testosterone, estrogen).

Saponification

This is the hydrolysis of a fat or oil with a strong base (like NaOH) to produce glycerol and the salt of a fatty acid, which is commonly known as soap.

5. Minerals of Biological Significance

Minerals are inorganic elements required by the body for various physiological functions. They are essential for maintaining health.

Key Minerals and Their Roles

  • Calcium (Ca): Essential for strong bones and teeth, muscle contraction, and nerve function.
  • Iron (Fe): A crucial component of hemoglobin, which is necessary for oxygen transport in the blood.
  • Phosphorus (P): Important for bone health and a key component of DNA, RNA, and ATP.
  • Zinc (Zn): Plays a role in wound healing, immune function, and the senses of taste and smell.

Chapter Summary

  • Biochemistry: The study of the molecules of life: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids.
  • Carbohydrates: Serve as the primary energy source. They range from simple monosaccharides (glucose) to complex polysaccharides (starch).
  • Proteins: Polymers of amino acids that perform a huge variety of tasks, from catalyzing reactions (enzymes) to providing structure. Their function is dictated by their 3D shape.
  • Lipids: Water-insoluble molecules used for long-term energy storage (fats), cell membranes (phospholipids), and as hormones (steroids).
  • Minerals: Inorganic elements like calcium and iron that are essential for various bodily functions.
