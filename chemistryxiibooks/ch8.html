1. Introduction to Carbonyl Compounds

Aldehydes and ketones are characterized by the presence of a carbonyl group (\( C=O \)). The main difference lies in what is attached to the carbonyl carbon.

Aldehydes vs. Ketones

  • Aldehyde: The carbonyl carbon is bonded to at least one hydrogen atom. The carbonyl group is at the end of a carbon chain. (General formula: R-CHO)
  • Ketone: The carbonyl carbon is bonded to two other carbon atoms. The carbonyl group is within a carbon chain. (General formula: R-CO-R')

The carbonyl group's carbon-oxygen double bond is polar, with the carbon being electrophilic (\(\delta+\)) and the oxygen being nucleophilic (\(\delta-\)). This polarity dictates the chemistry of these compounds.

2. Preparation of Aldehydes and Ketones

Several methods can be used to synthesize these important functional groups.

Key Preparation Methods

  • Oxidation of Alcohols:
    • Primary alcohols are oxidized to aldehydes using mild oxidizing agents like Pyridinium Chlorochromate (PCC).
    • Secondary alcohols are oxidized to ketones using stronger agents like \( \text{K}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7 \).
  • Ozonolysis of Alkenes: Cleavage of a C=C double bond with ozone (\(O_3\)) followed by a reducing agent (like zinc) yields aldehydes and/or ketones.
  • Hydration of Alkynes: Addition of water to an alkyne, catalyzed by acid and mercury(II) salts, forms an unstable enol that rearranges to a ketone (or acetaldehyde from ethyne).

3. Key Reactions of Aldehydes and Ketones

The primary reaction of carbonyl compounds is nucleophilic addition, where a nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon.

Nucleophilic Addition Reactions

  • Addition of HCN: Forms a cyanohydrin.
  • Addition of Grignard Reagents: Forms alcohols (formaldehyde \(\rightarrow\) \(1^\circ\) alcohol; other aldehydes \(\rightarrow\) \(2^\circ\) alcohol; ketones \(\rightarrow\) \(3^\circ\) alcohol).
  • Addition of Alcohols: Forms hemiacetals and acetals, which act as protecting groups for the carbonyl.

Relative Reactivity

Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones towards nucleophilic attack. This is due to two factors:

  1. Steric Hindrance: The single hydrogen on an aldehyde's carbonyl carbon creates less crowding than the two alkyl groups on a ketone.
  2. Electronic Effects: Alkyl groups are electron-donating, which reduces the partial positive charge on the carbonyl carbon of ketones, making it less electrophilic.

Oxidation and Reduction

  • Oxidation: Aldehydes are easily oxidized to carboxylic acids by various oxidizing agents. Ketones are generally resistant to oxidation.
  • Reduction: Both aldehydes and ketones are reduced to alcohols using agents like \( \text{NaBH}_4 \) or \( \text{LiAlH}_4 \).

4. Laboratory Tests to Distinguish Aldehydes and Ketones

The difference in oxidation potential is the basis for simple chemical tests to distinguish between aldehydes and ketones.

Key Tests

  • Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror Test): Aldehydes react with Tollens' reagent (\( [\text{Ag(NH}_3)_2]^+ \)) to form a silver mirror on the inside of the test tube. Ketones do not react.
  • Fehling's or Benedict's Test: Aldehydes react with the blue copper(II) solution in these reagents to form a red precipitate of copper(I) oxide (\(\text{Cu}_2\text{O}\)). Ketones do not react.

Chapter Summary

  • Carbonyl Group: Aldehydes (R-CHO) and ketones (R-CO-R') both contain the \(C=O\) functional group.
  • Reactivity: The polar \(C=O\) bond makes the carbon electrophilic, leading to nucleophilic addition as the characteristic reaction. Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones.
  • Preparation: Commonly prepared by oxidizing alcohols or through ozonolysis of alkenes.
  • Reactions: They undergo nucleophilic addition with Grignard reagents and HCN, and can be reduced to alcohols.
  • Distinguishing Test: Aldehydes can be easily oxidized and give positive results with Tollens' and Fehling's reagents, while ketones do not.
