1. Introduction to Representative Elements

The representative elements are the elements in the s-block and p-block of the periodic table. They are called "representative" because they exhibit a wide range of chemical and physical properties and follow periodic trends more consistently than other elements.

Key Groups

  • s-Block: Groups 1 (alkali metals) and 2 (alkaline earth metals). Their valence electrons are in the s-orbital.
  • p-Block: Groups 13 to 18. Their valence electrons are in the p-orbitals. This block contains metals, metalloids, and nonmetals.

2. s-Block Elements

The s-block elements are highly reactive metals known for forming ionic compounds.

Group 1: Alkali Metals

Alkali metals (Li, Na, K, etc.) have a single valence electron (\(ns^1\)), which they readily lose to form a +1 ion. They are soft, have low densities, and react vigorously with water to produce hydrogen gas and a metal hydroxide.

Example Reaction: Sodium with Water

\( 2\text{Na}(s) + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}(aq) + \text{H}_2(g) \)

Group 2: Alkaline Earth Metals

Alkaline earth metals (Be, Mg, Ca, etc.) have two valence electrons (\(ns^2\)) and tend to lose both to form a +2 ion. They are harder and less reactive than alkali metals but are still considered reactive metals.

3. p-Block Elements

The p-block is the most diverse block, containing a mix of nonmetals, metalloids, and metals.

Group 17: Halogens

Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I) are highly reactive nonmetals with seven valence electrons (\(ns^2np^5\)). They need only one more electron to complete their octet, so they are powerful oxidizing agents and readily form -1 ions (halides).

Group 18: Noble Gases

Noble gases (He, Ne, Ar, etc.) have completely filled valence shells (\(ns^2np^6\)). This stable electron configuration makes them chemically inert and unreactive under normal conditions. They exist as monatomic gases.

4. Periodic Trends in Representative Elements

The properties of representative elements change predictably across a period and down a group.

Key Periodic Trends

  • Atomic Radius: Decreases from left to right across a period (due to increasing nuclear charge) and increases down a group (due to adding new electron shells).
  • Ionization Energy: The energy required to remove an electron. It increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Electronegativity: An atom's ability to attract electrons in a bond. It increases across a period and decreases down a group.
  • Metallic Character: Decreases across a period and increases down a group.

Chapter Summary

  • Representative Elements: The s-block and p-block elements, which show clear periodic trends.
  • s-Block (Groups 1 & 2): Highly reactive metals (alkali and alkaline earth metals) that lose electrons to form cations.
  • p-Block (Groups 13-18): A diverse group including metals, metalloids, and nonmetals like the highly reactive halogens and the inert noble gases.
  • Periodic Trends: Properties like atomic radius, ionization energy, and electronegativity vary predictably across periods and down groups, helping to explain the chemical behavior of these elements.
