1. Alkyl Halides (Haloalkanes)

Alkyl halides are organic compounds in which a halogen atom (F, Cl, Br, or I) is bonded to an \(sp^3\)-hybridized carbon atom of an alkyl group. Their general formula is R-X.

Structure and Reactivity

The carbon-halogen bond (\(C-X\)) is polar because halogens are more electronegative than carbon. This creates a partial positive charge (\(\delta+\)) on the carbon and a partial negative charge (\(\delta-\)) on the halogen. This polarity makes the carbon atom an electrophilic site, susceptible to attack by nucleophiles.

Classification of Alkyl Halides

  • Primary (\(1^\circ\)): The carbon bonded to the halogen is attached to one other carbon.
  • Secondary (\(2^\circ\)): The carbon bonded to the halogen is attached to two other carbons.
  • Tertiary (\(3^\circ\)): The carbon bonded to the halogen is attached to three other carbons.

2. Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions (SN)

This is the hallmark reaction of alkyl halides. A nucleophile (a species with a lone pair of electrons) replaces the halogen atom (the leaving group).

\( \text{R}-\text{X} + \text{Nu:}^- \rightarrow \text{R}-\text{Nu} + \text{X:}^- \)

SN2 Mechanism (Bimolecular)

  • One-Step Process: Bond-making and bond-breaking occur simultaneously.
  • Backside Attack: The nucleophile attacks the carbon from the side opposite the leaving group.
  • Rate Law: Rate = k[Alkyl Halide][Nucleophile]. (Second-order kinetics).
  • Favored by: Primary (\(1^\circ\)) alkyl halides, strong nucleophiles, and less steric hindrance.

SN1 Mechanism (Unimolecular)

  • Two-Step Process: First, the leaving group departs to form a stable carbocation intermediate. Second, the nucleophile attacks the carbocation.
  • Rate-Determining Step: The first step (formation of the carbocation) is the slow step.
  • Rate Law: Rate = k[Alkyl Halide]. (First-order kinetics).
  • Favored by: Tertiary (\(3^\circ\)) alkyl halides, weak nucleophiles, and polar protic solvents that stabilize the carbocation.

3. Elimination Reactions

In an elimination reaction, atoms are removed from adjacent carbons to form a double bond (an alkene). These reactions compete with substitution reactions. Elimination is favored by strong, bulky bases and high temperatures.

  • E2 Mechanism: A one-step process where the base removes a proton at the same time the leaving group departs. Favored by \(1^\circ\) and \(2^\circ\) alkyl halides and strong bases.
  • E1 Mechanism: A two-step process that proceeds through a carbocation intermediate (just like SN1). Favored by \(3^\circ\) alkyl halides and weak bases.

4. Grignard Reagents (Organometallic Compounds)

A Grignard reagent is an organometallic compound with the general formula R-Mg-X. It is prepared by reacting an alkyl halide with magnesium metal in anhydrous ether.

Reactivity of Grignard Reagents

The C-Mg bond is highly polar, making the carbon atom strongly nucleophilic and basic. Grignard reagents are powerful tools for forming new carbon-carbon bonds.

They react vigorously with any source of protons (like water, alcohols) to form an alkane, and they attack the electrophilic carbon of carbonyl groups (aldehydes, ketones, esters).

5. Amines

Amines are organic derivatives of ammonia (\(\text{NH}_3\)) where one or more hydrogen atoms are replaced by alkyl or aryl groups.

Structure and Basicity

The nitrogen atom in an amine is \(sp^3\)-hybridized and has a lone pair of electrons. This lone pair makes amines basic (they can accept a proton) and nucleophilic.

  • Basicity Trend: In the gas phase, \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ > \text{NH}_3\). In aqueous solution, the trend is generally \(2^\circ > 1^\circ \approx 3^\circ > \text{NH}_3\).

Preparation and Reactions

  • Preparation: Can be prepared by alkylation of ammonia or reduction of nitriles and amides.
  • Reactions: Amines act as nucleophiles, reacting with alkyl halides, aldehydes, ketones, and acyl halides (to form amides).

Chapter Summary

  • Alkyl Halides (R-X): Contain a polar C-X bond, making the carbon electrophilic. They undergo nucleophilic substitution and elimination reactions.
  • Nucleophilic Substitution: Can occur via a one-step SN2 mechanism (favored by \(1^\circ\) R-X) or a two-step SN1 mechanism via a carbocation (favored by \(3^\circ\) R-X).
  • Elimination Reactions: Compete with substitution and form alkenes. Favored by strong bases and high heat.
  • Grignard Reagents (R-Mg-X): Powerful nucleophiles and bases used to form new C-C bonds.
  • Amines (R-NH‚ÇÇ): Organic bases containing a nitrogen with a lone pair. They are good nucleophiles and react with various electrophiles.
