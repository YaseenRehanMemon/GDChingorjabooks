Frames of Reference

All motion is relative. The speed and direction of an object depend on the frame of reference from which it is observed. A frame of reference is a coordinate system used to describe motion.

Inertial Frame of Reference

A frame that is not accelerating. In an inertial frame, Newton's First Law (the law of inertia) holds true. An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in uniform motion unless a force acts on it.

Non-Inertial Frame of Reference

A frame that is accelerating. In a non-inertial frame, Newton's laws do not apply in their simple form, and "fictitious forces" (like the centrifugal force) appear to act.

The Special Theory of Relativity

In 1905, Albert Einstein published his Special Theory of Relativity, which deals with motion in inertial frames of reference. It is based on two revolutionary postulates.

The Two Postulates of Special Relativity

  1. The Principle of Relativity: The laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames of reference. There is no absolute motion or "preferred" frame.
  2. The Constancy of the Speed of Light: The speed of light in a vacuum, \(c\), is the same for all inertial observers, regardless of the motion of the light source or the observer.

Consequences of Special Relativity

Einstein's postulates lead to some bizarre and counter-intuitive conclusions about the nature of space and time, especially for objects moving at speeds close to the speed of light (\(c\)).

Time Dilation

Moving clocks run slow. An observer will measure a longer time interval on a clock that is moving relative to them than on a clock at rest in their own frame.

\[ \Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \gamma \Delta t_0 \]

\(\Delta t_0\) is the "proper time" (time measured in the clock's own rest frame).

Length Contraction

Moving objects are measured to be shorter in their direction of motion. An observer will measure a shorter length for an object moving relative to them than for an identical object at rest.

\[ L = L_0 \sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2} = \frac{L_0}{\gamma} \]

\(L_0\) is the "proper length" (length measured in the object's own rest frame).

Mass-Energy Equivalence

Mass and energy are two different aspects of the same thing. Energy has mass, and mass has energy. This relationship is described by the most famous equation in physics.

\[ E_0 = mc^2 \]

This equation represents the "rest energy" (\(E_0\)) of an object, the immense amount of energy locked within its mass (\(m\)). The total energy of a moving object is \(E = \gamma mc^2\).

The General Theory of Relativity

Einstein's General Theory of Relativity (1915) is a theory of gravitation. It extends Special Relativity to include non-inertial (accelerating) frames of reference.

Gravity as Spacetime Curvature

The core idea of General Relativity is that gravity is not a force, but a consequence of the curvature of a four-dimensional continuum called spacetime. Mass and energy tell spacetime how to curve, and the curvature of spacetime tells mass how to move.

  • Equivalence Principle: There is no way for an observer to distinguish locally between gravity and acceleration.
  • Consequences: This theory predicts phenomena like the bending of starlight by gravity (gravitational lensing), the existence of black holes, and gravitational time dilation (clocks run slower in stronger gravitational fields).

Chapter Summary

Einstein's theories of relativity fundamentally changed our understanding of space, time, and gravity.

  • Special Relativity is based on two postulates: the laws of physics are the same for all inertial observers, and the speed of light is constant for all inertial observers.
  • This leads to strange consequences for objects at high speeds: time dilation (moving clocks run slow), length contraction (moving objects are shorter), and mass-energy equivalence (\(E=mc^2\)).
  • The speed of light, \(c\), is the ultimate speed limit in the universe for any object with mass.
  • General Relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy.
  • This modern view of gravity explains the orbits of planets and predicts phenomena like black holes and gravitational lensing.
