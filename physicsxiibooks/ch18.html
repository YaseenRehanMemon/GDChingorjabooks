Sources of Magnetic Fields

A magnetic field (\(\vec{B}\)) is a region of space where a magnetic force can be detected. Unlike electric fields which are created by static charges, magnetic fields are created by moving charges (i.e., electric currents).

Permanent Magnets

In materials like iron, the coordinated spinning of electrons in atoms creates tiny magnetic domains. When these domains align, a permanent macroscopic magnetic field is produced.

Electric Currents

Any wire carrying an electric current is surrounded by a magnetic field. The direction of the field can be found using the Right-Hand Rule: point your thumb in the direction of the current, and your fingers will curl in the direction of the magnetic field lines.

Magnetic Flux (\(\Phi_B\)) and Flux Density

Magnetic Flux is a measure of the total number of magnetic field lines passing through a given area. It is the scalar product of the magnetic field and area. Magnetic Flux Density is another term for the magnetic field strength (\(B\)), measured in Tesla (T).

\[ \Phi_B = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A} = BA \cos(\theta) \]

Force on a Moving Charge

A magnetic field exerts a force on a charged particle, but only if the particle is moving. This force is known as the Lorentz force.

Magnetic Force on a Charge

\[ \vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \]

The magnitude of the force is:

\[ F = |q|vB \sin(\theta) \]

\(q\) = Charge of the particle
\(v\) = Velocity of the particle
\(B\) = Magnetic field strength
\(\theta\) = Angle between the velocity and the magnetic field

Important Properties

  • The force is always perpendicular to both the velocity (\(\vec{v}\)) and the magnetic field (\(\vec{B}\)).
  • Because the force is always perpendicular to the direction of motion, a magnetic field does no work on a charged particle and cannot change its kinetic energy or speed.
  • A magnetic field can only change the direction of a particle's motion, causing it to move in a circular or helical path. This principle is used in particle accelerators and for determining the charge-to-mass ratio (e/m) of an electron.

Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

Since a current is a collection of moving charges, a wire carrying a current in a magnetic field will experience a magnetic force.

Magnetic Force on a Wire

\[ \vec{F} = I(\vec{L} \times \vec{B}) \]

The magnitude of the force is:

\[ F = BIL \sin(\theta) \]

\(I\) = Current in the wire
\(L\) = Length of the wire in the field
\(B\) = Magnetic field strength

The direction of the force is given by Fleming's Left-Hand Rule. This effect is the operating principle behind electric motors.

Ampere's Law

Ampere's Law relates the magnetic field in a closed loop to the electric current passing through that loop. It is the magnetic equivalent of Gauss's Law in electrostatics.

Solenoids and Toroids

Ampere's law is particularly useful for calculating the magnetic field inside specific structures:

  • Solenoid: A long coil of wire. It produces a strong, uniform magnetic field inside the coil. \(B = \mu_0 n I\), where n is the number of turns per unit length.
  • Toroid: A solenoid bent into a donut shape. It confines the magnetic field entirely within its core.

Measuring Instruments: The Galvanometer

A galvanometer is a device that detects small electric currents. It works on the principle that a current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a torque (\(\tau = NIAB\sin\theta\)). This torque causes the coil to rotate against a spring, and the amount of deflection is proportional to the current.

Conversions

  • Ammeter: A galvanometer can be converted into an ammeter (to measure current) by connecting a low-resistance shunt resistor in parallel with it.
  • Voltmeter: It can be converted into a voltmeter (to measure voltage) by connecting a high-resistance multiplier resistor in series with it.

Chapter Summary

Magnetic fields are produced by moving charges and exert forces on other moving charges.

  • Sources of \(\vec{B}\): Electric currents and permanent magnets.
  • Force on a moving charge: \(F = |q|vB \sin(\theta)\). This force is always perpendicular to both \(\vec{v}\) and \(\vec{B}\) and does no work.
  • Force on a current-carrying wire: \(F = BIL \sin(\theta)\). This is the principle behind electric motors.
  • Magnetic Flux (\(\Phi_B\)) is the measure of field lines through an area. The magnetic field strength (\(B\)) is also called flux density.
  • Ampere's Law relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the current passing through it. It is used to find the field in solenoids and toroids.
  • A galvanometer detects current via the torque on a coil in a magnetic field. It can be converted into an ammeter (with a parallel shunt) or a voltmeter (with a series multiplier).
