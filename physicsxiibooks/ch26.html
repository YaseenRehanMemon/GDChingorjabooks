Atomic Spectra

When atoms are excited (e.g., by heating), they emit light. However, they don't emit a continuous rainbow. Instead, they emit light only at specific, discrete wavelengths. This unique pattern of wavelengths is called an atomic spectrum, and it acts as a "fingerprint" for each element.

Emission Spectra

A series of bright lines on a dark background, corresponding to the specific frequencies of light emitted by an excited gas.

Absorption Spectra

A continuous spectrum with dark lines, created when light passes through a cool gas. The dark lines correspond to the same frequencies that the gas would emit if it were hot.

The existence of these discrete spectral lines was a major puzzle for classical physics and a key clue that led to the development of quantum theory.

Bohr's Model of the Hydrogen Atom

In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a quantum model for the hydrogen atom that successfully explained its observed spectrum. He blended classical physics with new quantum ideas.

Bohr's Postulates

  1. Electrons orbit the nucleus in specific, discrete circular orbits called stationary states.
  2. While in a stationary state, an electron does not radiate energy, contrary to classical predictions.
  3. An electron can "jump" from a higher energy orbit to a lower energy orbit by emitting a photon of light. The energy of the photon is exactly equal to the energy difference between the two orbits.

Photon Emission

\[ E_{\text{photon}} = hf = E_{\text{initial}} - E_{\text{final}} \]

Quantized Radii and Energy Levels

Bohr's model predicts that both the radii of the electron orbits and their corresponding energy levels are quantized, meaning they can only take on specific, discrete values determined by an integer \(n\), the principal quantum number.

X-Rays

X-rays are a form of high-energy electromagnetic radiation. They are produced when high-speed electrons are suddenly stopped by a metal target.

Production and Inner-Shell Transitions

When a fast-moving electron strikes a target atom, it can knock out an electron from one of the atom's inner shells (like the K-shell). This creates a vacancy. An electron from a higher energy shell then drops down to fill this vacancy, emitting the energy difference as a high-energy X-ray photon. Because these transitions involve specific inner-shell energy levels, the resulting X-rays have discrete, characteristic wavelengths for each element.

Lasers

LASER stands for Light Amplification by Stimulated Emission of Radiation. A laser is a device that produces a beam of light with unique properties.

Properties of Laser Light

  • Monochromatic: Consists of a single wavelength (one color).
  • Coherent: All the light waves are in phase with each other.
  • Directional: The light is emitted in a very narrow, concentrated beam that spreads out very little.

How Lasers Work: The Key Concepts

  • Stimulated Emission: An incoming photon can stimulate an already excited atom to drop to a lower energy level, emitting a second photon that is identical in phase, frequency, and direction to the first. This is the "amplification" part of the laser.
  • Population Inversion: To achieve amplification, there must be more atoms in a higher energy (excited) state than in a lower energy state. This is an unnatural condition called a population inversion, which is created by "pumping" energy into the system.
  • Metastable State: A special type of excited state with a longer-than-usual lifetime, which allows atoms to accumulate in that state and makes population inversion possible.

Chapter Summary

Atomic physics explores the quantum structure of atoms, explaining how they interact with light and produce unique spectra.

  • Atoms emit and absorb light at discrete frequencies, creating unique line spectra that act as an elemental "fingerprint."
  • Bohr's Model successfully explained the hydrogen spectrum by postulating that electrons exist in quantized energy levels and emit photons when they transition between these levels.
  • X-rays are high-energy photons produced when electrons make transitions to the innermost shells of an atom after an inner electron has been knocked out.
  • Lasers produce coherent, monochromatic, and directional light through the process of stimulated emission.
  • Laser operation requires creating a population inversion, a condition where more atoms are in an excited state than a lower state, often involving a metastable state.
