Core Concepts of Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the study of heat, work, and energy, and the relationships between them. We define a system as the collection of objects we are interested in, and the surroundings as everything else.

Internal Energy (U)

The total energy contained within a system. It is the sum of the random kinetic and potential energies of all its molecules. For an ideal gas, internal energy depends only on its temperature.

Heat (Q)

Heat is the transfer of energy between systems due to a temperature difference. Heat flows spontaneously from a hotter object to a colder one.

Work (W)

Work is the transfer of energy by mechanical means. In thermodynamics, we often consider the work done by a gas as it expands or is compressed. Work done by the system is positive; work done on the system is negative.

The First Law of Thermodynamics

The First Law is a statement of the conservation of energy applied to thermodynamic systems. It establishes a precise relationship between heat, work, and the change in a system's internal energy.

The First Law Equation

\[ \Delta U = Q - W \]

\(\Delta U\) = Change in the system's internal energy
\(Q\) = Net heat added to the system
\(W\) = Net work done by the system

Sign Convention

  • Q is positive (+) when heat is added to the system.
  • Q is negative (-) when heat is removed from the system.
  • W is positive (+) when work is done by the system (e.g., expansion of a gas).
  • W is negative (-) when work is done on the system (e.g., compression of a gas).

Thermodynamic Processes

We can analyze the First Law by considering several specific types of thermodynamic processes where one variable is held constant.

Process Condition Work Done (W) First Law (\(\Delta U = Q - W\))
Isochoric Constant Volume (\(\Delta V = 0\)) \(W = 0\) \(\Delta U = Q\) (All heat added changes internal energy)
Isobaric Constant Pressure (\(\Delta P = 0\)) \(W = P\Delta V\) \(\Delta U = Q - P\Delta V\)
Isothermal Constant Temperature (\(\Delta T = 0\)) \(W = Q\) \(\Delta U = 0\) (All heat added does work)
Adiabatic No Heat Exchange (\(Q = 0\)) \(W = -\Delta U\) \(\Delta U = -W\) (Work is done at the expense of internal energy)

Molar Specific Heats of an Ideal Gas

The specific heat of a substance is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of a unit mass by one degree. For gases, it's more convenient to use molar specific heat, and its value depends on whether the heat is added at constant volume or constant pressure.

Molar Specific Heat at Constant Volume (\(C_V\))

When heat is added at constant volume, no work is done (\(W=0\)), so all the heat goes into increasing the internal energy: \(Q = \Delta U = nC_V\Delta T\).

Molar Specific Heat at Constant Pressure (\(C_P\))

When heat is added at constant pressure, the gas expands and does work. Therefore, more heat is required to achieve the same temperature change as some of the energy is used for work: \(Q = nC_P\Delta T\).

Relation Between \(C_P\) and \(C_V\)

For an ideal gas, the molar specific heat at constant pressure is always greater than at constant volume. Their relationship is simple and important:

\[ C_P - C_V = R \]

Where R is the universal gas constant.

Chapter Summary

The First Law of Thermodynamics is a powerful and universal statement about energy conservation.

  • A system's internal energy (\(U\)) can be changed by transferring heat (\(Q\)) or doing work (\(W\)).
  • The First Law of Thermodynamics quantifies this relationship: the change in internal energy is the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system (\(\Delta U = Q - W\)).
  • This law can be applied to specific thermodynamic processes:
    • Isochoric: Constant volume, \(\Delta U = Q\).
    • Isobaric: Constant pressure, \(\Delta U = Q - P\Delta V\).
    • Isothermal: Constant temperature, \(\Delta U = 0\) and \(Q = W\).
    • Adiabatic: No heat exchange, \(\Delta U = -W\).
  • For gases, the molar specific heat at constant pressure (\(C_P\)) is greater than at constant volume (\(C_V\)) because some energy is used to do work during expansion. For an ideal gas, \(C_P - C_V = R\).
