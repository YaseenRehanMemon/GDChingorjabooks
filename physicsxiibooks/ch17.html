The Second Law: The Arrow of Time

While the First Law of Thermodynamics tells us that energy is conserved, it doesn't specify the direction in which processes occur. The Second Law provides this direction, often called "time's arrow." It explains why heat flows from hot to cold, and why some processes are irreversible.

Kelvin-Planck Statement

It is impossible to construct a heat engine that, operating in a cycle, produces no other effect than the extraction of heat from a single reservoir and the performance of an equivalent amount of work. (In short, no heat engine can be 100% efficient).

Clausius Statement

It is impossible to construct a device that, operating in a cycle, produces no other effect than the transfer of heat from a colder body to a hotter body. (In short, heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot).

Heat Engines

A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy (heat) into mechanical work. It operates in a cycle by taking heat from a high-temperature source (hot reservoir), converting some of it into work, and rejecting the rest to a lower-temperature sink (cold reservoir).

Thermal Efficiency (\(\eta\))

\[ \eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = \frac{Q_H - Q_C}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} \]

\(W\) = Work done by the engine
\(Q_H\) = Heat absorbed from the hot reservoir
\(Q_C\) = Heat exhausted to the cold reservoir

Because some heat must always be exhausted (\(Q_C > 0\)), the efficiency of any heat engine is always less than 100%.

The Carnot Engine: The Ideal Limit

Sadi Carnot conceived of an ideal, reversible engine operating in a special cycle known as the Carnot cycle. This theoretical engine sets the maximum possible efficiency for any heat engine operating between two given temperatures.

The Carnot Cycle

The cycle consists of four reversible processes: two isothermal (constant temperature) and two adiabatic (no heat exchange).

Efficiency of a Carnot Engine

\[ \eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} \]

Where \(T_C\) and \(T_H\) are the absolute temperatures (in Kelvin) of the cold and hot reservoirs, respectively.

This is the highest possible efficiency. All real engines (like petrol or diesel engines) are less efficient due to irreversible processes like friction.

Refrigerators and Heat Pumps

A refrigerator is essentially a heat engine running in reverse. It uses external work to extract heat from a cold space (the inside of the fridge) and exhaust it to a warmer space (the room).

Coefficient of Performance (COP)

The efficiency of a refrigerator is measured by its Coefficient of Performance (COP), which is the ratio of heat removed to the work done.

\[ \text{COP} = \frac{Q_C}{W} \]

For an ideal (Carnot) refrigerator:

\[ \text{COP}_{\text{Carnot}} = \frac{T_C}{T_H - T_C} \]

Entropy: A Measure of Disorder

Entropy (S) is a thermodynamic property that can be thought of as a measure of the disorder, randomness, or unavailability of energy in a system.

The Second Law in Terms of Entropy

The total entropy of an isolated system can never decrease over time. It remains constant for reversible processes and increases for irreversible (natural) processes.

\[ \Delta S_{\text{universe}} \geq 0 \]

This means that all natural processes tend to move towards a state of greater disorder. This increase in entropy is what gives time its direction and leads to the concept of the "degradation of energy"—as entropy increases, the amount of energy available to do useful work decreases.

Change in Entropy

For a reversible process at a constant temperature \(T\), the change in entropy is:

\[ \Delta S = \frac{Q}{T} \]

Chapter Summary

The Second Law of Thermodynamics governs the direction of natural processes, introducing the fundamental concepts of efficiency and disorder.

  • The Second Law states that heat does not spontaneously flow from cold to hot (Clausius) and that no heat engine can be 100% efficient (Kelvin-Planck).
  • A heat engine converts heat into work with an efficiency of \(\eta = 1 - Q_C/Q_H\).
  • The Carnot engine is an ideal, reversible engine with the maximum possible efficiency, which depends only on the temperatures of the hot and cold reservoirs: \(\eta_C = 1 - T_C/T_H\).
  • A refrigerator uses work to move heat from a cold region to a hot region. Its performance is measured by the COP.
  • Entropy (S) is a measure of the disorder of a system. The Second Law states that the total entropy of the universe always increases or stays the same (\(\Delta S \ge 0\)) for any process.
  • The increase in entropy signifies the "degradation of energy" and provides the "arrow of time."
