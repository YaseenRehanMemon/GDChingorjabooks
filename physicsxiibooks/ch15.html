Temperature & Thermal Equilibrium

Temperature is a fundamental quantity that describes the "hotness" or "coldness" of an object. At the microscopic level, temperature is a measure of the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules in a substance.

Temperature Scales

We use several scales to measure temperature. The relationship between Celsius (\(T_C\)), Fahrenheit (\(T_F\)), and Kelvin (\(T_K\)) is crucial for problem-solving.

Conversion Formulas

\[ T_K = T_C + 273.15 \] \[ T_F = \frac{9}{5}T_C + 32 \]

The Triple Point of Water

The triple point is a unique and specific temperature and pressure at which the solid, liquid, and vapor phases of a substance can coexist in stable equilibrium. For water, this occurs at precisely 273.16 K (\(0.01^\circ C\)) and a pressure of 611.73 Pa. It serves as the standard fixed point for defining the Kelvin scale.

The Gas Laws

The behavior of gases can be described by a set of laws that relate pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and the number of moles (n).

Gas Law Relationship Constant Variables
Boyle's Law Pressure is inversely proportional to volume. Temperature, Moles
Charles's Law Volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature. Pressure, Moles
Avogadro's Law Volume is directly proportional to the number of moles. Pressure, Temperature

The General Gas Law (Ideal Gas Law)

These three laws can be combined into a single equation of state for an ideal gas.

\[ PV = nRT \]

Where R is the universal gas constant (\(8.314 \, \text{J/(molÂ·K)}\)).

Kinetic Theory of Gases

The kinetic theory provides a microscopic model that explains the macroscopic properties of gases (like pressure and temperature) based on the motion of their constituent molecules.

Basic Postulates of Kinetic Theory

  • Gases consist of a large number of tiny molecules that are in constant, random motion.
  • The volume of the molecules themselves is negligible compared to the volume of the container.
  • Collisions between molecules and with the walls of the container are perfectly elastic.
  • There are no intermolecular forces between the molecules except during collisions.

Pressure of a Gas

From these postulates, we can derive an expression for the pressure exerted by a gas. The pressure is the result of the countless collisions of gas molecules with the walls of the container. The pressure is proportional to the number of molecules and their average kinetic energy.

Relation Between Kinetic Energy and Temperature

One of the most important results of the kinetic theory is the direct link between the macroscopic property of temperature and the microscopic average kinetic energy of the gas molecules.

Average Translational KE per Molecule

\[ \overline{KE}_{\text{trans}} = \frac{3}{2}kT \]

\(\overline{KE}\) = Average translational kinetic energy
k = Boltzmann's constant (\(1.38 \times 10^{-23} \, \text{J/K}\))
T = Absolute temperature (in Kelvin)

This equation shows that absolute temperature is a direct measure of the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules. If you double the absolute temperature of a gas, you double the average kinetic energy of its molecules.

Chapter Summary

This chapter connects the macroscopic behavior of gases to the microscopic motion of their molecules.

  • Temperature is a measure of the average translational kinetic energy of molecules.
  • The Triple Point of Water (273.16 K) is the standard for defining the Kelvin scale.
  • The Gas Laws (Boyle's, Charles's, Avogadro's) describe the relationships between P, V, T, and n, which are combined in the Ideal Gas Law (\(PV=nRT\)).
  • The Kinetic Theory of Gases explains these macroscopic laws through a model of molecules in constant, random, elastic motion.
  • The pressure exerted by a gas is due to the cumulative force of molecular collisions with the container walls.
  • There is a direct, linear relationship between the absolute temperature of a gas and the average kinetic energy of its molecules (\(\overline{KE} = \frac{3}{2}kT\)).
