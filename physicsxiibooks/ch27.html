The Atomic Nucleus

Nuclear physics is the study of the atomic nucleus, its constituents, and interactions. The nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons, collectively called nucleons.

Nuclide Notation

\[ ^A_Z X \]

X = Chemical symbol of the element
Z = Atomic Number (number of protons)
A = Mass Number (total number of protons and neutrons)

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons (\(Z\)) but different numbers of neutrons. This means they have the same chemical properties but different masses. For example, Carbon-12, Carbon-13, and Carbon-14 are all isotopes of carbon.

Mass Defect and Binding Energy

A remarkable discovery in nuclear physics is that the mass of a nucleus is always slightly less than the sum of the individual masses of its protons and neutrons. This "missing" mass is known as the mass defect.

Binding Energy

The mass defect is not actually lost; it has been converted into energy according to Einstein's equation, \(E=mc^2\). This energy is the nuclear binding energy. It is the energy that holds the nucleus together, and it represents the energy that would be required to break the nucleus apart into its individual nucleons.

The binding energy per nucleon is a measure of the stability of a nucleus. Nuclei with the highest binding energy per nucleon (around iron, \(^{56}\text{Fe}\)) are the most stable.

Radioactive Decay

Radioactivity is the spontaneous process by which an unstable atomic nucleus loses energy by emitting radiation. This process, called decay, is random and spontaneous.

Types of Radioactive Decay

Decay TypeParticle EmittedDescription
Alpha (\(\alpha\))Helium nucleus (\(^{4}_{2}\text{He}\))Reduces mass number by 4 and atomic number by 2.
Beta (\(\beta^-\))Electron (\(^{0}_{-1}\text{e}\))A neutron turns into a proton. Atomic number increases by 1.
Gamma (\(\gamma\))PhotonAn excited nucleus drops to a lower energy state. No change in mass or atomic number.

Half-Life (\(T_{1/2}\))

The half-life is the time it takes for half of the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. It is a constant for a given isotope and is related to the decay constant (\(\lambda\)).

\[ T_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)}{\lambda} \approx \frac{0.693}{\lambda} \]

Nuclear Reactions

A nuclear reaction is a process in which two nuclei, or a nucleus and an external subatomic particle, collide to produce one or more new nuclides.

Nuclear Fission

Fission is the splitting of a large, heavy nucleus (like Uranium-235) into two smaller nuclei. This process releases a tremendous amount of energy and several neutrons, which can go on to cause a chain reaction. This is the principle behind nuclear power reactors and atomic bombs.

Nuclear Fusion

Fusion is the process of combining two light nuclei to form a heavier, more stable nucleus. This process releases even more energy per nucleon than fission. Fusion is the energy source of the Sun and other stars. Achieving controlled fusion on Earth is a major goal of physics research.

Conservation Laws

In all nuclear reactions, several quantities must be conserved:

  • Mass-energy (the total of mass and energy is constant)
  • Charge (total Z is constant)
  • Nucleon number (total A is constant)
  • Momentum

Radiation and Health

Nuclear radiation can be harmful to living tissue because it is a form of ionizing radiation, meaning it has enough energy to knock electrons out of atoms and molecules, breaking chemical bonds.

Biological Effects and Dosimetry

The biological damage depends on the type of radiation and the absorbed dose. Dosimetry is the science of measuring radiation dose. The effects can range from short-term radiation sickness to long-term increased cancer risk.

Applications of Radiation

Despite the risks, nuclear radiation has many beneficial uses:

  • Medical Diagnosis and Therapy: Radioactive tracers are used in imaging (like PET scans) to diagnose diseases. Radiation therapy is used to target and destroy cancer cells.
  • Agriculture and Industry: Tracers can be used to study fertilizer uptake in plants, and radiation can be used to sterilize medical equipment.

Chapter Summary

Nuclear physics deals with the structure, properties, and reactions of the atomic nucleus.

  • The nucleus is composed of protons and neutrons, and its properties are defined by its atomic number (\(Z\)) and mass number (\(A\)). Isotopes have the same \(Z\) but different \(A\).
  • The stability of a nucleus is determined by its binding energy, which arises from the conversion of a small amount of mass (the mass defect) into energy.
  • Unstable nuclei undergo radioactive decay (\(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\)), a process characterized by the half-life.
  • Nuclear Fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus, releasing energy and enabling chain reactions used in nuclear reactors.
  • Nuclear Fusion is the combining of light nuclei, releasing immense energy and powering the stars.
  • While ionizing radiation poses biological risks, it also has vital applications in medicine, industry, and research.
