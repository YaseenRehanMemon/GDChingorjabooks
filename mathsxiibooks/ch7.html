7.1 Division of a Line Segment

Analytic geometry uses algebra to describe geometric figures. We can find the coordinates of points that divide a line segment in a specific ratio.

Section Formula

The coordinates \((x, y)\) of a point that divides the line segment joining \(A(x_1, y_1)\) and \(B(x_2, y_2)\) in the ratio \(m:n\) are:

Internally: \( \left( \frac{mx_2 + nx_1}{m+n}, \frac{my_2 + ny_1}{m+n} \right) \)

Externally: \( \left( \frac{mx_2 - nx_1}{m-n}, \frac{my_2 - ny_1}{m-n} \right) \)

For the midpoint, the ratio is 1:1, so the formula simplifies to \( \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right) \).

7.2 Slope (Gradient) of a Line

The slope of a line measures its steepness and direction. It is the "rise" (vertical change) over the "run" (horizontal change).

Calculating Slope

  • From two points \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\): \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)
  • Parallel Lines: Two lines are parallel if and only if their slopes are equal (\(m_1 = m_2\)).
  • Perpendicular Lines: Two lines are perpendicular if and only if the product of their slopes is -1 (\(m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1\)).

7.3 Forms of Linear Equations

The equation of a straight line can be expressed in several standard forms, each useful in different contexts.

Standard Forms

  • Slope-Intercept Form: \(y = mx + c\), where \(m\) is the slope and \(c\) is the y-intercept.
  • Point-Slope Form: \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\), where \(m\) is the slope and \((x_1, y_1)\) is a point on the line.
  • Two-Point Form: \(y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1)\), for a line passing through \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\).
  • Intercept Form: \(\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} = 1\), where \(a\) is the x-intercept and \(b\) is the y-intercept.
  • General Form: \(Ax + By + C = 0\), where A, B, and C are constants.

7.4 Distance Formulas

Perpendicular Distance from a Point to a Line

The perpendicular (shortest) distance from a point \((x_1, y_1)\) to the line \(Ax + By + C = 0\) is given by:

\[ d = \frac{|Ax_1 + By_1 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}} \]

Distance Between Two Parallel Lines

For two parallel lines \(Ax + By + C_1 = 0\) and \(Ax + By + C_2 = 0\), the distance between them is:

\[ d = \frac{|C_1 - C_2|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}} \]

7.5 Concurrency of Lines in a Triangle

In a triangle, certain sets of special lines are concurrent, meaning they all intersect at a single point.

Points of Concurrency

  • Centroid: The intersection of the three medians (lines from a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side).
  • Orthocenter: The intersection of the three altitudes (perpendicular lines from a vertex to the opposite side).
  • Circumcenter: The intersection of the three perpendicular bisectors of the sides. It is the center of the circle that passes through all three vertices.
  • Incenter: The intersection of the three angle bisectors. It is the center of the circle inscribed within the triangle.
