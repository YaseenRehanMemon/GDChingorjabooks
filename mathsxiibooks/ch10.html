10.1 Introduction to Differential Equations

A differential equation (DE) is an equation that relates a function with its derivatives. These equations are fundamental in science and engineering because they describe how things change.

Key Terminology

  • Order: The order of the highest derivative in the equation. For example, \(\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} + y = 0\) is a second-order DE.
  • Degree: The power of the highest-order derivative after the equation has been cleared of radicals and fractions.
  • Solution: A function that satisfies the differential equation.
    • General Solution: A solution containing arbitrary constants.
    • Particular Solution: A solution where the constants have been determined by initial conditions.

10.2 Formation of Differential Equations

We can form a differential equation from a general solution by eliminating the arbitrary constants through differentiation.

Process

If a solution has \(n\) arbitrary constants, you need to differentiate the equation \(n\) times. This will create a system of \(n+1\) equations, which you can then use to algebraically eliminate the \(n\) constants, resulting in a differential equation.

10.3 Solving First-Order Differential Equations

First-order DEs involve only the first derivative. We will look at two common types that can be solved analytically.

Separable Equations

A DE is separable if it can be written in the form \(g(y) \cdot dy = f(x) \cdot dx\). To solve it, you integrate both sides of the equation.

Example

To solve \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{x}{y}\):

  1. Separate variables: \(y \,dy = x \,dx\)
  2. Integrate both sides: \(\int y \,dy = \int x \,dx\)
  3. Result: \(\frac{y^2}{2} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C\)

Homogeneous Equations

A first-order DE is homogeneous if it can be written in the form \(\frac{dy}{dx} = F(\frac{y}{x})\). These are solved using the substitution \(y = vx\), which transforms the equation into a separable one in terms of \(v\) and \(x\).

10.4 Real-Life Applications

Differential equations are used to model dynamic real-world phenomena.

Common Models

  • Population Growth: The rate of population growth is often proportional to the current population size, leading to the equation \(\frac{dP}{dt} = kP\).
  • Newton's Law of Cooling: The rate at which an object cools is proportional to the difference between its temperature and the ambient temperature.
  • Electrical Circuits: DEs model the flow of current in circuits containing resistors, capacitors, and inductors.

10.5 Orthogonal Trajectories

An orthogonal trajectory is a curve that intersects every curve in a given family of curves at a right angle (90¬∞).

Finding Orthogonal Trajectories

  1. Find the differential equation for the given family of curves by eliminating the arbitrary constant. Let the slope be \(\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y)\).
  2. The slope of the orthogonal trajectories will be the negative reciprocal: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{f(x, y)}\).
  3. Solve this new differential equation to find the family of orthogonal trajectories.
