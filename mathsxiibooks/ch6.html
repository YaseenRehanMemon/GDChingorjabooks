6.1 Antiderivatives and Indefinite Integration

Integration is the reverse process of differentiation. It is often called finding the antiderivative. If the derivative of \(F(x)\) is \(f(x)\), then the antiderivative of \(f(x)\) is \(F(x)\).

The Constant of Integration, C

Because the derivative of a constant is zero, the antiderivative of a function is not unique. For example, the derivative of \(x^2\), \(x^2+5\), and \(x^2-100\) are all \(2x\). To account for this, we add a constant of integration, \(C\), to every indefinite integral.

\[ \int f(x) \,dx = F(x) + C \]

6.2 The Definite Integral

The definite integral represents the area under the curve of a function \(f(x)\) between two points, \(a\) and \(b\). It gives a specific numerical value.

Notation and Interpretation

The definite integral from \(a\) to \(b\) is written as:

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \,dx \]
  • \(\int\) is the integral symbol.
  • \(a\) is the lower limit of integration.
  • \(b\) is the upper limit of integration.
  • \(f(x)\) is the integrand.

This expression represents the accumulated area between the curve \(y=f(x)\) and the x-axis, from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\).

6.3 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects the concepts of differentiation and integration. It provides a powerful method for evaluating definite integrals without using area summations.

The Fundamental Theorem

If \(f(x)\) is a continuous function on the interval \([a, b]\) and \(F(x)\) is any antiderivative of \(f(x)\) (meaning \(F'(x) = f(x)\)), then:

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \,dx = F(b) - F(a) \]

6.4 Integration by Substitution (u-Substitution)

Substitution is a technique for finding antiderivatives that reverses the chain rule of differentiation. It is used when the integrand is a composite function.

Steps for u-Substitution

  1. Choose 'u': Identify a part of the integrand, usually the "inner function," to be \(u\).
  2. Find du: Differentiate \(u\) to find \(du = u' \,dx\).
  3. Substitute: Replace the parts of the original integral with \(u\) and \(du\). The goal is to have an integral only in terms of \(u\).
  4. Integrate: Evaluate the new, simpler integral with respect to \(u\).
  5. Back-substitute: Replace \(u\) with its original expression in terms of \(x\).

6.5 Integration by Parts

Integration by Parts is a technique that reverses the product rule of differentiation. It is useful for integrating the product of two functions.

The Integration by Parts Formula

\[ \int u \,dv = uv - \int v \,du \]

The key is to choose \(u\) and \(dv\) strategically. A good rule of thumb is the LIATE principle for choosing \(u\): Logarithmic, Inverse Trig, Algebraic, Trigonometric, Exponential.

6.6 Integration by Partial Fractions

This method is used to integrate rational functions (a ratio of two polynomials). It involves breaking down a complex fraction into a sum of simpler fractions that are easier to integrate.

The Process

  1. Factor the Denominator: Completely factor the denominator of the rational function.
  2. Set up the Decomposition: Write the fraction as a sum of simpler fractions with unknown numerators (A, B, C, etc.) based on the factors of the denominator.
  3. Solve for Constants: Solve for the unknown numerators by equating coefficients or substituting convenient values for \(x\).
  4. Integrate: Integrate the sum of the simpler fractions.
