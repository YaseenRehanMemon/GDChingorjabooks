3.1 The Derivative

Differentiation is the process of finding the derivative of a function. The derivative represents the instantaneous rate of change of the function, which can be visualized as the slope of the tangent line to the function's graph at a specific point.

Definition of the Derivative (First Principle)

The derivative of a function \(f(x)\), denoted as \(f'(x)\) or \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), is defined by the limit:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]

This formula calculates the slope of the secant line between two points on the curve as the distance between them (h) approaches zero.

3.2 Basic Differentiation Rules

While the first principle is fundamental, we typically use a set of rules to find derivatives more efficiently.

Core Rules

  • Constant Rule: The derivative of a constant is zero. If \(f(x) = c\), then \(f'(x) = 0\).
  • Power Rule: A crucial rule for differentiating polynomials.

Power Rule: If \(f(x) = x^n\), then \(f'(x) = n \cdot x^{n-1}\).

Example: The derivative of \(x^3\) is \(3x^2\).

  • Constant Multiple Rule: The derivative of a constant multiplied by a function is the constant times the derivative of the function. \(\frac{d}{dx}[c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot f'(x)\).
  • Sum/Difference Rule: The derivative of a sum or difference of functions is the sum or difference of their derivatives. \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \pm g(x)] = f'(x) \pm g'(x)\).

3.3 The Product and Quotient Rules

These rules are used to differentiate functions that are multiplied or divided.

Product Rule

If \(h(x) = f(x)g(x)\), then:

\[ h'(x) = f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) \]

In words: "The first times the derivative of the second, plus the second times the derivative of the first."

Quotient Rule

If \(h(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\), then:

\[ h'(x) = \frac{g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \]

In words: "Low d-high minus high d-low, over the square of what's below."

3.4 The Chain Rule

The chain rule is essential for differentiating composite functions (a function inside another function).

Defining the Chain Rule

If \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\), then the derivative of the composite function \(y = f(g(x))\) is:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \]

In words: "The derivative of the outer function (with the inside function left alone) times the derivative of the inside function."

Example: To differentiate \((x^2+1)^3\), the outer function is \(u^3\) and the inner is \(x^2+1\). The derivative is \(3(x^2+1)^2 \cdot (2x)\).

3.5 Implicit Differentiation

Implicit differentiation is a technique used when a function is not given in the form \(y=f(x)\). It involves differentiating both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\) and then solving for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).

Steps for Implicit Differentiation

  1. Differentiate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\).
  2. When differentiating a term with \(y\), apply the chain rule by multiplying by \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).
  3. Isolate all terms containing \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) on one side of the equation.
  4. Factor out \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).
  5. Solve for \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).
