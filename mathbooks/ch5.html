5.1 Sigma (\(\Sigma\)) Notation

The Greek letter Sigma (\(\Sigma\)) is used in mathematics to denote summation. It provides a concise way to represent the sum of a series of terms.

Definition of Sigma Notation

If \(x_1, x_2, x_3, \dots, x_n\) is a series of terms, their sum is denoted by:

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i = x_1 + x_2 + x_3 + \dots + x_n \]
  • \(i\) is the index of summation.
  • \(x_i\) is the \(i\)-th element of the series.
  • \(1\) is the lower limit and \(n\) is the upper limit of summation.

Example: \(\sum_{i=1}^{10} x_i = x_1 + x_2 + \dots + x_{10}\).

5.2 Sum of Powers of Natural Numbers

Formulas exist to directly calculate the sum of the first \(n\) natural numbers, their squares, and their cubes.

Sum of First \(n\) Natural Numbers:

\[ \sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \]

Sum of Squares of First \(n\) Natural Numbers:

\[ \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \]

Sum of Cubes of First \(n\) Natural Numbers:

\[ \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left[\frac{n(n+1)}{2}\right]^2 \]

5.3 Arithmetico-Geometric Series (A.G. Series)

An arithmetico-geometric series is formed by multiplying the corresponding terms of an Arithmetic Progression (A.P.) and a Geometric Progression (G.P.).

Form of an A.G. Series:

\[ a + (a+d)r + (a+2d)r^2 + \dots + \{a+(n-1)d\}r^{n-1} \]

5.3.1 Finding the Sum to \(n\) Terms of an A.G. Series

The sum of \(n\) terms of an A.G. series can be found using a specific method involving multiplying the series by the common ratio of the G.P. and then subtracting the two series.

\[ S_n = \frac{a}{1-r} + \frac{dr(1-r^{n-1})}{(1-r)^2} - \frac{\{a+(n-1)d\}r^n}{1-r} \]

This formula applies when \(r \neq 1\).

5.4 Method of Differences

The Method of Differences is a technique used to find the sum of a series when the differences between consecutive terms form an arithmetic or geometric sequence.

Theorem:

If a series can be expressed in the form \(\sum_{r=1}^{n} [f(r+1) - f(r)]\), then its sum is \(f(n+1) - f(1)\).

Similarly, if it's \(\sum_{r=1}^{n} [f(r) - f(r+1)]\), then its sum is \(f(1) - f(n+1)\).

5.5 Summation of Series using Partial Fractions

Some series whose general terms are rational expressions can be summed by first decomposing the general term into partial fractions. This transforms the series into a telescoping sum that can be easily evaluated.

Example:

To sum a series where the general term is \(\frac{1}{(r+1)(r+2)}\), we first use partial fractions to write it as \(\frac{1}{r+1} - \frac{1}{r+2}\). When summed, intermediate terms cancel out.
