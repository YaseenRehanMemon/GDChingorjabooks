7.1 Mathematical Induction

Mathematical induction is a powerful method used in mathematics to prove that a statement, formula, or inequality is true for all positive integers (natural numbers). It's a form of deductive reasoning that establishes conclusions with certainty.

7.1.1 Principle of Mathematical Induction

To prove a proposition \(P(n)\) is true for all positive integers \(n\), follow these steps:

  • Base Case: Show that \(P(n)\) is true for \(n=1\).
  • Inductive Step: Assume that \(P(n)\) is true for some positive integer \(n=k\) (the inductive hypothesis). Then, prove that \(P(n)\) is also true for \(n=k+1\).

If both steps are successfully demonstrated, then \(P(n)\) is true for all positive integers \(n\).

Modified Principle for \(P(n)\) not true for first few values

If \(P(n)\) is true for all successive values after a certain stage, say \(n=i\), then the principle is modified:

  • Base Case: Show that \(P(n)\) is true for \(n=i\), where \(i\) is a positive integer.
  • Inductive Step: Assume \(P(n)\) is true for some positive integer \(n=k\) (\(k \ge i\)). Then, prove \(P(n)\) is true for \(n=k+1\).

7.2 Binomial Theorem (for Positive Integral Index)

A binomial expression consists of two terms connected by a plus or minus sign, like \((x+y)\) or \((2x-3y)\). The Binomial Theorem provides a formula to expand binomial expressions raised to a positive integral power \((n)\).

7.2.1 Pascal's Triangle: Coefficients of Binomial Expansion

The coefficients of binomial expansions can be found using Pascal's Triangle. Each number in the triangle is the sum of the two numbers directly above it. The edges of the triangle are always 1s.

For \((x+y)^0\): 1

For \((x+y)^1\): 1   1

For \((x+y)^2\): 1   2   1

For \((x+y)^3\): 1   3   3   1

For \((x+y)^4\): 1   4   6   4   1

and so on...

7.2.2 Statement and Proof of Binomial Theorem

Statement: If \(n\) is a positive integer, then

\[ (a+b)^n = \binom{n}{0}a^n + \binom{n}{1}a^{n-1}b + \binom{n}{2}a^{n-2}b^2 + \dots + \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r + \dots + \binom{n}{n}b^n \]

where \(\binom{n}{r}\) are binomial coefficients, calculated as:

\[ \binom{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!} \]

Characteristics of Binomial Expansion

  • The number of terms in the expansion is \(n+1\).
  • The sum of the powers of \(a\) and \(b\) in each term is always \(n\).
  • The coefficients are symmetrical: \(\binom{n}{r} = \binom{n}{n-r}\).
  • The general term (or \((r+1)\)th term) is \(T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r}a^{n-r}b^r\).

7.3 Binomial Series (for Rational Index)

When the exponent \(n\) is a negative integer or a fraction (rational number), the binomial expansion becomes an infinite series, known as the Binomial Series.

7.3.1 Expansion of \((1+x)^n\) for Rational Values of \(n\)

If \(n\) is a rational number, then

\[ (1+x)^n = 1 + nx + \frac{n(n-1)}{2!}x^2 + \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)}{3!}x^3 + \dots \]

Validity / Convergence

This infinite series is valid (converges) only when \(|x| < 1\).

Applications of Binomial Series

The binomial series can be used to approximate values of expressions involving roots or small powers, particularly when \(x\) is very small, allowing us to neglect terms with \(x^2\) and higher powers for a first approximation.

Chapter Summary

  • Mathematical Induction: A proof technique used to verify statements for all natural numbers, involving a base case and an inductive step.
  • Binomial Theorem: Provides a formula to expand \((a+b)^n\) for positive integer \(n\), generating \(n+1\) terms with coefficients from Pascal's Triangle or \(\binom{n}{r}\).
  • Binomial Series: An infinite expansion for \((1+x)^n\) when \(n\) is a rational number. It converges for \(|x| < 1\).
