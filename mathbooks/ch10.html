10.1 Fundamental Trigonometric Ratios

Trigonometry deals with the relationships between the angles and sides of triangles. For a right-angled triangle, the six basic trigonometric ratios are defined relative to an acute angle \(\theta\):

  • \(\sin \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}\)
  • \(\cos \theta = \frac{\text{Adjacent}}{\text{Hypotenuse}}\)
  • \(\tan \theta = \frac{\text{Opposite}}{\text{Adjacent}}\)
  • \(\csc \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}\)
  • \(\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}\)
  • \(\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta}\)

10.1.2 Fundamental Law of Trigonometry & Deductions

The fundamental law of trigonometry, derived from the distance formula on a unit circle, is crucial for deriving many other identities.

Fundamental Law:

\[ \cos(\alpha - \beta) = \cos \alpha \cos \beta + \sin \alpha \sin \beta \]

Deductions from the Fundamental Law:

  • \(\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos \alpha \cos \beta - \sin \alpha \sin \beta\)
  • \(\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin \alpha \cos \beta + \cos \alpha \sin \beta\)
  • \(\sin(\alpha - \beta) = \sin \alpha \cos \beta - \cos \alpha \sin \beta\)
  • \(\tan(\alpha + \beta) = \frac{\tan \alpha + \tan \beta}{1 - \tan \alpha \tan \beta}\)
  • \(\tan(\alpha - \beta) = \frac{\tan \alpha - \tan \beta}{1 + \tan \alpha \tan \beta}\)

10.2 Trigonometric Ratios of Allied Angles

Allied angles are angles whose sum or difference with a basic angle \(\theta\) is an integral multiple of \(90^\circ\) or \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) radians. These include angles like \(90^\circ \pm \theta\), \(180^\circ \pm \theta\), \(270^\circ \pm \theta\), and \(360^\circ \pm \theta\).

10.2.1 General Rules for Allied Angles

The trigonometric ratio of an allied angle depends on two things:

  1. Quadrant: The sign (\(+\) or \(-\)) depends on the quadrant in which the allied angle lies.
  2. Multiple of \(90^\circ\):
    • If the multiple is even (\(180^\circ \pm \theta\), \(360^\circ \pm \theta\)), the ratio remains the same (e.g., \(\sin \to \sin\), \(\cos \to \cos\)).
    • If the multiple is odd (\(90^\circ \pm \theta\), \(270^\circ \pm \theta\)), the ratio changes to its co-ratio (e.g., \(\sin \to \cos\), \(\tan \to \cot\)).

Examples of Allied Angle Identities:

  • \(\sin(90^\circ + \theta) = \cos \theta\)
  • \(\cos(180^\circ - \theta) = -\cos \theta\)
  • \(\tan(360^\circ - \theta) = -\tan \theta\)

10.3 Double, Half, and Triple Angle Identities

These identities are derived from the fundamental sum and difference formulas by replacing \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) with specific values (e.g., setting \(\alpha = \beta = \theta\) for double angles).

10.3.1 Double Angle Identities

  • \(\sin(2\theta) = 2 \sin \theta \cos \theta\)
  • \(\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2 \theta - \sin^2 \theta\)
  • \(\cos(2\theta) = 2 \cos^2 \theta - 1\)
  • \(\cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta\)
  • \(\tan(2\theta) = \frac{2 \tan \theta}{1 - \tan^2 \theta}\)

10.3.2 Half Angle Identities

Derived from double angle identities by setting \(\theta = \frac{\alpha}{2}\).

  • \(\sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 - \cos \theta}{2}}\)
  • \(\cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \pm\sqrt{\frac{1 + \cos \theta}{2}}\)
  • \(\tan\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right) = \frac{1 - \cos \theta}{\sin \theta} = \frac{\sin \theta}{1 + \cos \theta}\)

10.3.3 Triple Angle Identities

  • \(\sin(3\theta) = 3 \sin \theta - 4 \sin^3 \theta\)
  • \(\cos(3\theta) = 4 \cos^3 \theta - 3 \cos \theta\)
  • \(\tan(3\theta) = \frac{3 \tan \theta - \tan^3 \theta}{1 - 3 \tan^2 \theta}\)

10.4 Sum and Difference to Product Identities

These formulas convert sums or differences of sines and cosines into products, which can simplify expressions and help in solving trigonometric equations.

  • \(\sin u + \sin v = 2 \sin\left(\frac{u+v}{2}\right) \cos\left(\frac{u-v}{2}\right)\)
  • \(\sin u - \sin v = 2 \cos\left(\frac{u+v}{2}\right) \sin\left(\frac{u-v}{2}\right)\)
  • \(\cos u + \cos v = 2 \cos\left(\frac{u+v}{2}\right) \cos\left(\frac{u-v}{2}\right)\)
  • \(\cos u - \cos v = -2 \sin\left(\frac{u+v}{2}\right) \sin\left(\frac{u-v}{2}\right)\)

10.4.1 Product to Sum and Difference Identities

These are the reverse of the above, converting products of sines and cosines into sums or differences.

  • \(2 \sin \alpha \cos \beta = \sin(\alpha + \beta) + \sin(\alpha - \beta)\)
  • \(2 \cos \alpha \sin \beta = \sin(\alpha + \beta) - \sin(\alpha - \beta)\)
  • \(2 \cos \alpha \cos \beta = \cos(\alpha + \beta) + \cos(\alpha - \beta)\)
  • \(2 \sin \alpha \sin \beta = \cos(\alpha - \beta) - \cos(\alpha + \beta)\)

Chapter Summary

  • The fundamental law \(\cos(\alpha - \beta) = \cos \alpha \cos \beta + \sin \alpha \sin \beta\) is the basis for sum and difference identities for sine, cosine, and tangent.
  • Allied angles (\(90^\circ \pm \theta\), \(180^\circ \pm \theta\), etc.) have trigonometric ratios whose signs and functions depend on the quadrant and multiple of \(90^\circ\).
  • Double, half, and triple angle identities are derived from sum/difference formulas to express trigonometric functions of multiples or fractions of an angle.
  • Sum/difference to product and product to sum/difference formulas convert expressions between sums/differences and products of trigonometric functions.
