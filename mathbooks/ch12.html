12.1 Periodicity and Graphs of Trigonometric Functions

Trigonometric functions (sine, cosine, tangent, etc.) describe oscillating behavior and are inherently periodic, meaning their values repeat over a regular interval.

12.1.1 Domain and Range

FunctionDomainRangePeriod
\(\sin \theta\)\(\mathbb{R}\)\([-1, 1]\)\(2\pi\)
\(\cos \theta\)\(\mathbb{R}\)\([-1, 1]\)\(2\pi\)
\(\tan \theta\)\(\mathbb{R} \setminus \left\{\frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi\right\}\)\(\mathbb{R}\)\(\pi\)
\(\csc \theta\)\(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{n\pi\}\)\((-\infty, -1] \cup [1, \infty)\)\(2\pi\)
\(\sec \theta\)\(\mathbb{R} \setminus \left\{\frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi\right\}\)\((-\infty, -1] \cup [1, \infty)\)\(2\pi\)
\(\cot \theta\)\(\mathbb{R} \setminus \{n\pi\}\)\(\mathbb{R}\)\(\pi\)

12.1.2 Even and Odd Functions

  • Even Functions: \(f(-x) = f(x)\) (e.g., \(\cos x\), \(\sec x\)). Their graphs are symmetric about the y-axis.
  • Odd Functions: \(f(-x) = -f(x)\) (e.g., \(\sin x\), \(\tan x\), \(\csc x\), \(\cot x\)). Their graphs are symmetric about the origin.

12.1.3 Periodicity and Amplitude

The period of a trigonometric function is the smallest positive value \(P\) for which \(f(x+P) = f(x)\). The amplitude is half the distance between the maximum and minimum values of the function.

Period for \(f(kx)\):

For \(\sin(kx)\), \(\cos(kx)\), \(\csc(kx)\), \(\sec(kx)\), period is \(\frac{2\pi}{|k|}\).

For \(\tan(kx)\), \(\cot(kx)\), period is \(\frac{\pi}{|k|}\).

12.2 Inverse Trigonometric Functions

Since trigonometric functions are periodic, they are not one-to-one over their entire domain. To define inverse functions, we restrict their domains to create principal trigonometric functions, which are bijective.

12.2.1 Definitions and Properties

  • Inverse Sine (\(\arcsin x\) or \(\sin^{-1} x\)):
    • Domain: \([-1, 1]\)
    • Range: \(\left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right]\)
  • Inverse Cosine (\(\arccos x\) or \(\cos^{-1} x\)):
    • Domain: \([-1, 1]\)
    • Range: \([0, \pi]\)
  • Inverse Tangent (\(\arctan x\) or \(\tan^{-1} x\)):
    • Domain: \(\mathbb{R}\)
    • Range: \(\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\)

And similarly for \(\sec^{-1}x\), \(\csc^{-1}x\), and \(\cot^{-1}x\).

12.2.2 Addition and Subtraction Formulas for Inverse Trigonometric Functions

  • \(\sin^{-1}A + \sin^{-1}B = \sin^{-1}\left(A\sqrt{1-B^2} + B\sqrt{1-A^2}\right)\)
  • \(\cos^{-1}A + \cos^{-1}B = \cos^{-1}\left(AB - \sqrt{1-A^2}\sqrt{1-B^2}\right)\)
  • \(\tan^{-1}A + \tan^{-1}B = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{A+B}{1-AB}\right)\)

(with appropriate conditions on A and B for the formulas to hold true).

12.3 Solving Trigonometric Equations

A trigonometric equation is an equation that involves one or more trigonometric functions. Due to the periodic nature of these functions, trigonometric equations often have an infinite number of solutions.

12.3.1 General Solution of Trigonometric Equations

To find the general solution, we first find the principal solutions within a specific interval (e.g., \(0 \le \theta < 2\pi\) or \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}\)), and then add integer multiples of the function's period.

Example: Solving \(\sin \theta = k\)

If \(\sin \theta = k\), where \(|k| \le 1\), and \(\alpha\) is the principal value \(\left(-\frac{\pi}{2} \le \alpha \le \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\), then the general solution is:

\[ \theta = n\pi + (-1)^n \alpha, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z} \]

Example: Solving \(\cos \theta = k\)

If \(\cos \theta = k\), where \(|k| \le 1\), and \(\alpha\) is the principal value \((0 \le \alpha \le \pi)\), then the general solution is:

\[ \theta = 2n\pi \pm \alpha, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z} \]

Example: Solving \(\tan \theta = k\)

If \(\tan \theta = k\), and \(\alpha\) is the principal value \(\left(-\frac{\pi}{2} < \alpha < \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\), then the general solution is:

\[ \theta = n\pi + \alpha, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z} \]

12.3.2 Graphical Solution of Trigonometric Equations

Trigonometric equations can also be solved graphically by plotting the graphs of the functions involved and finding their points of intersection. For example, to solve \(\sin \theta = \frac{1}{2}\), you would graph \(y = \sin \theta\) and \(y = \frac{1}{2}\) and find where they intersect.

Chapter Summary

  • Trigonometric Functions: Are periodic, with specific domains, ranges, and properties (even/odd). Their graphs are sinusoidal (sine, cosine) or repetitive curves (tangent, cotangent).
  • Inverse Trigonometric Functions: Defined by restricting the domains of trig functions to make them bijective. They have specific domains and ranges (principal values).
  • Trigonometric Equations: Equations involving trig functions, often having infinite solutions due to periodicity. General solutions are found by adding integer multiples of the period to principal values. Graphical methods can also be used.
