3.1 Scalars and Vectors

Physical quantities can be classified based on whether they have direction. This chapter introduces vectors, quantities that require both magnitude and direction for their complete specification.

3.1.1 Scalar Quantity

A scalar quantity is completely specified by its magnitude and an appropriate unit. It has no direction.

Examples: Mass, volume, area, distance, speed, energy, time.

3.1.2 Vector Quantity

A vector quantity is specified by both its magnitude and its direction, along with an appropriate unit.

Examples: Weight, force, displacement, velocity, acceleration, momentum.

3.2 Geometrical and Analytical Representation of a Vector

3.2.1 Geometrical Representation

Geometrically, a vector is represented by a directed line segment (an arrow). The length of the arrow represents the magnitude, and the arrowhead indicates the direction.

  • The starting point is the initial point (tail).
  • The ending point is the terminal point (tip).

3.2.2 Unit Vectors (\(\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k}\))

A unit vector is a vector with a magnitude of 1. The fundamental unit vectors in 3D Cartesian space are \(\hat{i}\), \(\hat{j}\), and \(\hat{k}\), representing the positive x, y, and z directions, respectively.

3.2.3 Analytic Representation

Any vector \(\vec{A}\) in 3D space can be expressed in terms of its components along the coordinate axes using unit vectors:

\[ \vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j} + A_z\hat{k} \]

The numbers \(A_x, A_y, A_z\) are the scalar components of \(\vec{A}\).

Magnitude of a Vector

The magnitude of a vector \(\vec{A} = A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j} + A_z\hat{k}\) is given by:

\[ |\vec{A}| = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2 + A_z^2} \]

Direction Cosines

The direction cosines of a vector are the cosines of the angles it makes with the positive x, y, and z axes. They are denoted by \(l, m, n\).

\[ l = \cos \alpha = \frac{A_x}{|\vec{A}|}, \quad m = \cos \beta = \frac{A_y}{|\vec{A}|}, \quad n = \cos \gamma = \frac{A_z}{|\vec{A}|} \]

A key property is \(l^2 + m^2 + n^2 = 1\).

3.3 Vector Operations

3.3.1 Addition and Subtraction of Vectors

Vectors can be added or subtracted component-wise.

\[ \vec{A} + \vec{B} = (A_x+B_x)\hat{i} + (A_y+B_y)\hat{j} + (A_z+B_z)\hat{k} \] \[ \vec{A} - \vec{B} = (A_x-B_x)\hat{i} + (A_y-B_y)\hat{j} + (A_z-B_z)\hat{k} \]

Laws of Vector Addition

  • Triangle Law: If two vectors are represented by two sides of a triangle taken in order, their resultant is the third side taken in opposite order.
  • Parallelogram Law: If two vectors from a common initial point represent adjacent sides of a parallelogram, their resultant is the diagonal from that common point.
  • Polygon Law: For multiple vectors, if represented by consecutive sides of a polygon, their resultant is the closing side in opposite order.
  • Commutative Law: \(\vec{A} + \vec{B} = \vec{B} + \vec{A}\).
  • Associative Law: \((\vec{A} + \vec{B}) + \vec{C} = \vec{A} + (\vec{B} + \vec{C})\).

3.3.2 Scalar Multiplication of Vectors

Multiplying a vector \(\vec{A}\) by a scalar \(k\) results in a new vector \(k\vec{A}\) whose magnitude is \(|k|\) times the magnitude of \(\vec{A}\). Its direction is the same as \(\vec{A}\) if \(k>0\), and opposite if \(k<0\).

\[ k\vec{A} = (kA_x)\hat{i} + (kA_y)\hat{j} + (kA_z)\hat{k} \]

Properties of Scalar Multiplication

  • Commutative: \(k\vec{A} = \vec{A}k\).
  • Associative: \(m(n\vec{A}) = (mn)\vec{A}\).
  • Distributive: \(k(\vec{A} + \vec{B}) = k\vec{A} + k\vec{B}\) and \((m+n)\vec{A} = m\vec{A} + n\vec{A}\).

3.4 Dot (Scalar) Product of Vectors

The dot product (or scalar product) of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is a scalar quantity defined as:

\[ \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = |\vec{A}||\vec{B}| \cos \theta \]

Where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors. The dot product can also be calculated from components:

\[ \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z \]

Properties of the Dot Product

  • Commutative: \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{A}\).
  • Distributive: \(\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} + \vec{A} \cdot \vec{C}\).
  • Orthogonality Condition: If \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = 0\) (and \(\vec{A}, \vec{B} \neq 0\)), then \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are perpendicular (orthogonal).
  • Work Done: The work done by a constant force \(\vec{F}\) causing displacement \(\vec{d}\) is given by \(W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{d}\).

3.5 Cross (Vector) Product of Vectors

The cross product (or vector product) of two vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is a vector quantity that is perpendicular to both \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\). Its magnitude and direction are defined as:

\[ \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = (|\vec{A}||\vec{B}| \sin \theta) \hat{n} \]

Where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors, and \(\hat{n}\) is a unit vector perpendicular to the plane of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), determined by the right-hand rule.

The cross product can also be calculated from components using a determinant:

\[ \vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \begin{vmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k} \\ A_x & A_y & A_z \\ B_x & B_y & B_z \end{vmatrix} \]

Properties of the Cross Product

  • Anti-Commutative: \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = -(\vec{B} \times \vec{A})\).
  • Distributive: \(\vec{A} \times (\vec{B} + \vec{C}) = (\vec{A} \times \vec{B}) + (\vec{A} \times \vec{C})\).
  • Parallelism Condition: If \(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \vec{0}\) (and \(\vec{A}, \vec{B} \neq \vec{0}\)), then \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are parallel.
  • Area of Parallelogram: The magnitude \(|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}|\) represents the area of the parallelogram formed by vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\).
  • Area of Triangle: The area of a triangle formed by vectors \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is \(\frac{1}{2}|\vec{A} \times \vec{B}|\).

3.5.1 Scalar Triple Product

The scalar triple product of three vectors \(\vec{A}, \vec{B}, \vec{C}\) is a scalar quantity defined as \(\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} \times \vec{C})\). It represents the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the three vectors.

\[ \vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} \times \vec{C}) = \begin{vmatrix} A_x & A_y & A_z \\ B_x & B_y & B_z \\ C_x & C_y & C_z \end{vmatrix} \]

If the scalar triple product is zero, the three vectors are coplanar (lie on the same plane).

Chapter Summary

  • Scalars: Magnitude only (e.g., mass, speed). Vectors: Magnitude and direction (e.g., force, velocity).
  • Representation: Geometrically by arrows; analytically by components using unit vectors \(\hat{i}, \hat{j}, \hat{k}\).
  • Vector Operations: Addition/subtraction (component-wise, obeys Triangle/Parallelogram Laws), scalar multiplication.
  • Dot Product (\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\)): A scalar, \(|\vec{A}||\vec{B}| \cos \theta\), used to find angles and check orthogonality (\(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = 0\)).
  • Cross Product (\(\vec{A} \times \vec{B}\)): A vector, \(|\vec{A}||\vec{B}| \sin \theta \, \hat{n}\), used to find perpendicular vectors, areas of parallelograms/triangles, and check parallelism (\(\vec{A} \times \vec{B} = \vec{0}\)).
  • Scalar Triple Product: \(\vec{A} \cdot (\vec{B} \times \vec{C})\), gives the volume of a parallelepiped and checks coplanarity (\(= 0\)).
