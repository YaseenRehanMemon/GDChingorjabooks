1.1 Introduction to Complex Numbers

The system of real numbers is insufficient to solve all algebraic equations. For instance, equations like \(x^2 = -1\) or \(x^2 + 4 = 0\) have no real solutions. To address this, imaginary numbers and then complex numbers were introduced.

1.1.1 Definition of a Complex Number

A complex number \(z\) is represented in the form \(z = a + ib\) or as an ordered pair \((a, b)\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers, and \(i = \sqrt{-1}\) is the imaginary unit.

  • \(a\) is called the real part, denoted as \(\text{Re}(z)\).
  • \(b\) is called the imaginary part, denoted as \(\text{Im}(z)\).

Example: For \(z = 3+2i\), \(\text{Re}(z) = 3\) and \(\text{Im}(z) = 2\).

1.1.2 Equality of Complex Numbers

Two complex numbers \(z_1 = a_1 + ib_1\) and \(z_2 = a_2 + ib_2\) are equal if and only if their real parts are equal (\(a_1 = a_2\)) and their imaginary parts are equal (\(b_1 = b_2\)).

1.1.3 Complex Conjugate

The complex conjugate of \(z = a + ib\), denoted as \(\bar{z}\), is given by \(\bar{z} = a - ib\). Geometrically, it represents a reflection of \(z\) across the real axis in the complex plane.

1.1.4 Absolute Value (Modulus) of a Complex Number

The absolute value or modulus of a complex number \(z = a + ib\), denoted as \(|z|\), is the distance of the point \((a, b)\) from the origin in the complex plane.

\[ |z| = |a + ib| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} \]

1.2 Basic Operations on Complex Numbers

Complex numbers can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided just like real numbers, with careful attention to the imaginary unit \(i\).

1.2.1 Addition and Subtraction

Addition: \((a+ib) + (c+id) = (a+c) + i(b+d)\)

Subtraction: \((a+ib) - (c+id) = (a-c) + i(b-d)\)

1.2.2 Multiplication

Multiplication: \((a+ib)(c+id) = (ac-bd) + i(ad+bc)\)

Remember that \(i^2 = -1\).

1.2.3 Division

To divide complex numbers, we multiply the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator to eliminate \(i\) from the denominator.

\[ \frac{a+ib}{c+id} = \frac{a+ib}{c+id} \times \frac{c-id}{c-id} = \frac{(ac+bd) + i(bc-ad)}{c^2+d^2} \]

1.2.4 Additive and Multiplicative Inverses

  • Additive Inverse of \(z = a+ib\): \(-z = -a-ib\). The sum is \((0,0)\).
  • Multiplicative Inverse of \(z = a+ib\): \(z^{-1} = \frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^2} = \frac{a-ib}{a^2+b^2}\). The product is \((1,0)\).

1.3 Properties of Complex Numbers

Complex numbers follow several important algebraic and geometric properties.

1.3.1 Algebraic Properties (Commutative, Associative, Distributive)

Complex numbers obey the commutative, associative, and distributive laws for both addition and multiplication, similar to real numbers.

  • Commutative: \(z_1 + z_2 = z_2 + z_1\) and \(z_1 z_2 = z_2 z_1\).
  • Associative: \((z_1 + z_2) + z_3 = z_1 + (z_2 + z_3)\) and \((z_1 z_2)z_3 = z_1(z_2 z_3)\).
  • Distributive: \(z_1(z_2 + z_3) = z_1 z_2 + z_1 z_3\).

1.3.2 Properties of Conjugates and Modulus

  • \(\overline{(\bar{z})} = z\) (Double conjugate returns the original number).
  • \(z \bar{z} = |z|^2\).
  • \(\overline{z_1 + z_2} = \bar{z_1} + \bar{z_2}\) and \(\overline{z_1 z_2} = \bar{z_1} \bar{z_2}\).

1.3.3 Triangle Inequality

\[ |z_1 + z_2| \le |z_1| + |z_2| \]

This inequality states that the length of the sum of two vectors is less than or equal to the sum of their lengths.

1.3.4 Solving Complex Equations

Quadratic equations with complex coefficients can be solved using the quadratic formula or by completing the square, similar to real-valued equations. Polynomials with complex roots can be factored into linear factors.

Chapter Summary

  • Complex Numbers: Numbers of the form \(a + ib\), where \(a, b \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(i = \sqrt{-1}\).
  • Operations: Follow standard algebraic rules, remembering \(i^2 = -1\).
  • Conjugate (\(\bar{z}\)): \(a-ib\). Modulus (\(|z|\)): \(\sqrt{a^2+b^2}\).
  • Properties: Complex numbers are commutative, associative, and distributive for addition and multiplication. They obey properties like \(z\bar{z} = |z|^2\) and the Triangle Inequality.
  • Equations: Can be solved using standard algebraic methods, yielding complex solutions.
