11.1 Solving Triangles

Solving a triangle means finding the measures of its unknown sides and angles. This is possible if at least one side and any two other elements (sides or angles) are known.

11.1.1 Solving Right-Angled Triangles

For a right-angled triangle (one angle is \(90^\circ\)), we can use basic trigonometric ratios (sine, cosine, tangent) and the Pythagorean theorem.

Pythagorean Theorem:

\[ a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \]

Where \(a\) and \(b\) are the lengths of the legs, and \(c\) is the length of the hypotenuse.

11.1.2 Solving Oblique Triangles

An oblique triangle is a triangle that does not have a right angle. We use specific laws to solve them.

Law of Sines:

\[ \frac{a}{\sin \alpha} = \frac{b}{\sin \beta} = \frac{c}{\sin \gamma} \]

Where \(a, b, c\) are side lengths, and \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma\) are the angles opposite those sides.

Law of Cosines:

\[ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab \cos \gamma \]

(and similar forms for \(a^2\) and \(b^2\)).

Law of Tangents:

\[ \frac{a - b}{a + b} = \frac{\tan\left(\frac{\alpha - \beta}{2}\right)}{\tan\left(\frac{\alpha + \beta}{2}\right)} \]

(and similar forms for other sides/angles).

11.1.3 Half-Angle Formulas (Sine, Cosine, Tangent)

These formulas relate the sine, cosine, or tangent of half an angle to the lengths of the sides of the triangle. They are particularly useful when all three side lengths are known.

\[ \sin\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right) = \sqrt{\frac{(s-b)(s-c)}{bc}} \] \[ \cos\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right) = \sqrt{\frac{s(s-a)}{bc}} \] \[ \tan\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right) = \sqrt{\frac{(s-b)(s-c)}{s(s-a)}} \]

Where \(s = \frac{a+b+c}{2}\) is the semi-perimeter.

11.2 Area of a Triangle

Trigonometry provides convenient formulas to calculate the area of a triangle without needing the perpendicular height directly.

11.2.1 Formulas for Area of a Triangle

Using Two Sides and Included Angle:

\[ \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}ab \sin \gamma \]

(and similar forms: \(\frac{1}{2}bc \sin \alpha\), \(\frac{1}{2}ca \sin \beta\)).

Using One Side and Two Angles:

\[ \text{Area} = \frac{a^2 \sin \beta \sin \gamma}{2 \sin \alpha} \]

(and similar forms for \(b^2\) and \(c^2\)).

Using Three Sides (Hero's Formula):

\[ \text{Area} = \sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)} \]

Where \(s = \frac{a+b+c}{2}\) is the semi-perimeter.

11.3 Circles Connected with a Triangle

There are three special types of circles that are related to a triangle: the circum-circle, the in-circle, and escribed circles.

11.3.1 Definitions

  • Circum-circle: A circle that passes through all three vertices of the triangle. Its center is the circum-center, and its radius is the circum-radius (\(R\)).
  • In-circle: A circle inscribed within the triangle that touches all three sides. Its center is the in-center, and its radius is the in-radius (\(r\)).
  • Escribed Circles (Ex-circles): Circles that touch one side of the triangle externally and the extensions of the other two sides. A triangle has three escribed circles, with radii \(r_1, r_2, r_3\).

11.3.2 Formulas for Radii

Circum-radius (\(R\)):

\[ R = \frac{a}{2 \sin \alpha} = \frac{b}{2 \sin \beta} = \frac{c}{2 \sin \gamma} \] \[ R = \frac{abc}{4 \times \text{Area}} \]

In-radius (\(r\)):

\[ r = \frac{\text{Area}}{s} \]

Escribed Radii (\(r_1, r_2, r_3\)):

\[ r_1 = \frac{\text{Area}}{s-a} \]

(and similar forms for \(r_2\) and \(r_3\)).

Chapter Summary

  • Solving Triangles: Use basic trig ratios and Pythagorean theorem for right triangles. Use the Law of Sines, Law of Cosines, and Law of Tangents for oblique triangles.
  • Area Formulas: Calculate area using two sides and an included angle, one side and two angles, or Hero's formula (all three sides).
  • Circles & Triangles: Understand the definitions and formulas for the radii of the circum-circle (\(R\)), in-circle (\(r\)), and escribed circles (\(r_1, r_2, r_3\)).
