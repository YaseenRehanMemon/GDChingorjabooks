1. The Nature of Behaviour

Behaviour is how an organism responds to changes in its environment. These responses are adaptive, meaning they increase the organism's chances of survival and reproduction. The scientific study of animal behaviour is called Ethology.

Stimulus and Response

A stimulus is any detectable change (physical or chemical) in the environment that causes a response. Organisms have sensory cells (receptors) that detect stimuli and send signals to the central nervous system (CNS), which then interprets the signal and coordinates a response.

Types of Responses

  • Positive Response: An action that moves an organism towards a stimulus (e.g., a person feeling happy about good news).
  • Negative Response: An action that moves an organism away from a stimulus (e.g., a dog hiding to avoid a bath).
  • Ignored Response: A decision by an organism to not react to a stimulus (e.g., ignoring a slight change in room temperature).

Heredity and Biological Rhythms

Behaviour is influenced by an organism's genes (heredity), which control the development of the nervous and hormonal systems. Many behaviours follow cyclical patterns called biological rhythms, controlled by an internal biological clock.

  • Circadian Rhythms: 24-hour cycles of activity (day-night).
  • Diurnal: Active during the day (e.g., birds, butterflies).
  • Nocturnal: Active at night (e.g., owls, bats).
  • Crepuscular: Active at dawn and dusk (e.g., mosquitoes).

2. Innate Behaviour

Innate behaviour is genetically determined and does not require any prior experience or learning. It is often referred to as inborn or instinctive behaviour and is performed similarly by all members of a species. Innate behaviours are crucial for the survival of animals, especially those with short lifespans.

Key Definition: Innate Behaviour

An inherited pattern of behaviour that an animal performs correctly the first time it is exposed to a specific stimulus, without any prior practice.

Orientation Behaviours

These are behaviours where an organism moves in a specific way in response to a stimulus.

  • Taxis: A directional movement toward (positive taxis) or away from (negative taxis) a stimulus. For example, a moth flying towards light (positive phototaxis).
  • Kinesis: A non-directional change in the rate of movement in response to the intensity of a stimulus. For example, woodlice move faster in dry areas and slower in humid areas to stay in a favorable environment.

Instincts (Fixed Action Patterns)

Instincts are complex, unchangeable sequences of innate behaviours that, once initiated, are usually carried to completion. They are triggered by a specific external cue known as a sign stimulus.

Examples of Instincts

  • Migration of Salmon: Salmon hatch in freshwater, migrate to the ocean to mature, and then instinctively travel hundreds of miles back to their exact birth stream to reproduce.
  • Dances of Honeybees: Scout bees perform complex dances (the round dance and waggle dance) to communicate the direction and distance of a food source to other bees in the hive.
  • Nest Building in Birds: A tailorbird instinctively knows how to build a complex hanging nest using twigs and grass without being taught.
  • Spider's Orb Web: Spiders can spin intricate, functional webs based on a genetically programmed set of rules, without any prior experience.

3. Learning Behaviour

Learning is the modification of behaviour based on specific experiences. It allows an animal to adapt to changing environmental conditions and is more prominent in animals with longer lifespans and more complex nervous systems.

Innate Behaviour Learning Behaviour
Inherited and genetically determined. Acquired through experience or from society.
Permanent and does not change with experience. Can be modified and is not always permanent.
Common in animals with short lifespans. Common in animals with long lifespans.
Example: A newborn baby grasping an object. Example: A person learning to play cricket.

Types of Learning

  • Habituation: The simplest form of learning, where an animal learns to ignore a persistent, harmless stimulus. This is a reversible process. (e.g., a squirrel in a park eventually ignoring humans).
  • Imprinting: A type of learning that is restricted to a specific "critical period" early in an animal's life, where it forms a strong attachment to the first moving object it sees. This is irreversible. (e.g., Konrad Lorenz's geese following him as if he were their mother).
  • Classical Conditioning: An animal learns to associate a neutral stimulus with a significant stimulus. (e.g., Pavlov's dogs salivating at the sound of a bell because they associated it with food).
  • Instrumental Conditioning (Trial-and-Error): An animal learns to associate a voluntary action with a reward or punishment. (e.g., a rat in a Skinner box learning to press a lever for a food reward).
  • Insight Learning: Solving a problem with a sudden understanding of how to do it, without trial-and-error. (e.g., K√∂hler's chimpanzees stacking boxes to reach bananas).

4. Social Behaviour

Social behaviour involves the interactions between two or more individuals of the same species. Living in social groups can help animals avoid predators, find food, and raise offspring.

Agonistic Behaviour & Dominance Hierarchies

Conflicts over resources like food, mates, or territory are often settled through agonistic behaviour, which includes threats, rituals, and sometimes combat. This behaviour often leads to the formation of a dominance hierarchy, a ranking system within the group.

Example: Pecking Order in Chickens

When hens are grouped, they fight until a clear ranking is established. The "alpha" hen can peck all others but is not pecked herself, giving her first access to food and roosting sites. This hierarchy minimizes future conflicts.

Territorial Behaviour

A territory is an area that one or more individuals defend against others of the same species. It is used for feeding, mating, and raising young. Animals like gorillas and baboons use agonistic displays to establish and defend their territories.

Altruism

Altruism is a behaviour that reduces an individual's own fitness but increases the fitness of other individuals in the population. It is common in social insects.

Example: Honeybee Society

A honeybee colony consists of a queen (reproductive female), drones (males), and workers (sterile females). The sterile worker bees sacrifice their own reproduction to care for the queen, forage for food, and defend the hive, ensuring the survival of the queen's offspring and the colony as a whole.

Chapter Summary

  • Behaviour is an organism's response to stimuli, crucial for survival and reproduction.
  • Innate Behaviour is genetically determined and includes simple reflexes (taxis, kinesis) and complex instincts (fixed action patterns like migration and bee dances).
  • Learning Behaviour is the modification of behaviour through experience. Types include habituation, imprinting, classical and instrumental conditioning, and insight learning.
  • Social Behaviour involves interactions within a species. It includes agonistic behaviour to establish dominance hierarchies (pecking order), territoriality, and altruism (self-sacrificing behaviour for the good of the group).
