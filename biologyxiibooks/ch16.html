1. The Human Skeleton

The human skeleton provides the framework for the body, offering support, protection for internal organs, allowing movement, storing minerals, and producing blood cells. An adult human has 206 bones.

Divisions of the Skeleton

  • Axial Skeleton (80 bones): Forms the central axis of the body. It includes the skull, vertebral column, ribs, and sternum.
  • Appendicular Skeleton (126 bones): Consists of the bones of the limbs (arms and legs) and the pectoral (shoulder) and pelvic (hip) girdles that attach them to the axial skeleton.

Structure of Bone and Cartilage

Bones are rigid organs made of collagen and hardened by calcium phosphate. Cartilage is a flexible connective tissue found at the ends of bones in joints, in the nose, and ears. It provides cushioning and reduces friction.

2. Joints

Joints, or articulations, are the functional junctions between two or more bones.

Types of Joints

  • Fibrous Joints: Immovable joints where bones are held together by dense connective tissue (e.g., the sutures of the skull).
  • Cartilaginous Joints: Slightly movable joints where bones are joined by cartilage (e.g., between vertebrae).
  • Synovial Joints: Freely movable joints that are the most common type in the body. They feature a fluid-filled synovial cavity that lubricates the joint. Examples include hinge joints (knee), ball-and-socket joints (hip), and pivot joints (neck).

3. Disorders of the Skeleton

Various conditions can affect the health and function of the skeletal system.

Common Skeletal Disorders

  • Fractures: A break in a bone. The body repairs fractures in stages, starting with a hematoma (blood clot), followed by the formation of a soft and then a hard callus, and finally bone remodeling.
  • Disc Slip: The protrusion of the soft inner part of an intervertebral disc, which can press on spinal nerves and cause pain.
  • Arthritis: Inflammation of the joints, causing pain and stiffness. Osteoarthritis is caused by cartilage wear-and-tear, while Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune disease where the body attacks its own joints.

4. Muscles

Muscles are tissues that contract and relax to produce movement.

Types of Muscles

  • Skeletal Muscle: Attached to bones, responsible for voluntary movements. Its cells are long, cylindrical, and striated (striped).
  • Smooth Muscle: Found in the walls of internal organs like the stomach and blood vessels. It is responsible for involuntary movements. Its cells are spindle-shaped and not striated.
  • Cardiac Muscle: Found only in the heart. It is striated but its contractions are involuntary. Its cells are branched and connected by intercalated discs.

5. Muscle Contraction

Muscle contraction at the cellular level is explained by the Sliding Filament Model.

The Sarcomere and Sliding Filaments

Skeletal muscle fibers are made of myofibrils, which are composed of repeating contractile units called sarcomeres. Each sarcomere consists of overlapping thick filaments (myosin) and thin filaments (actin).

During contraction, myosin heads bind to actin, forming cross-bridges. The myosin heads then pull the actin filaments toward the center of the sarcomere. This sliding movement shortens the sarcomere, and thus the entire muscle fiber, without the filaments themselves changing length. This process is powered by ATP and triggered by the release of calcium ions (\(\text{Ca}^{2+}\)).

Antagonistic Muscles

Muscles can only pull, they cannot push. Therefore, they work in antagonistic pairs. One muscle (the agonist) contracts to produce a movement, while the other (the antagonist) relaxes. For example, to bend the elbow, the biceps (flexor) contracts and the triceps (extensor) relaxes.

Chapter Summary

  • Human Skeleton: Divided into the axial (skull, spine, ribs) and appendicular (limbs, girdles) skeletons, providing support and protection.
  • Joints: Connections between bones, classified as fibrous (immovable), cartilaginous (slightly movable), and synovial (freely movable).
  • Skeletal Disorders: Include fractures, disc slips, and arthritis (inflammation of joints).
  • Muscle Types: Three types exist: skeletal (voluntary, striated), smooth (involuntary, non-striated), and cardiac (involuntary, striated, found in the heart).
  • Muscle Contraction: The Sliding Filament Model describes how myosin (thick) filaments pull on actin (thin) filaments to shorten the sarcomere, causing contraction. This requires ATP and is triggered by calcium.
