1. Human Embryonic Development

Development is the process by which a single-celled zygote becomes a complex, multicellular organism.

Key Stages of Early Development

  1. Fertilization: The fusion of a sperm and egg to form a diploid zygote.
  2. Cleavage: A series of rapid mitotic divisions of the zygote, forming a solid ball of cells called a morula.
  3. Blastulation: The morula develops into a blastocyst, a hollow ball of cells with an inner cell mass that will become the embryo.
  4. Gastrulation: A dramatic reorganization of cells where the blastocyst folds inward to form the three primary germ layers.

The Three Germ Layers

These layers are the foundation for all tissues and organs in the body.

  • Ectoderm (outer layer): Forms the nervous system, skin, and hair.
  • Mesoderm (middle layer): Forms the skeleton, muscles, circulatory system, and reproductive organs.
  • Endoderm (inner layer): Forms the lining of the digestive and respiratory tracts, and glands like the liver and pancreas.

2. Control of Development

Development is a precisely controlled process governed by genetic information and environmental cues.

Key Control Mechanisms

  • Role of the Nucleus: The nucleus contains the genetic blueprint (DNA) that directs development. Hammerling's experiments with Acetabularia algae showed that the nucleus determines the organism's physical traits.
  • Role of the Cytoplasm: The cytoplasm of the egg contains regulatory molecules that are unevenly distributed. This creates differences in daughter cells after cleavage, influencing their developmental fate.
  • Embryonic Induction: A process where one group of embryonic cells (an organizer) influences the development of another. The organizer cells release chemical signals that direct neighboring cells to follow a specific developmental path.

3. Pregnancy and Human Development

Human pregnancy, or gestation, is the period of fetal development within the uterus, lasting approximately 40 weeks (about 9 months). It is divided into three trimesters.

The Three Trimesters

  • First Trimester (Weeks 1-12): The period of most radical change. Gastrulation occurs, and all major organs and body systems begin to form (organogenesis).
  • Second Trimester (Weeks 13-27): The fetus grows rapidly and becomes more active. Details like hair and fingernails form.
  • Third Trimester (Weeks 28-Birth): The fetus continues to grow in size and mass, accumulating fat. The respiratory and circulatory systems mature in preparation for birth.

Placenta and Umbilical Cord

The placenta is a vital organ that forms from embryonic and maternal tissues. It facilitates the exchange of nutrients, gases, and waste products between the mother and the fetus. The umbilical cord connects the fetus to the placenta.

4. Disorders During Embryonic Development

Errors in development can be caused by genetic abnormalities or exposure to harmful environmental factors.

  • Genetic Abnormalities: Spontaneous abortion (miscarriage) is often due to major chromosomal errors in the embryo.
  • Maternal-Derived Abnormalities: Maternal health issues (like diabetes) or infections (like Rubella) can cause birth defects.
  • Neural Tube Defects: Conditions like spina bifida occur when the neural tube fails to close completely during development.

5. Aging

Aging is the process of natural, progressive, and irreversible changes that occur in an organism over time, leading to a decline in physical and physiological function.

Factors Contributing to Aging

  • Genetic Factors: The shortening of telomeres (protective caps on chromosomes) with each cell division limits the number of times a cell can divide. Genes involved in DNA repair also play a crucial role.
  • Extrinsic Factors: Lifestyle choices (diet, exercise) and environmental exposures (pollution, UV radiation) can accelerate aging by causing cellular damage.

Chapter Summary

  • Embryonic Development: Begins with fertilization and proceeds through cleavage, blastulation, and gastrulation, establishing the three germ layers (ectoderm, mesoderm, endoderm).
  • Control of Development: Directed by genetic information in the nucleus and regulatory molecules in the cytoplasm. Embryonic induction is a key process where one group of cells influences the fate of another.
  • Pregnancy: A ~40-week period divided into three trimesters, during which the fetus develops and matures, supported by the placenta.
  • Developmental Disorders: Can arise from genetic errors or harmful maternal exposures.
  • Aging: A complex biological process involving the gradual decline of bodily functions, influenced by genetic factors (like telomere shortening) and environmental damage.
