1. Introduction to Biotechnology

Biotechnology is the use of living organisms or their components to produce useful products or carry out processes. Modern biotechnology largely relies on genetic engineering, the direct manipulation of an organism's genes using recombinant DNA technology.

2. Cloning of Genes

Gene cloning is the process of producing multiple, identical copies of a specific gene.

Recombinant DNA Technology

This is the core technique for gene cloning.

Steps in Creating Recombinant DNA

  1. Isolate Gene of Interest: The desired gene is cut out from the source DNA using restriction enzymes, which act like molecular scissors that cut at specific DNA sequences.
  2. Insert into a Vector: The isolated gene is pasted into a plasmid (a small, circular piece of DNA from bacteria) that has been cut with the same restriction enzyme. The enzyme DNA ligase seals the gene into the plasmid, creating a recombinant DNA molecule.
  3. Transformation: The recombinant plasmid is introduced into a host cell, usually a bacterium like E. coli.
  4. Selection and Cloning: As the host bacteria reproduce, they also replicate the plasmid, making millions of copies of the gene of interest.

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

PCR is a technique used to amplify a specific segment of DNA in a test tube, creating billions of copies in just a few hours. It is an alternative to using bacteria for cloning and is essential for DNA analysis when only a small sample is available.

3. DNA Analysis

Once DNA is cloned or amplified, it can be analyzed using several techniques.

Key Analysis Techniques

  • Gel Electrophoresis: Separates DNA fragments based on their size. An electric current is used to move DNA fragments through a gel matrix; smaller fragments move faster and farther.
  • DNA Sequencing: Determines the exact nucleotide sequence of a piece of DNA.
  • DNA Fingerprinting (RFLP Analysis): A technique to identify individuals based on unique patterns in their DNA, created by cutting the DNA with restriction enzymes and separating the fragments by gel electrophoresis.

4. Transgenic Organisms (GMOs)

A transgenic organism, or Genetically Modified Organism (GMO), is one that has been altered through genetic engineering to contain a gene from a different species.

Applications

  • Transgenic Bacteria: Used to produce valuable proteins like human insulin, human growth hormone, and vaccines.
  • Transgenic Plants: Engineered for desirable traits such as pest resistance (Bt corn), herbicide resistance (Roundup Ready soybeans), or improved nutritional value (Golden Rice).
  • Transgenic Animals: Used to produce pharmaceuticals in their milk ("pharming") or to serve as models for studying human diseases.

5. Biotechnology and Healthcare

Biotechnology has revolutionized medicine, providing new ways to diagnose and treat diseases.

Applications in Healthcare

  • Production of Pharmaceuticals: Manufacturing proteins like insulin and vaccines using transgenic bacteria.
  • Disease Diagnosis: Using DNA probes and PCR to diagnose genetic disorders and detect infectious agents.
  • Gene Therapy: A technique that aims to treat genetic disorders by replacing a defective gene with a normal one. It is still largely experimental but holds great promise.

Chapter Summary

  • Genetic Engineering: The direct manipulation of genes, primarily through recombinant DNA technology.
  • Gene Cloning: Making many copies of a gene, either by inserting it into bacteria via a plasmid or by using the Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR).
  • DNA Analysis: Techniques like gel electrophoresis (separates DNA by size) and DNA sequencing (reads the nucleotide order) are used to study genes.
  • Transgenic Organisms (GMOs): Organisms that contain genes from another species. They are used in agriculture to create pest-resistant crops and in medicine to produce therapeutic proteins.
  • Healthcare Applications: Biotechnology is used to diagnose genetic diseases, produce vaccines and medicines like insulin, and develop new treatments like gene therapy.
