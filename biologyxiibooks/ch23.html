1. Mendel's Laws of Inheritance

Gregor Mendel, through his experiments with pea plants, established the fundamental principles of heredity.

Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation: For any given trait, the two alleles an individual has separate (segregate) during gamete formation, so that each gamete receives only one of the two alleles. This explains the 3:1 phenotypic ratio in a monohybrid cross.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Alleles for different traits are inherited independently of one another. The inheritance of one trait does not affect the inheritance of another. This explains the 9:3:3:1 phenotypic ratio in a dihybrid cross.

A test cross (crossing an individual with a dominant phenotype to a homozygous recessive individual) is used to determine the unknown genotype of the dominant parent.

2. Non-Mendelian Inheritance

Not all traits follow the simple dominant/recessive pattern described by Mendel.

Variations on Mendelian Genetics

  • Incomplete Dominance: The heterozygous phenotype is an intermediate blend of the two homozygous phenotypes (e.g., red and white snapdragons producing pink offspring).
  • Codominance: Both alleles are fully and separately expressed in the heterozygous phenotype (e.g., roan cattle with both red and white hairs; the human AB blood type).
  • Multiple Alleles: A gene that has more than two possible alleles in a population, although an individual can only carry two (e.g., the ABO blood group system, with \(I^A\), \(I^B\), and \(i\) alleles).

3. Polygenic Inheritance and Epistasis

Polygenic Inheritance

Polygenic inheritance occurs when a single trait is controlled by two or more genes. This often results in a continuous range of phenotypes, like human height or skin color.

Epistasis

Epistasis is a phenomenon where the expression of one gene is affected (masked or modified) by one or more other genes at different loci. For example, in Labrador retrievers, one gene determines black/brown pigment, but a second, separate gene determines whether that pigment is deposited in the fur at all, resulting in yellow labs.

4. Gene Linkage and Crossing Over

Genes located close together on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together. This is known as gene linkage.

Crossing Over

Linkage can be broken by crossing over, a process that occurs during meiosis where homologous chromosomes exchange segments. This creates new combinations of alleles (recombinant chromatids) and increases genetic variation.

5. Sex Determination and Sex Linkage

Sex Determination

Sex is determined by specific sex chromosomes. In humans and fruit flies, the system is XX/XY:

  • XX: Female
  • XY: Male

Other systems exist, like the ZZ/ZW system in birds (where the female is heterogametic) and the XO system in grasshoppers.

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Sex-linked traits are traits controlled by genes located on the sex chromosomes. Most are X-linked because the X chromosome is much larger than the Y.

Characteristics of X-linked Recessive Traits

  • They appear much more often in males than in females, as males only have one X chromosome.
  • Affected sons are usually born to unaffected mothers who are carriers.
  • The trait skips generations.
  • Examples include red-green color blindness and hemophilia.

Chapter Summary

  • Mendel's Laws: The Law of Segregation and the Law of Independent Assortment describe the inheritance of traits with dominant and recessive alleles.
  • Non-Mendelian Patterns: Include incomplete dominance (blending), codominance (both expressed), and multiple alleles (e.g., ABO blood types).
  • Polygenic Traits: Traits controlled by multiple genes, resulting in continuous variation (e.g., skin color).
  • Gene Linkage: Genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together, but this can be broken by crossing over during meiosis.
  • Sex-Linked Traits: Traits determined by genes on sex chromosomes (usually the X chromosome). X-linked recessive disorders are more common in males.
