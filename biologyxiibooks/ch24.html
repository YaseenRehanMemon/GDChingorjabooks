1. Concepts of Evolution

Biological evolution is the process of gradual change in the heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. It is the central organizing principle of modern biology.

Early Theories

  • Lamarckism (Inheritance of Acquired Characteristics): Proposed by Jean-Baptiste Lamarck. He suggested that traits acquired during an organism's lifetime could be passed on to its offspring (e.g., a giraffe stretching its neck to reach leaves would have offspring with longer necks). This idea has been largely discredited because acquired traits do not alter an organism's genes.

2. Evidence for Evolution

The theory of evolution is supported by a vast and diverse body of evidence from multiple scientific disciplines.

Lines of Evidence

  • Paleontology (Fossil Record): Fossils show a historical sequence of life forms, with simpler organisms in older rock layers and more complex ones in younger layers. Transitional fossils, like Archaeopteryx, show features of both ancestral and descendant groups (reptiles and birds).
  • Comparative Anatomy:
    • Homologous Structures: Structures with a similar underlying anatomy due to common ancestry, but which may have different functions (e.g., the forelimbs of humans, cats, whales, and bats).
    • Analogous Structures: Structures that serve a similar function but have different evolutionary origins, resulting from convergent evolution (e.g., the wings of a bird and an insect).
  • Biogeography: The geographic distribution of species. The presence of similar species in geographically separated areas (like marsupials in Australia and the Americas) is explained by continental drift from a common supercontinent, Pangaea.
  • Molecular Biology: The universality of the genetic code (DNA) and the similarity in DNA sequences and protein structures between different species provide powerful evidence for a common ancestor.

3. Darwinism and Natural Selection

Charles Darwin, in his book On the Origin of Species (1859), proposed the theory of evolution by natural selection. Alfred Russel Wallace independently conceived of the theory.

Darwin's Key Observations and Inferences

  1. Overproduction: Organisms produce more offspring than can survive.
  2. Variation: Individuals within a population vary in their heritable traits.
  3. Struggle for Existence: Offspring compete for limited resources.
  4. Inference - Survival of the Fittest: Individuals with inherited traits that give them a higher probability of surviving and reproducing in a given environment tend to leave more offspring than other individuals.
  5. Inference - Descent with Modification: This unequal ability of individuals to survive and reproduce will lead to the accumulation of favorable traits in the population over generations.

4. Neo-Darwinism (The Modern Synthesis)

Neo-Darwinism combines Darwin's theory of natural selection with Mendelian genetics. It provides a genetic basis for the variation and inheritance that Darwin observed.

Key Concepts

  • Population Genetics: The study of how allele frequencies in a population change over time. Evolution is defined as a change in allele frequencies.
  • Hardy-Weinberg Theorem: Describes a non-evolving population where allele and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation, provided that only Mendelian segregation and recombination of alleles are at work.
  • Mechanisms of Allele Frequency Change: Natural selection, genetic drift, and gene flow are the primary mechanisms that cause evolution.

Genetic Drift

Genetic drift refers to random fluctuations in allele frequencies from one generation to the next, especially in small populations. It can be caused by:

  • Bottleneck Effect: A sudden reduction in population size due to a disaster, where the surviving population's gene pool may not reflect the original.
  • Founder Effect: When a few individuals become isolated from a larger population and establish a new population whose gene pool differs from the source population.

5. Speciation

Speciation is the evolutionary process by which new biological species arise.

Mechanisms of Speciation

  • Allopatric Speciation: A population is geographically divided into two isolated subpopulations. Over time, genetic differences accumulate (due to mutation, natural selection, and genetic drift) until they become reproductively isolated.
  • Sympatric Speciation: Speciation occurs in populations that live in the same geographic area. It can result from factors like polyploidy (common in plants), habitat differentiation, or sexual selection.

Chapter Summary

  • Evolution: The process of descent with modification, where populations of organisms change over generations.
  • Evidence: Comes from the fossil record, comparative anatomy (homologous and analogous structures), biogeography, and molecular biology.
  • Natural Selection: The primary mechanism of evolution, where individuals with more advantageous heritable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce.
  • Neo-Darwinism: The modern synthesis of Darwinian evolution and Mendelian genetics, focusing on changes in allele frequencies in populations.
  • Speciation: The formation of new species, most commonly occurring when a population becomes reproductively isolated from its parent population (allopatric speciation).
