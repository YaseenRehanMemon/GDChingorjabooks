1. Introduction to Nervous Coordination

The nervous system provides rapid, point-to-point communication between different parts of the body using electrical and chemical signals. This allows for quick responses to internal and external stimuli.

2. The Neuron

The neuron is the fundamental structural and functional unit of the nervous system. It is specialized for transmitting nerve impulses.

Structure of a Neuron

  • Cell Body (Soma): Contains the nucleus and most of the cell's organelles.
  • Dendrites: Highly branched extensions that receive signals from other neurons.
  • Axon: A long extension that transmits signals to other cells. Many axons are insulated by a myelin sheath, which speeds up signal transmission.

Types of Neurons

  • Sensory Neurons: Transmit signals from sensory receptors to the central nervous system (CNS).
  • Interneurons: Located within the CNS, they integrate information and connect sensory and motor neurons.
  • Motor Neurons: Transmit signals from the CNS to effectors (muscles or glands).

3. The Nerve Impulse (Action Potential)

A nerve impulse is an electrical signal that travels along the axon of a neuron. It is a rapid, temporary change in the membrane potential.

Resting Potential

An unstimulated neuron has a resting potential of about -70mV. This is due to the sodium-potassium pump (\(\text{Na}^+/\text{K}^+\) pump), which actively transports more positive ions (\(3 \text{ Na}^+\)) out of the cell than it brings in (\(2 \text{ K}^+\)). The inside of the neuron is negative relative to the outside.

Action Potential

An action potential is an "all-or-none" event triggered when a stimulus causes the membrane potential to reach a threshold (around -55mV).

Steps of an Action Potential

  1. Depolarization: Voltage-gated sodium channels open, and \( \text{Na}^+ \) ions rush into the cell, causing the inside to become positive.
  2. Repolarization: Sodium channels close, and voltage-gated potassium channels open. \( \text{K}^+ \) ions rush out of the cell, restoring the negative charge inside.
  3. Hyperpolarization: The potassium channels are slow to close, causing a brief "undershoot" where the membrane potential becomes more negative than the resting potential.

In myelinated axons, the action potential "jumps" between the gaps in the myelin (Nodes of Ranvier), a process called saltatory conduction, which is much faster.

4. The Synapse: Transmitting the Signal

A synapse is the junction where a signal is transmitted from one neuron to another cell. Most synapses are chemical synapses.

Events at a Chemical Synapse

  1. The action potential arrives at the axon terminal.
  2. Voltage-gated \( \text{Ca}^{2+} \) channels open, and calcium ions rush in.
  3. The influx of calcium causes synaptic vesicles to fuse with the presynaptic membrane, releasing neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft.
  4. Neurotransmitters diffuse across the cleft and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane.
  5. This binding opens ion channels, causing a change in the membrane potential of the postsynaptic cell, either exciting or inhibiting it.

5. The Human Nervous System

The nervous system is broadly divided into two main parts.

Major Divisions

  • Central Nervous System (CNS): Consists of the brain and spinal cord. It is the primary site for integrating information and making decisions.
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): Consists of all the nerves outside the CNS. It connects the CNS to the limbs and organs.
    • The Somatic division controls voluntary movements (skeletal muscles).
    • The Autonomic division controls involuntary functions (heart rate, digestion). The autonomic system is further divided into the sympathetic ("fight-or-flight") and parasympathetic ("rest-and-digest") branches.

Chapter Summary

  • Neuron: The basic unit of the nervous system, composed of a cell body, dendrites, and an axon.
  • Action Potential: A rapid, all-or-none electrical signal that travels down an axon, involving the depolarization and repolarization of the membrane.
  • Synapse: The junction where a signal is transmitted between neurons via chemical messengers called neurotransmitters.
  • CNS: The brain and spinal cord, acting as the body's control center.
  • PNS: Nerves connecting the CNS to the rest of the body, divided into somatic (voluntary) and autonomic (involuntary) systems.
