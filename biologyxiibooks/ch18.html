1. Hormones and the Endocrine System

Chemical coordination relies on the endocrine system, which consists of glands that produce and secrete chemical messengers called hormones directly into the bloodstream. Hormones travel throughout the body but only affect specific target cells that have receptors for them.

Chemical Nature of Hormones

  • Peptide/Protein Hormones: Water-soluble hormones made of amino acid chains (e.g., Insulin, ADH). They cannot cross the cell membrane and bind to surface receptors.
  • Amino Acid Derivatives: Modified amino acids (e.g., Thyroxine, Epinephrine).
  • Steroid Hormones: Lipid-soluble hormones derived from cholesterol (e.g., Testosterone, Estrogen). They can cross the cell membrane and bind to intracellular receptors.

2. The Hypothalamus and Pituitary Gland

The hypothalamus is a region of the brain that acts as the main control center linking the nervous system to the endocrine system. It controls the pituitary gland, often called the "master gland."

The Pituitary Gland

  • Anterior Pituitary: Produces and releases several hormones that regulate other endocrine glands (tropic hormones). Key hormones include Growth Hormone (GH), Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH), and Follicle-Stimulating Hormone (FSH).
  • Posterior Pituitary: Stores and releases two hormones made by the hypothalamus:
    • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Regulates water balance by increasing water reabsorption in the kidneys.
    • Oxytocin: Stimulates uterine contractions during childbirth and milk release.

3. The Thyroid and Parathyroid Glands

These glands are located in the neck and are crucial for metabolism and calcium regulation.

Thyroid Gland

Produces hormones that regulate metabolism.

  • Thyroxine (T4) and Triiodothyronine (T3): Increase metabolic rate, oxygen consumption, and heat production.
  • Calcitonin: Lowers blood calcium levels by stimulating calcium deposition in bones.

Parathyroid Glands

Produce Parathyroid Hormone (PTH), which has the opposite effect of calcitonin. PTH raises blood calcium levels by stimulating its release from bones and reabsorption by the kidneys.

4. The Pancreas

The pancreas has both exocrine (digestive enzymes) and endocrine functions. The endocrine cells are located in clusters called the Islets of Langerhans.

Regulation of Blood Glucose

Two antagonistic hormones from the pancreas maintain blood glucose homeostasis:

  • Insulin (from Beta cells): Released when blood sugar is high. It stimulates cells to take up glucose from the blood, lowering blood glucose levels.
  • Glucagon (from Alpha cells): Released when blood sugar is low. It stimulates the liver to break down glycogen and release glucose into the blood, raising blood glucose levels.

Diabetes Mellitus is a disorder caused by a deficiency of insulin or a decreased response to it.

5. The Adrenal Glands and Gonads

Adrenal Glands

Located on top of the kidneys, each adrenal gland has two parts:

  • Adrenal Medulla (inner): Secretes epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine, which mediate the "fight-or-flight" response to short-term stress.
  • Adrenal Cortex (outer): Secretes steroid hormones (corticosteroids) that regulate long-term stress responses, blood pressure, and metabolism.

The Gonads (Testes and Ovaries)

The gonads produce sex hormones responsible for the development of primary and secondary sexual characteristics.

  • Testes (male): Produce androgens, primarily testosterone.
  • Ovaries (female): Produce estrogens and progesterone.

6. Feedback Mechanisms in Hormone Regulation

Hormone levels are primarily controlled by negative feedback loops, which maintain homeostasis.

Negative Feedback

The final product of a pathway inhibits an earlier step in the pathway. For example, high levels of thyroxine in the blood inhibit the hypothalamus and pituitary from releasing TRH and TSH, thereby reducing thyroxine production.

Positive Feedback

The final product stimulates an earlier step, amplifying the response. This is rare but occurs during processes like childbirth, where oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions, which in turn stimulate more oxytocin release.

Chapter Summary

  • Endocrine System: A system of glands that secrete hormones into the bloodstream to regulate body functions.
  • Hypothalamus & Pituitary: The "master control center." The hypothalamus controls the pituitary, which in turn regulates many other glands like the thyroid and adrenals.
  • Blood Glucose Regulation: Maintained by the pancreatic hormones insulin (lowers glucose) and glucagon (raises glucose).
  • Calcium Regulation: Controlled by calcitonin (lowers calcium) from the thyroid and parathyroid hormone (raises calcium).
  • Stress Response: Managed by hormones from the adrenal glands (epinephrine and corticosteroids).
  • Regulation: Hormone secretion is tightly controlled, primarily through negative feedback loops to maintain homeostasis.
