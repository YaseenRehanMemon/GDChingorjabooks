Introduction to Fluid Dynamics

Fluid dynamics is the study of fluids (liquids and gases) in motion. Unlike fluid statics, this branch of physics deals with the complex behaviors of flowing substances, from a smoothly flowing river to turbulent smoke rising in the air.

Viscosity & Drag

Real fluids are not "ideal"; they resist flow. This internal friction within a fluid is called viscosity.

Viscosity (η)

Viscosity is a measure of a fluid's resistance to flow. A fluid with high viscosity (like honey) flows slowly, while a fluid with low viscosity (like water) flows easily. It is caused by the cohesive forces between molecules in the fluid.

Viscous Force (Drag)

When an object moves through a fluid, it experiences a retarding force, or drag, due to the fluid's viscosity. This force opposes the object's motion.

Stokes' Law

For a small sphere moving slowly through a fluid, the drag force is given by Stokes' Law:

Fdrag = 6πηrv

Where η is the viscosity, r is the radius of the sphere, and v is its velocity.

Terminal Velocity

When an object falls through a fluid (like a raindrop in air), the drag force increases as its speed increases. Eventually, the drag force becomes equal and opposite to the force of gravity. At this point, the net force is zero, the acceleration stops, and the object falls at a constant maximum speed called terminal velocity.

Types of Fluid Flow

The motion of a fluid can be categorized into different types of flow.

Type of Flow Description
Laminar (Streamline) Flow A smooth, orderly flow where fluid particles move in parallel layers or "streamlines" without mixing. Occurs at low speeds.
Turbulent Flow A chaotic, irregular flow characterized by eddies and whirlpools. Occurs at high speeds and wastes energy. Most practical fluid flow is turbulent.
Ideal Fluid A theoretical fluid that is incompressible (constant density) and has zero viscosity. This simplifies many calculations.

Equation of Continuity

The equation of continuity is a statement of the conservation of mass for a flowing fluid. It describes how the flow speed changes as the size of the pipe changes.

The Equation of Continuity

ρ1A1v1 = ρ2A2v2

For an incompressible fluid (where density ρ is constant):

A1v1 = A2v2

A = Cross-sectional area of the pipe
v = Fluid velocity

What it Means

The product of the area and velocity (Av) is constant. This means that where the pipe is wider (large A), the fluid flows slower (small v). Where the pipe is narrower (small A), the fluid flows faster (large v). This is why water speeds up when you cover part of a hose opening with your thumb.

Bernoulli's Principle

Bernoulli's principle is a statement of the conservation of energy for an ideal fluid in motion. It relates the pressure, velocity, and height of a moving fluid.

Bernoulli's Equation

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

P = Pressure
ρ = Density of the fluid
v = Velocity of the fluid
g = Acceleration due to gravity
h = Height

The Bernoulli Effect

A key consequence of this principle is the Bernoulli effect: where the speed of a fluid is high, its pressure is low, and where the speed is low, the pressure is high.

Practical Applications:

  • Aerofoil (Airplane Wing): The wing is shaped so that air travels faster over the top surface than the bottom. This creates lower pressure on top and higher pressure below, generating an upward force called lift.
  • Venturi Meter: Measures fluid speed by measuring the pressure drop in a constricted section of a pipe.
  • Atomizers/Sprayers: Fast-moving air across the top of a tube creates low pressure, which draws the liquid up and out as a fine mist.

Chapter Summary

Fluid dynamics describes the complex behavior of fluids in motion, based on principles of conservation.

  • Viscosity is the internal friction in a fluid that causes a drag force on moving objects, leading to a constant terminal velocity for falling objects.
  • Fluid flow can be smooth and orderly (laminar) or chaotic and irregular (turbulent).
  • The Equation of Continuity (A1v1 = A2v2) is based on the conservation of mass and states that fluid flows faster through narrower sections of a pipe.
  • Bernoulli's Principle (P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant) is based on the conservation of energy. It leads to the Bernoulli effect: fast-moving fluids have low pressure, and slow-moving fluids have high pressure.
  • These principles explain many phenomena, including how airplanes fly, how spray bottles work, and how blood flows in our veins.
