Sound Waves

Sound is a mechanical wave that propagates through a medium (like air, water, or solids) as vibrations. Specifically, it is a longitudinal wave, meaning the particles of the medium vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel.

Characteristics of Sound

  • Production: Sound is produced by vibrating objects (e.g., vocal cords, guitar strings, speaker cones).
  • Propagation: It travels through a medium by creating alternating regions of high pressure (compressions) and low pressure (rarefactions).
  • Medium Requirement: Sound cannot travel in a vacuum because there are no particles to vibrate.

Speed of Sound

The speed of sound depends on the properties of the medium it is traveling through, primarily its elasticity and density.

General Formula: \(v = \sqrt{\frac{\text{Elastic Modulus}}{\text{Density}}}\)

Factors Affecting the Speed of Sound in Air

  • Temperature: The speed of sound increases as the temperature increases. At \(0^\circ C\), the speed is about 331 m/s; it increases by about 0.6 m/s for every \(1^\circ C\) rise.
  • Density: Sound travels faster in less dense media.
  • Medium: Sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases because of the differences in particle spacing and intermolecular forces.

Newton's Formula & Laplace's Correction

Newton initially assumed the propagation of sound was an isothermal (constant temperature) process, which gave a value about 16% lower than the experimental speed. Laplace corrected this by proposing that the process is adiabatic (no heat exchange), as the compressions and rarefactions happen too quickly. This correction led to the accurate formula for the speed of sound.

Interference and Beats

When two or more sound waves overlap, they interfere with each other according to the principle of superposition.

Constructive Interference

Occurs when waves are in phase (crest meets crest). The resultant amplitude is the sum of the individual amplitudes, resulting in a louder sound.

Destructive Interference

Occurs when waves are out of phase (crest meets trough). The resultant amplitude is the difference of the individual amplitudes, resulting in a softer sound. If amplitudes are equal, it results in silence.

Beats

Beats are the periodic variations in loudness (amplitude) produced by the interference of two sound waves with slightly different frequencies. The number of beats heard per second is equal to the difference between the two frequencies.

\(f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2|\)

This phenomenon is used to tune musical instruments.

Standing Waves in Sound

A standing wave is formed from the interference of two waves traveling in opposite directions. In sound, this occurs when waves reflect from a boundary, such as in an organ pipe or on a guitar string.

Nodes and Antinodes

  • Nodes: Points of zero amplitude (destructive interference).
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum amplitude (constructive interference).

Modes of Vibration (Harmonics)

Strings and air columns can vibrate at specific frequencies called harmonics or overtones.

  • Fundamental Frequency (First Harmonic): The lowest possible frequency of vibration.
  • Overtones: Higher frequencies, which are integer multiples of the fundamental.

The Doppler Effect

The Doppler effect is the apparent change in the frequency of a wave in relation to an observer who is moving relative to the wave source. It is the reason the pitch of a siren changes as an ambulance passes you.

Key Scenarios

  • Source Moving Towards Observer: The observer perceives a higher frequency (higher pitch).
  • Source Moving Away From Observer: The observer perceives a lower frequency (lower pitch).

Applications:

The Doppler effect is used in many technologies, including RADAR for weather forecasting and speed detection, SONAR for mapping the ocean floor, and in astronomy to determine the motion of stars and galaxies.

Chapter Summary

Acoustics is the study of sound, a longitudinal mechanical wave that requires a medium to travel.

  • The speed of sound depends on the medium's elasticity, density, and temperature.
  • Interference occurs when sound waves overlap. Interference of two waves with slightly different frequencies produces beats (\(f_{\text{beat}} = |f_1 - f_2|\)).
  • Standing waves are produced by the interference of a wave with its reflection, creating points of no vibration (nodes) and maximum vibration (antinodes). They are responsible for the sounds made by musical instruments.
  • The Doppler effect is the observed change in frequency due to relative motion between a sound source and an observer. The frequency increases as they approach and decreases as they move apart.
