Coulomb's Law: The Force Between Charges

The fundamental force between two stationary charged particles is called the electrostatic force. Charles-Augustin de Coulomb was the first to quantify this force.

Coulomb's Law

The magnitude of the electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Formula: F = \(\frac{k \cdot |q_1q_2|}{r^2}\)

F = Electrostatic Force (in Newtons, N)
k = Coulomb's constant (≈ 8.99 x 10⁹ N·m²/C²)
q₁, q₂ = Magnitudes of the charges (in Coulombs, C)
r = Distance between the charges (in meters, m)

The direction of the force is along the line connecting the two charges. It is repulsive if the charges have the same sign and attractive if they have opposite signs.

The Electric Field

The concept of a field helps us understand how forces can act over a distance. A charge creates an electric field in the space around it, and this field then exerts a force on any other charge that enters it.

Defining the Electric Field (E)

The electric field at any point in space is defined as the electrostatic force that would be exerted on a small positive "test charge" placed at that point, divided by the magnitude of the test charge.

E = F / q₀

The unit of the electric field is Newtons per Coulomb (N/C). The direction of the electric field is the direction of the force on a positive charge.

Electric Field of a Point Charge

Using the definition of the electric field and Coulomb's Law, we can find the field created by a single point charge 'q'.

E = \(\frac{k \cdot |q|}{r^2}\)

Electric fields from multiple charges add up as vectors (the principle of superposition).

Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges separated by a small distance. Dipoles are important because they model the behavior of many molecules and are fundamental to understanding how materials interact with electric fields.

Electric Flux

Electric flux is a measure of the "flow" of the electric field through a given surface. It's a way of quantifying the number of electric field lines that pass through an area.

Calculating Electric Flux (ΦE)

For a uniform electric field passing through a flat area:

ΦE = E ⋅ A = EA cos(θ)

E = Electric field strength
A = Area of the surface
θ = Angle between the electric field lines and the normal (perpendicular) to the surface.

Electric Potential

Just as we used energy to understand mechanics, we can use the concept of electric potential energy to understand electrostatics. From this, we define electric potential, commonly known as voltage.

Electric Potential Energy (U)

This is the potential energy a charge has due to its position in an electric field. Work must be done to move a charge against an electric field, and this work is stored as potential energy.

Electric Potential (V)

Electric potential is the electric potential energy per unit charge. It is a scalar quantity and is often referred to as voltage.

V = U / q or V = W / q

The unit of electric potential is the Volt (V), where 1 Volt = 1 Joule per Coulomb.

Potential Difference

The potential difference (or voltage) between two points is the work done per unit charge in moving a charge from one point to the other. It is the change in electric potential, ΔV.

Electron Volt (eV)

The electron volt is a unit of energy. It is defined as the energy gained by an electron when it is accelerated through a potential difference of 1 volt.

1 eV = 1.602 x 10⁻¹⁹ J

Relationship Between Electric Field and Potential

The electric field and electric potential are intimately related. The electric field points in the direction of the steepest decrease in potential.

Field from Potential

The magnitude of the electric field is the change in potential per unit distance, also known as the negative potential gradient.

E = - ΔV / Δd

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduces the fundamental concepts of stationary charges, the forces they exert, and the fields and potentials they create.

  • Coulomb's Law (F = \(\frac{k|q_1q_2|}{r^2}\)) describes the force between two point charges.
  • An Electric Field (E = \(\frac{F}{q}\)) is a region of space around a charge that exerts a force on other charges. For a point charge, E = \(\frac{k|q|}{r²}\).
  • Electric Flux (Φ = EAcosθ) measures the number of field lines passing through a surface.
  • Electric Potential (V = \(\frac{W}{q}\)), or voltage, is the work done per unit charge. It is a scalar quantity measured in Volts.
  • The Potential Difference (ΔV) is what drives current in a circuit.
  • The electric field is related to the potential by the negative potential gradient: E = - ΔV / Δd.
