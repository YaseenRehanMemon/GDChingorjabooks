What is Dynamics?

Dynamics is the branch of mechanics that studies why objects move the way they do. It focuses on **forces** and how they cause changes in motion (acceleration). While kinematics describes motion, dynamics explains the causes behind it.

Newton's Laws of Motion

Sir Isaac Newton formulated three fundamental laws that form the foundation of classical mechanics. They explain the relationship between a physical object and the forces acting upon it.

1. Newton's First Law: The Law of Inertia

An object will remain at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a net external force.

  • Inertia is the property of a body to resist changes in its state of motion.
  • The more mass an object has, the more inertia it has. Mass is a measure of inertia.
  • If you're in a car that suddenly stops, your body continues to move forward because of inertia.

2. Newton's Second Law: Force and Acceleration

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. The direction of the acceleration is in the direction of the net force.

Formula: F = ma

F = Net Force (in Newtons, N)
m = Mass (in kilograms, kg)
a = Acceleration (in m/s²)

This law also defines **weight** as the force of gravity acting on a mass: Weight (W) = mg, where 'g' is the acceleration due to gravity.

3. Newton's Third Law: Action and Reaction

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

  • Forces always occur in pairs. If Object A exerts a force on Object B, then Object B simultaneously exerts an equal and opposite force on Object A.
  • Example: A rocket pushes hot gases downward (action), and the gases push the rocket upward (reaction).

Momentum & Impulse

To understand complex interactions like collisions, we need the concepts of momentum and impulse.

Linear Momentum (p)

Momentum is often called the "quantity of motion." It is the product of an object's mass and its velocity.

p = mv

Momentum is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

Impulse (J)

Impulse is the change in momentum of an object. It is equal to the force applied multiplied by the time interval over which the force acts.

J = FΔt = Δp

A large force for a short time can produce the same impulse as a small force for a long time.

Relation between Force and Momentum

Newton's Second Law can also be stated in terms of momentum: "The net force acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of its momentum."

F = Δp / Δt

Collisions

A collision is any event in which two or more bodies exert forces on each other in a relatively short time. The law of conservation of momentum is crucial for analyzing all collisions.

Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum

If no net external force acts on a system, the total linear momentum of the system remains constant.

Total pinitial = Total pfinal

This principle is always true for any isolated collision, whether it's between billiard balls or galaxies.

Types of Collisions

Type of Collision Description Example
Elastic Collision Both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. Objects bounce off each other perfectly without any loss of energy. Collisions between billiard balls or atoms.
Inelastic Collision Momentum is conserved, but kinetic energy is not. Some energy is converted into heat, sound, or deformation. If objects stick together, it's a perfectly inelastic collision. A car crash where the vehicles get damaged; a clay ball hitting a wall and sticking to it.

Application: How a Rocket Works

A rocket is a prime example of momentum conservation. It expels hot gas (fuel) at high velocity out of its back. By the law of conservation of momentum, as the gas gains momentum in one direction, the rocket must gain an equal and opposite momentum, pushing it forward. This works even in the vacuum of space because the rocket is not pushing against anything external—it's an isolated system.

Chapter Summary

Dynamics explains the 'why' behind motion, focusing on forces. Here are the core concepts for revision:

  • Newton's First Law: Objects resist changes in motion (inertia). Mass is the measure of inertia.
  • Newton's Second Law: The relationship between force, mass, and acceleration is defined by F = ma. It also defines weight as W = mg.
  • Newton's Third Law: Forces always come in equal and opposite action-reaction pairs.
  • Momentum (p = mv) is the quantity of motion. Impulse (J = FΔt) is the change in momentum.
  • Law of Conservation of Momentum: In any isolated system (like a collision or a rocket firing), the total momentum before the event is equal to the total momentum after.
  • Elastic collisions conserve both momentum and kinetic energy. Inelastic collisions conserve momentum but lose kinetic energy.
