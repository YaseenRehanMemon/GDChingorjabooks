Communication Channels

A communication channel is the medium used to transmit a signal from a sender (transmitter) to a receiver. Modern communication relies on a variety of advanced channels.

Channel Type Description Relative Merits
Wire-Pairs Two insulated copper wires twisted together (e.g., telephone lines, Ethernet cables). Inexpensive, easy to install, but susceptible to interference and limited bandwidth.
Coaxial Cables A central copper core surrounded by an insulator and a braided metal shield (e.g., cable TV). Higher bandwidth and less interference than wire-pairs.
Optical Fibers Thin strands of glass or plastic that transmit information as pulses of light. Extremely high bandwidth, immune to electromagnetic interference, very secure, low signal loss.
Radio & Microwave Links Uses electromagnetic waves to transmit signals through the air. Requires line-of-sight for microwaves. Wireless, covers large areas, but can be affected by weather and obstacles.
Satellites Acts as a relay station in space, receiving signals from Earth and retransmitting them to other locations. Provides global coverage, ideal for broadcasting and reaching remote areas.

Modulation: Encoding Information

Modulation is the process of superimposing a low-frequency information signal (like voice or data) onto a high-frequency carrier wave. This is necessary for efficient transmission, especially over radio waves.

Amplitude Modulation (AM)

The amplitude of the carrier wave is varied in proportion to the information signal, while the frequency remains constant.

Advantages: Simple, requires less bandwidth.
Disadvantages: Highly susceptible to noise and interference.

Frequency Modulation (FM)

The frequency of the carrier wave is varied in proportion to the information signal, while the amplitude remains constant.

Advantages: High-quality transmission, resistant to noise.
Disadvantages: Requires a wider bandwidth.

Bandwidth

Bandwidth refers to the range of frequencies required to transmit a signal without significant distortion. FM requires a larger bandwidth than AM.

Digital vs. Analog Transmission

Information can be transmitted in two forms: analog or digital.

Analog Transmission

The signal is a continuous wave that is analogous to the original information (e.g., a vinyl record). It is prone to degradation by noise.

Digital Transmission

The information is converted into a series of discrete binary values (1s and 0s). This form is much more robust and is the basis of modern communication.

Advantages of Digital Transmission

  • Noise Immunity: Digital signals are much less affected by noise. Repeaters can regenerate the signal perfectly.
  • Data Compression: Digital data can be easily compressed to save bandwidth.
  • Security: Digital signals can be encrypted for secure communication.
  • Multiplexing: It is easier to combine multiple digital signals into one channel.

Digital Transmission of Analog Signals

Since most real-world information (like sound) is analog, it must be converted to a digital format before transmission. This is done by an Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC).

The ADC Process

  1. Sampling: The amplitude of the continuous analog signal is measured at regular, discrete time intervals. The sampling rate must be at least twice the highest frequency in the signal (Nyquist theorem).
  2. Quantizing: Each sample's amplitude is rounded to the nearest value on a finite scale of discrete levels.
  3. Encoding: Each quantized level is assigned a unique binary code (a string of 1s and 0s).

After transmission, a Digital-to-Analog Converter (DAC) at the receiver reverses the process to reconstruct an approximation of the original analog signal.

Chapter Summary

Modern communication systems rely on transmitting information efficiently and accurately over various channels.

  • Information is carried through channels like wire-pairs, coaxial cables, optical fibers, and wireless links (radio, microwave, satellite).
  • Modulation is the process of encoding an information signal onto a high-frequency carrier wave. Key types are Amplitude Modulation (AM) and Frequency Modulation (FM).
  • Digital transmission offers significant advantages over analog, including better noise immunity, security, and efficiency.
  • To transmit analog information digitally, it must first be converted using an Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) through a process of sampling, quantizing, and encoding.
  • At the receiving end, a Digital-to-Analog Converter (DAC) reconstructs the original signal.
