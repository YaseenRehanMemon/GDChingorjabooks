Introduction to Physics

Physics is the fundamental science that studies matter, energy, and their interactions. From the smallest subatomic particles to the largest galaxies, physics explains how the universe works. Everything around us—a flying bird, a moving car, or a smartphone—operates on the principles of physics.

Scope of Physics in Our World

  • Science: It provides the fundamental laws that form the basis for other sciences like chemistry and biology.
  • Technology: Principles of physics are behind almost all modern technology, including electricity, computers, and medical imaging (like X-rays and MRI).
  • Society: Physics helps us understand and address global challenges like climate change, energy production, and space exploration.

Physical Quantities & SI Units

To measure and describe the world, we use physical quantities. To keep measurements consistent everywhere, scientists use the International System of Units (SI).

Three Types of SI Units

  1. Base Units: The seven fundamental units upon which all others are built.
  2. Derived Units: Formed by combining base units (e.g., speed is \(\frac{meters}{second}\)).
  3. Supplementary Units: Dimensionless units for measuring angles (radian and steradian).

Dimensional Analysis

Dimensional analysis is a powerful tool used to check if a physics equation is consistent. Every physical quantity has a dimension, represented by symbols like [L] for length, [M] for mass, and [T] for time.

Principle of Homogeneity

An equation is dimensionally correct only if the dimensions on both sides are the same. You can't add apples and oranges, and you can't equate length to time!

Example: For the equation Speed = Distance / Time
Dimensions: [L]/[T] = [L]/[T]. The equation is correct.

We can also use dimensions to derive formulas in simple cases, though it doesn't give us the value of dimensionless constants (like 2π).

Errors & Uncertainty

No measurement is ever perfect. Every measurement has some uncertainty, which reflects the limitations of the instrument and the person making the measurement.

Systematic Errors

These are consistent, repeatable errors caused by faulty equipment or a flawed experimental setup (e.g., a scale that always reads 2g too high). They can often be identified and corrected.

Random Errors

These are unpredictable fluctuations in measurements (e.g., slight variations in reading a ruler). They can be minimized by taking multiple readings and calculating an average.

Assessing Uncertainty

Uncertainty tells us the range within which the true value likely lies.
  • Absolute Uncertainty: The direct uncertainty in a measurement (e.g., 5.2 ± 0.1 cm).
  • Fractional Uncertainty: (Absolute Uncertainty) / (Measured Value)
  • Percentage Uncertainty: Fractional Uncertainty × 100%

Measurement & Precision

Understanding the quality of a measurement involves knowing about significant figures, precision, and accuracy.

Accuracy vs. Precision

Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true, accepted value.

Precision: How close multiple measurements are to each other. You can be precise without being accurate!

Least Count (Resolution)

The smallest increment a measuring instrument can detect. A smaller least count means a more precise instrument, which is crucial for reducing uncertainty.

Significant Figures

Significant figures are the meaningful digits in a measurement. They indicate the precision of the instrument used.

Key Rules for Significant Figures

  • Non-zero digits are always significant. (e.g., 123 has 3)
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant. (e.g., 101 has 3)
  • Leading zeros are not significant. (e.g., 0.054 has 2)
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant. (e.g., 2.500 has 4)
  • In calculations (multiplication/division), the result should have the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest significant figures.

Working with Graphs

Graphs are a powerful way to visualize the relationship between variables and analyze experimental data.

Dependent & Independent Variables

  • Independent Variable: The variable you change or control (plotted on the x-axis).
  • Dependent Variable: The variable you measure, which changes in response (plotted on the y-axis).

Interpreting Graphs

  • Slope: Represents the rate of change (rise/run). In a distance-time graph, the slope is velocity.
  • Intercept: The point where the line crosses an axis, often representing an initial value.
  • Line of Best Fit: A straight line drawn through the center of a group of data points to show the general trend.
  • Error Bars: Lines on data points that show the uncertainty in each measurement. Shorter bars mean more precise data.
  • Extrapolation: Extending a graph beyond the measured data to predict values.

Chapter Summary

This chapter introduced the fundamentals of physics and measurement. Here are the key takeaways for quick revision:

  • Physics is the study of matter, energy, and their interactions, forming the basis for technology and other sciences.
  • SI Units provide a global standard for measurement, categorized into base, derived, and supplementary units.
  • Dimensional Analysis is used to check the consistency of equations by comparing the dimensions ([M], [L], [T]) on both sides.
  • All measurements have uncertainty. Errors can be systematic (consistent flaws) or random (unpredictable variations).
  • Accuracy is closeness to the true value, while Precision is the consistency of repeated measurements.
  • Significant Figures indicate the precision of a measurement. Rules for their use must be followed in calculations.
  • Graphs are essential tools for analyzing data by visualizing the relationship between independent and dependent variables through slopes, intercepts, and lines of best fit.
