The Nature of Light: Wave-Particle Duality

Physical optics explores phenomena that can only be explained by treating light as a wave. This contrasts with geometric optics, which treats light as rays. Light is an electromagnetic wave, a transverse wave consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields.

Huygens' Principle

This principle provides a model for how waves propagate. It states that every point on a wavefront can be considered a source of secondary spherical wavelets. The new wavefront at a later time is the surface tangent to all these wavelets. This principle is key to understanding both interference and diffraction.

Interference of Light

Interference is the phenomenon that occurs when two or more waves overlap, resulting in a new wave of greater, lower, or the same amplitude. For this to be easily observed with light, the sources must be coherent (having a constant phase relationship).

Constructive Interference

Occurs when waves meet in phase (crest meets crest). This results in a brighter light (a bright fringe or maximum).

Condition: Path difference = nλ

Destructive Interference

Occurs when waves meet out of phase (crest meets trough). This results in darkness (a dark fringe or minimum).

Condition: Path difference = (n + ½)λ

Young's Double-Slit Experiment

This classic experiment, performed by Thomas Young, provided the first definitive evidence for the wave theory of light. It demonstrated that light exhibits interference.

The Setup

Monochromatic light is passed through a single slit and then through two parallel, closely spaced slits. The light waves diffract at the slits and then interfere with each other, creating a pattern of alternating bright and dark fringes on a screen.

Fringe Position & Spacing

Position of bright fringes: d sin(θ) = nλ
Position of dark fringes: d sin(θ) = (n + ½)λ
Fringe spacing: Δx = λL / d

Where 'd' is the slit separation, 'L' is the distance to the screen, and 'λ' is the wavelength of light.

Interference in Thin Films

The shimmering colors seen in soap bubbles, oil slicks, and peacock feathers are caused by the interference of light waves reflecting off the top and bottom surfaces of a thin film.

Phase Change on Reflection

A crucial factor is that light undergoes a 180Â° phase change when it reflects from a medium with a higher refractive index. This is equivalent to adding half a wavelength to the path difference.

Newton's Rings

A specific example is Newton's rings, an interference pattern created by the reflection of light between a spherical surface and an adjacent flat surface. This creates a circular pattern of bright and dark rings.

Diffraction: The Bending of Waves

Diffraction is the bending of waves as they pass through an opening (aperture) or around an obstacle. The effect is most noticeable when the size of the opening is comparable to the wavelength of the wave.

Single-Slit Diffraction

When light passes through a single narrow slit, it diffracts and creates a pattern with a large, bright central maximum, flanked by dimmer and narrower secondary maxima.

Condition for minima (dark fringes):
a sin(θ) = nλ

(Here 'a' is the width of the slit)

Diffraction Grating

A diffraction grating is a device with a large number of equally spaced parallel slits. It produces interference patterns that are much sharper and brighter than a double slit, making it ideal for separating light into its constituent wavelengths (like a prism).

Condition for maxima (bright fringes):
d sin(θ) = nλ

(Here 'd' is the distance between slits)

X-Ray Diffraction

Because X-rays have very short wavelengths, they can be diffracted by the regularly spaced atoms in a crystal lattice. This technique is a powerful tool for determining the atomic structure of crystalline solids.

Chapter Summary

Physical optics demonstrates the wave nature of light through the phenomena of interference and diffraction.

  • Huygens' Principle describes how waves propagate and is the basis for understanding wave optics.
  • Interference occurs when coherent waves superpose, leading to constructive (bright) and destructive (dark) patterns. Young's double-slit experiment was a key proof of this.
  • The colorful patterns on thin films (like soap bubbles) are caused by the interference of reflected light waves.
  • Diffraction is the bending of light as it passes an obstacle or through a narrow opening.
  • A diffraction grating uses many slits to separate light into a spectrum, based on the principle of diffraction and interference.
  • X-ray diffraction uses the atoms in a crystal as a natural grating to study crystal structures.
