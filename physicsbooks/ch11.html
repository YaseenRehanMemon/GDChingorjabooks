Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

An oscillation is a repetitive, back-and-forth motion about an equilibrium position. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is the simplest and most fundamental type of oscillation.

Conditions for SHM

An object undergoes SHM if it experiences a restoring force that is directly proportional to its displacement from the equilibrium position and always acts towards that position.

Hooke's Law: F = -kx

This is the defining condition for SHM.
F = Restoring Force
k = Force constant (a measure of stiffness)
x = Displacement from equilibrium
The negative sign indicates the force is always opposite to the displacement.

Key Terms in SHM

TermSymbolDescription
AmplitudeA or x₀The maximum displacement from the equilibrium position.
PeriodTThe time taken to complete one full oscillation.
FrequencyfThe number of complete oscillations per second (f = 1/T). Unit: Hertz (Hz).
Angular FrequencyωRelated to frequency by ω = 2πf. It represents the rate of oscillation in radians per second.

Energy in Simple Harmonic Motion

In SHM, there is a continuous conversion between potential energy and kinetic energy. Assuming no friction, the total mechanical energy of the system is conserved.

Potential Energy (PE)

The energy stored due to the object's position. For a mass on a spring, this is elastic potential energy.

PE = ½kx²

PE is maximum at the amplitude (extreme positions) and zero at equilibrium.

Kinetic Energy (KE)

The energy of motion.

KE = ½mv²

KE is maximum at the equilibrium position (where speed is greatest) and zero at the amplitude.

Total Mechanical Energy (E)

The total energy is the sum of KE and PE. It remains constant throughout the motion.

E = KE + PE = ½kA² = Constant

Examples of Oscillating Systems

Two classic examples of systems that exhibit SHM are the mass-spring system and the simple pendulum.

Mass-Spring System

A mass attached to a spring oscillating horizontally or vertically. Its motion is described by Hooke's Law.

Period (T) = 2π√(m/k)

Simple Pendulum

A point mass suspended from a string. For small angles of swing, its motion is approximately SHM.

Period (T) = 2π√(L/g)

Note: The period of a simple pendulum does not depend on its mass.

Damped Oscillations

In real-world systems, friction and air resistance cause the energy of an oscillator to gradually dissipate. This effect is called damping. Damped oscillations are oscillations where the amplitude decreases over time.

Practical Example: Shock Absorbers

A car's suspension system uses shock absorbers to create damped oscillations. When the car hits a bump, the springs oscillate, but the shock absorbers quickly dissipate the energy to prevent the car from bouncing up and down for a long time. This is an example of critical damping, where the system returns to equilibrium as quickly as possible without oscillating.

Forced Oscillations and Resonance

Forced oscillations occur when a periodic driving force is applied to an oscillating system. The system is forced to oscillate at the frequency of the driving force.

Resonance

Resonance is a phenomenon that occurs when the frequency of the driving force is equal or very close to the natural frequency of the oscillating system. When this happens, the system absorbs energy very efficiently from the driving force, leading to a dramatic increase in the amplitude of the oscillations.

Examples of Resonance:

  • Pushing a swing: To get a swing to go higher, you push it in time with its natural frequency.
  • Shattering a glass with sound: If a singer matches the natural frequency of a wine glass, the sound waves can cause the glass to vibrate with such a large amplitude that it shatters.
  • The Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse: Wind provided a periodic driving force that matched the bridge's natural frequency, leading to a catastrophic structural failure.

Chapter Summary

Oscillations are a fundamental type of motion found everywhere in nature and technology.

  • Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM) is a special type of oscillation where the restoring force is proportional to the displacement (F = -kx).
  • The key parameters of SHM are amplitude, period (T), and frequency (f).
  • In SHM, energy is continuously converted between kinetic and potential forms, with the total mechanical energy remaining constant (in the absence of friction).
  • The periods of a mass-spring system (T = 2π√(m/k)) and a simple pendulum (T = 2π√(L/g)) depend on their physical properties.
  • Damping is the gradual decrease in amplitude of an oscillation due to energy loss from forces like friction.
  • Resonance is the large increase in amplitude that occurs when a system is driven at its natural frequency.
