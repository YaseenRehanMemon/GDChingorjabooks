EMF and Potential Difference

To make a direct current (D.C.) flow, we need a source of energy. This is provided by a source of electromotive force (emf).

Electromotive Force (EMF, \(\mathcal{E}\))

EMF is the work done per unit charge by a source (like a battery) to move charge completely around a circuit. It's the "push" that drives the current. Despite its name, it is a potential (voltage), not a force.

Terminal Voltage and Internal Resistance

Real batteries have internal resistance (r), which causes a voltage drop inside the source. The actual voltage available to the external circuit is called the terminal voltage (\(V_t\)).

\(V_t = \mathcal{E} - Ir\)

The terminal voltage is only equal to the EMF when no current is flowing (\(I=0\)).

Resistance and Ohm's Law

Resistance is the opposition to the flow of electric current.

Ohm's Law

\(V = IR\)

\(V\) = Voltage (Potential Difference)
\(I\) = Current (in Amperes, A)
\(R\) = Resistance (in Ohms, \(\Omega\))

Resistivity (\(\rho\))

Resistance depends on the material's properties and its shape. Resistivity is an intrinsic property of a material that describes how strongly it resists electric current.

\(R = \frac{\rho L}{A}\)

Resistance increases with length (L) and decreases with cross-sectional area (A).

Temperature Dependence

For most conductors, resistivity (and thus resistance) increases as temperature increases.

Electric Power

Power is the rate at which energy is delivered or dissipated in a circuit.

Formulas for Electric Power (P)

\(P = IV = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R}\)

The unit of power is the Watt (W), where 1 Watt = 1 Joule/second.

Maximum Power Transfer

For a source with a fixed internal resistance (r), the maximum power is delivered to an external load resistor (R) when the load resistance is equal to the internal resistance (\(R = r\)).

Combinations of Resistors

Resistors can be combined to achieve a desired equivalent resistance (\(R_{eq}\)).

Combination Description Equivalent Resistance (\(R_{eq}\))
Series Resistors are connected end-to-end. The current is the same through each. \(R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \dots\)
Parallel Resistors are connected side-by-side. The voltage is the same across each. \(\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \dots\)

Kirchhoff's Rules for Complex Circuits

For circuits that cannot be simplified by series and parallel combinations, we use Kirchhoff's rules.

1. Junction Rule (Conservation of Charge)

The sum of the currents entering any junction must equal the sum of the currents leaving that junction.

\(\Sigma I_{in} = \Sigma I_{out}\)

2. Loop Rule (Conservation of Energy)

The sum of the potential differences (voltages) around any closed circuit loop must be zero.

\(\Sigma V = 0\)

Measuring Circuits

Specialized circuits are used to measure unknown resistances and EMFs accurately.

Wheatstone Bridge

A circuit used to precisely measure an unknown resistance. It consists of four resistors. When the bridge is "balanced" (zero current flows through the central galvanometer), the unknown resistance can be calculated from the other three known resistances.

Potentiometer

A device used to measure an unknown EMF or potential difference accurately without drawing any current from the source being measured. It works by balancing the unknown voltage against a known voltage along a uniform wire.

Chapter Summary

D.C. circuits involve the steady flow of charge, governed by fundamental principles of energy and charge conservation.

  • EMF is the energy source that drives current, while terminal voltage is the actual voltage supplied to the circuit due to internal resistance.
  • Ohm's Law (\(V=IR\)) relates voltage, current, and resistance for many materials.
  • Resistance (\(R=\frac{\rho L}{A}\)) depends on a material's resistivity and its dimensions.
  • Electric Power (\(P=IV\)) is the rate at which energy is consumed in a circuit.
  • Resistors in series add directly (\(R_{eq} = R_1 + R_2\)), while resistors in parallel add as reciprocals (\(\frac{1}{R_{eq}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}\)).
  • Kirchhoff's Rules (Junction Rule and Loop Rule) are used to analyze complex circuits.
  • The Wheatstone Bridge and Potentiometer are precision instruments for measuring resistance and EMF.
