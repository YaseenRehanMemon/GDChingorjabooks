Angular Kinematics: The Language of Rotation

When an object spins or revolves, we use angular quantities to describe its motion instead of linear ones. This is the kinematics of rotation.

Angular Quantity Symbol Description Unit
Angular Displacement θ The angle through which an object has rotated. radians (rad)
Angular Velocity ω The rate of change of angular displacement (how fast it's spinning). rad/s
Angular Acceleration α The rate of change of angular velocity (how fast its spin is changing). rad/s²

Connecting Linear and Angular Motion

For any point at a distance 'r' from the axis of rotation, its linear (or tangential) motion is related to the angular motion of the object.

Arc Length: s = rθ
Tangential Velocity: v = rω
Tangential Acceleration: a = rα

Centripetal Force & Acceleration

An object moving in a circle is constantly changing its direction, which means it is accelerating, even if its speed is constant. This is called centripetal acceleration.

Centripetal Acceleration (ac)

This is the acceleration that an object experiences when moving in a curved path. It is always directed towards the center of the circle.

ac = v²/r = ω²r

Centripetal Force (Fc)

According to Newton's Second Law, if there's an acceleration, there must be a net force. The force that causes centripetal acceleration is called centripetal force. It is also directed towards the center of the circle.

Fc = mac = mv²/r

Important: Centripetal force is not a new kind of force. It is simply the net force that points towards the center of the circular path. This role can be played by tension, friction, gravity, or a normal force.

Banked Curves

Roads are "banked" on curves to help vehicles turn safely. On a banked curve, a component of the normal force provides the necessary centripetal force, reducing the reliance on friction.

Ideal Banking Angle: tanθ = v²/rg

Orbital Motion

Orbital motion, like a satellite orbiting the Earth, is a perfect example of circular motion where gravity provides the centripetal force.

Orbital Velocity

To stay in a stable orbit of radius R around a celestial body of mass M, a satellite must travel at a specific speed called orbital velocity.

vorbital = √(GM/R)

Here, the centripetal force (mv²/R) is provided by the gravitational force (GMm/R²).

Rotational Dynamics

Just as force causes linear acceleration, torque causes angular acceleration. This section explores the dynamics of spinning objects.

Torque (τ)

Torque is the rotational equivalent of force. It's the "twist" or "turning effect" that a force produces. It is the cross product of the position vector (r) and the force (F).

τ = r × F

Moment of Inertia (I)

Moment of inertia is the rotational equivalent of mass. It measures an object's resistance to changes in its rotational motion. It depends not just on mass, but also on how that mass is distributed relative to the axis of rotation.

I = Σmr²

Newton's Second Law for Rotation

The relationship between torque, moment of inertia, and angular acceleration is the rotational version of F = ma.

τnet = Iα

Angular Momentum (L)

Angular momentum is the rotational equivalent of linear momentum. For a rigid body, it is the product of its moment of inertia and its angular velocity.

L = Iω

Law of Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque on a system is zero, its total angular momentum remains constant. This is why an ice skater spins faster when she pulls her arms in (decreasing her moment of inertia 'I', which causes her angular velocity 'ω' to increase).

Chapter Summary

This chapter connects linear motion to the spinning and revolving world of rotational and circular motion.

  • Angular Kinematics: Motion is described by angular displacement (θ), velocity (ω), and acceleration (α). These relate to linear quantities by the radius 'r' (e.g., v = rω).
  • Circular Motion: Requires a centripetal force (Fc = mv²/r) directed towards the center of the circle to produce a centripetal acceleration (ac = v²/r).
  • Orbital Velocity: A specific case where gravity provides the centripetal force required to keep an object in orbit.
  • Torque (τ): The rotational equivalent of force; it causes an object to rotate.
  • Moment of Inertia (I): The rotational equivalent of mass; it's the resistance to angular acceleration.
  • Angular Momentum (L = Iω): The rotational equivalent of linear momentum. It is conserved when no net external torque acts on a system.
  • Newton's Second Law for Rotation: The net torque on an object is equal to its moment of inertia times its angular acceleration (τ = Iα).
