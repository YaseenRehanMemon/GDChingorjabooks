Introduction to Kinematics

Kinematics is the branch of physics that describes the motion of objects without considering the forces that cause the motion. We use terms like distance, speed, and acceleration to define how things move.

Scalar Quantities

Quantities that have only magnitude (a numerical value with units). They can be added or subtracted using simple arithmetic.

Examples: Distance, speed, mass, time, temperature.

Vector Quantities

Quantities that have both magnitude and direction. They are represented graphically by arrows and require special rules for addition.

Examples: Displacement, velocity, acceleration, force.

Working with Vectors

Vectors are essential for describing motion in more than one dimension. They can be represented and manipulated in several ways.

Vector Representation

  • Graphical: An arrow where the length represents magnitude and the arrowhead points in the direction.
  • Symbolic: A letter with an arrow above it (e.g., →A) or in bold (A).

Vector Operations

  • Addition (Head-to-Tail Rule): To add vectors A and B, draw A first, then draw B starting from the head of A. The resultant vector R goes from the tail of A to the head of B.
  • Resolution of Vectors: A vector can be broken down into perpendicular components, usually along the x and y axes of a Cartesian coordinate system. This is useful for analyzing motion in two dimensions.
  • Vector Products:
    • Scalar (Dot) Product: Results in a scalar quantity. Used to find the work done by a force.
    • Vector (Cross) Product: Results in a new vector that is perpendicular to the original two. Used to find torque.

Describing Motion

We use specific terms to accurately describe how an object's position changes over time.

Concept Description Type
Distance The total path length covered by an object. Scalar
Displacement The change in position of an object in a specific direction (a straight line from start to finish). Its nature is vectorial. Vector
Speed How fast an object is moving (rate of change of distance). Scalar
Velocity The rate of change of displacement (speed in a given direction). Vector
Acceleration The rate of change of velocity. An object accelerates if its speed, direction, or both change. Vector

Instantaneous vs. Average

Average velocity is the total displacement divided by the total time. Instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a specific moment in time. For example, a car's average velocity on a trip might be 60 km/h, but its instantaneous velocity (shown on the speedometer) changes constantly.

Graphical Analysis of Motion

Graphs are a powerful tool for visualizing and analyzing motion.

Displacement-Time Graph

  • The slope (gradient) of the line gives the velocity.
  • A straight line means constant velocity.
  • A curved line means the velocity is changing (acceleration).

Velocity-Time Graph

  • The slope of the line gives the acceleration.
  • The area under the graph gives the displacement.

Equations of Uniformly Accelerated Motion

For an object moving with constant acceleration, we can use three key equations to relate displacement (s), initial velocity (vi), final velocity (vf), acceleration (a), and time (t).

The Three Kinematic Equations

  1. vf = vi + at
  2. s = vit + ½at²
  3. 2as = vf² - vi²

These equations are fundamental for solving problems involving objects speeding up or slowing down at a constant rate, such as an object in free fall.

Projectile Motion

A projectile is any object that is thrown, kicked, or otherwise launched into the air and moves under the influence of gravity alone (assuming no air resistance).

Key Principles of Projectile Motion

  • Independence of Motion: The horizontal and vertical components of the motion are independent of each other.
  • Horizontal Motion: Constant velocity (acceleration is zero).
  • Vertical Motion: Constant downward acceleration due to gravity (g ≈ 9.8 m/s²).

The curved path a projectile follows is called its trajectory, which is parabolic in shape.

Key Formulas for Projectiles

For a projectile launched with initial velocity vo at an angle θ:

Time of Flight (T)

T = \(\frac{2vo sinθ}{g}\)

Maximum Height (H)

H = (vo² sin²θ) / 2g

Range (R)

R = (vo² sin2θ) / g

The maximum range is achieved when the launch angle is 45°.

Chapter Summary

Kinematics provides the tools to describe motion. The key concepts are:

  • Scalars vs. Vectors: Scalars have magnitude only; vectors have both magnitude and direction.
  • Vector Operations: Vectors can be added, resolved into components, and multiplied.
  • Motion Descriptors: Displacement (change in position), velocity (rate of change of displacement), and acceleration (rate of change of velocity).
  • Graphical Analysis: The slope and area of motion graphs provide key information about velocity, acceleration, and displacement.
  • Equations of Motion: Three core equations describe motion under constant acceleration.
  • Projectile Motion: Motion in two dimensions under the influence of gravity, analyzed by separating it into independent horizontal (constant velocity) and vertical (constant acceleration) components.
