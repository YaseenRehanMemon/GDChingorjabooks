Work: More Than Just Effort

In physics, work is done on an object when a force causes it to move a certain distance. If there is no displacement, no work is done, no matter how much force is applied.

Calculating Work

Work is the scalar product of force and displacement.

W = F ⋅ d = Fd cos(θ)

W = Work (in Joules, J)
F = Magnitude of the force
d = Magnitude of the displacement
θ = Angle between the force and displacement vectors

Types of Work

Type Condition Description Example
Positive Work 0° ≤ θ < 90° The force has a component in the direction of motion, adding energy to the object. Pushing a box across the floor.
Zero Work θ = 90° The force is perpendicular to the motion; no energy is transferred. Carrying a heavy bag horizontally.
Negative Work 90° < θ ≤ 180° The force opposes the motion, removing energy from the object. Friction slowing down a sliding puck.

Work can also be calculated as the area under a force-displacement graph.

Energy: The Ability to Do Work

Energy is a fundamental concept in physics, defined as the capacity to do work. It comes in many forms, and this chapter focuses on mechanical energy.

Kinetic Energy (KE)

This is the energy of motion. Any object that is moving has kinetic energy.

KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2

Kinetic energy depends on the mass (m) and the square of the velocity (v) of the object.

Potential Energy (PE)

This is stored energy due to an object's position or configuration. Work done against a conservative force (like gravity) gets stored as potential energy.

PEgravity = mgh

Gravitational potential energy depends on mass (m), gravitational acceleration (g), and height (h) relative to a zero reference level.

Work-Energy Theorem

This crucial theorem states that the net work done on an object is equal to the change in its kinetic energy.

Wnet = ΔKE = KEfinal - KEinitial

Positive net work increases the object's kinetic energy (speeds it up), while negative net work decreases it (slows it down).

The Law of Conservation of Energy

This is one of the most important principles in all of science. It states that energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another.

Conservation of Mechanical Energy

In an isolated system where only conservative forces (like gravity) are acting, the total mechanical energy (the sum of kinetic and potential energy) remains constant.

Total Energy = KE + PE = Constant
KEinitial + PEinitial = KEfinal + PEfinal

For example, as a roller coaster goes down a hill, its potential energy is converted into kinetic energy, but the total energy stays the same (ignoring friction).

Non-Conservative Forces

When non-conservative forces like friction or air resistance are present, mechanical energy is not conserved. Work done by these forces is dissipated, often as heat. In these cases, the total energy of the universe is still conserved, but the mechanical energy of the system decreases.

Escape Velocity

Escape velocity is the minimum speed an object needs to escape the gravitational pull of a planet without any further propulsion. To escape, the object's initial kinetic energy must be equal to its absolute gravitational potential energy.

vescape = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}

Power: The Rate of Doing Work

Power is the measure of how quickly work is done or energy is transferred. Doing the same amount of work in less time requires more power.

Calculating Power

P = W / t = Fv

P = Power (in Watts, W)
W = Work done (in Joules)
t = time taken (in seconds)
F = Force
v = velocity

One Watt is defined as one Joule of work done per second (1 W = 1 \(\frac{J}{s}\)).

Chapter Summary

Work, energy, and power are interconnected concepts that describe how forces transfer energy and change the state of motion of objects.

  • Work (W = Fd cosθ) is the transfer of energy by a force acting over a distance.
  • Kinetic Energy (KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2) is the energy of motion.
  • Potential Energy (PE = mgh) is stored energy due to position.
  • The Work-Energy Theorem states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy (Wnet = ΔKE).
  • The Law of Conservation of Energy states that total energy is always conserved. In the absence of friction, total mechanical energy (KE + PE) is also conserved.
  • Power (P = \(\frac{W}{t}\)) is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred.
