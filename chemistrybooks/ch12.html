Redox Reactions

Electrochemistry is the study of the relationship between chemical reactions and electrical energy. It is centered around oxidation-reduction (redox) reactions, where electrons are transferred from one substance to another.

Key Definitions (OIL RIG)

  • Oxidation Is Loss of electrons. The substance that is oxidized is called the reducing agent.
  • Reduction Is Gain of electrons. The substance that is reduced is called the oxidizing agent.

Electrochemical Cells

Electrochemical cells are devices that either generate electrical energy from a spontaneous chemical reaction or use electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous reaction.

Anodes and Cathodes

Regardless of the cell type, the definitions of the electrodes remain the same:

  • The Anode is the electrode where Oxidation occurs (An Ox).
  • The Cathode is the electrode where Reduction occurs (Red Cat).

Batteries

A battery is a portable, self-contained electrochemical power source, typically consisting of one or more galvanic cells connected in series.

Primary vs. Secondary Batteries

  • Primary Batteries (e.g., a dry cell) are single-use. The redox reaction cannot be easily reversed, so they are discarded when depleted.
  • Secondary Batteries (e.g., a lead-acid car battery) are rechargeable. The reaction can be reversed by applying an external electric current, restoring the reactants.

Fuel Cells

A fuel cell is a special type of galvanic cell where the reactants (like hydrogen and oxygen) are continuously supplied from an external source to produce electricity. They don't store energy; they convert it as long as fuel is available.

Corrosion

Corrosion is the gradual destruction of a metal through an unwanted electrochemical (redox) process. The most common example is the rusting of iron.

The Electrochemistry of Rusting

In the presence of water and oxygen, iron acts as the anode (it is oxidized) while another site on the iron acts as the cathode (where oxygen is reduced). This creates a tiny galvanic cell that leads to the formation of iron(III) oxide, or rust.

Preventing Corrosion

Corrosion can be prevented by several methods:

  • Protective Coatings: Painting or greasing the metal to prevent contact with air and moisture.
  • Electroplating: Coating the metal with a thin layer of a different, more resistant metal like chromium or tin.
  • Galvanizing: Coating iron with zinc. Since zinc is more easily oxidized than iron, it corrodes first, acting as a "sacrificial" anode.

Chapter Summary

  • Electrochemistry links chemical reactions to electrical energy through redox reactions (transfer of electrons).
  • Oxidation is the loss of electrons; Reduction is the gain of electrons (OIL RIG).
  • Galvanic Cells (batteries) use spontaneous redox reactions to produce electricity.
  • Electrolytic Cells use electricity to force non-spontaneous reactions to occur (e.g., recharging, electroplating).
  • Batteries can be single-use (primary) or rechargeable (secondary). Fuel cells generate power by continuously consuming fuel.
  • Corrosion (like rusting) is an undesirable electrochemical process that can be prevented with coatings or by using a sacrificial anode (galvanizing).
