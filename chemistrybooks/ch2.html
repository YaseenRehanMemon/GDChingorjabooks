What is an Atom?

Everything in the universe is made of tiny building blocks called atoms. For centuries, they were thought to be indivisible. However, we now know that atoms have an internal structure, composed of even smaller subatomic particles.

Understanding this structure is key to understanding chemistry. The arrangement of these particles defines an element's identity and its chemical behavior.

The Three Key Subatomic Particles

An atom consists of a tiny, dense nucleus at its center, containing protons and neutrons. Electrons orbit this nucleus in specific regions.

Properties of Subatomic Particles

Particle Charge Location Relative Mass (amu)
Proton (p⁺) Positive (+1) Nucleus ~1
Neutron (n⁰) Neutral (0) Nucleus ~1
Electron (e⁻) Negative (-1) Outside Nucleus ~1/1836 (negligible)

Bohr's Atomic Model

Niels Bohr developed a revolutionary model of the atom in 1913, primarily for hydrogen. While it has been replaced by a more accurate model, it introduced critical concepts about electron energy.

Key Postulates of Bohr's Model

  • Fixed Orbits: Electrons revolve around the nucleus in fixed, circular paths called orbits or energy levels.
  • Quantized Energy: Each orbit has a specific, constant energy. An electron in a particular orbit does not radiate energy.
  • Energy Jumps: An electron can jump to a higher energy level by absorbing a specific amount of energy (a photon). It can fall back to a lower level by emitting a photon of light with a specific energy.

Atomic Spectra Explained

Bohr's model explained why elements emit light only at specific frequencies when heated, creating a line spectrum. Each line corresponds to an electron falling from a higher to a lower, specific energy level.

The Quantum Mechanical Model

The modern view of the atom is more probabilistic. We can't know the exact path of an electron; we can only describe the region where it is most likely to be found.

From Orbits to Orbitals

An orbital is a three-dimensional region around the nucleus that indicates the probable location of an electron. Unlike a 2D orbit, an orbital is a "cloud of probability" with a specific shape and energy.

Quantum Numbers: The Electron's Address

Four quantum numbers are used to describe the state of an electron in an atom completely.

Number Symbol What It Describes
Principal n Main energy level (shell). Larger n means higher energy. (n = 1, 2, 3...)
Azimuthal l Shape of the orbital (subshell). (l = 0 is 's', 1 is 'p', 2 is 'd')
Magnetic m Orientation of the orbital in space (e.g., pₓ, pᵧ, p₂)
Spin s Electron's intrinsic spin (either +½ or -½).

Electronic Configuration

The distribution of electrons among the various atomic orbitals is called its electronic configuration. Filling these orbitals follows a set of rules.

Rules for Electron Filling

  1. Aufbau Principle: Electrons enter the lowest energy orbitals available first. The general order is 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, etc.
  2. Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons in an atom can have the same four quantum numbers. This means an orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons, and they must have opposite spins.
  3. Hund's Rule: When filling orbitals of equal energy (like the three p orbitals), electrons will occupy each orbital singly before any orbital is doubly occupied.

Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of particles or energy from an unstable atomic nucleus. This process, called radioactive decay, transforms the nucleus into a more stable state.

Types of Nuclear Radiation

The three main types of radiation are:

  • Alpha (α) particles: Positively charged particles (helium nuclei) with low penetrating power.
  • Beta (β) particles: Negatively charged particles (electrons) with medium penetrating power.
  • Gamma (γ) rays: High-energy electromagnetic waves with no charge and very high penetrating power.

Uses in Daily Life

Controlled radiation is used in medicine for cancer therapy and medical imaging, and in agriculture to improve crop varieties and preserve food.

Chapter Summary

This chapter outlined the fundamental structure of the atom. We explored the subatomic particles, the evolution from Bohr's simple model to the complex Quantum Mechanical model, and the rules governing how electrons are organized within an atom. Key concepts include:

  • Subatomic Particles: Atoms are made of protons (+), neutrons (0), and electrons (-). Protons and neutrons reside in the nucleus.
  • Bohr's Model: Introduced the concept of quantized energy levels (orbits) for electrons.
  • Quantum Model: Describes electrons in terms of orbitals (probability clouds) defined by four quantum numbers.
  • Electronic Configuration: The arrangement of electrons, determined by the Aufbau Principle, Pauli Exclusion Principle, and Hund's Rule.
  • Radioactivity: The emission of radiation from unstable nuclei, with important applications in medicine and industry.
