Introduction to Acids and Bases

Acids and bases are fundamental categories of chemical substances found everywhere, from our kitchens (vinegar is an acid, baking soda is a base) to complex industrial processes. They are defined by their distinct chemical properties and their ability to neutralize each other to form salts.

The Brønsted-Lowry Theory

The Brønsted-Lowry theory provides a broad and useful definition for acids and bases based on the transfer of protons (H⁺ ions).

Key Definitions

  • Brønsted-Lowry Acid: A substance that can donate a proton (H⁺).
  • Brønsted-Lowry Base: A substance that can accept a proton (H⁺).

Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs

In any acid-base reaction, there are two "conjugate pairs." When an acid donates its proton, what remains is called its conjugate base. When a base accepts a proton, what is formed is its conjugate acid.

HCl (acid) + H₂O (base) ⇌ Cl⁻ (conjugate base) + H₃O⁺ (conjugate acid)

The Lewis Theory

The Lewis theory offers the most general definition of acids and bases, focusing on electron pairs instead of protons.

Key Definitions

  • Lewis Acid: A substance that can accept a pair of electrons.
  • Lewis Base: A substance that can donate a pair of electrons.

This definition is very broad and includes many reactions that don't involve proton transfer.

The pH Scale

The pH scale is a convenient way to express the acidity or basicity of an aqueous solution. It is a logarithmic scale ranging from 0 to 14.

Understanding pH

  • pH < 7: The solution is acidic.
  • pH = 7: The solution is neutral.
  • pH > 7: The solution is basic (or alkaline).

Because the scale is logarithmic, a change of one pH unit represents a tenfold change in H⁺ ion concentration.

Formulas for pH and pOH

pH = -log[H⁺]
pOH = -log[OH⁻]

In any aqueous solution: pH + pOH = 14

Buffer Solutions

A buffer is a special solution that can resist significant changes in pH when an acid or a base is added. They act like "chemical sponges" for H⁺ and OH⁻ ions.

How Buffers Work

A buffer is made by mixing a weak acid with its conjugate base (e.g., acetic acid and sodium acetate) or a weak base with its conjugate acid (e.g., ammonia and ammonium chloride).

  • If you add acid (H⁺), the weak base component of the buffer neutralizes it.
  • If you add base (OH⁻), the weak acid component neutralizes it.

Buffers are crucial in biological systems; for example, our blood is buffered to maintain a constant pH of about 7.4.

Salts and Hydrolysis

A salt is an ionic compound formed from the neutralization reaction between an acid and a base. When dissolved in water, some salts can react with the water in a process called hydrolysis, causing the solution to become acidic or basic.

Salt Formed From... Hydrolysis Behavior Resulting pH
Strong Acid + Strong Base No hydrolysis occurs. Neutral (pH = 7)
Strong Acid + Weak Base The cation from the weak base hydrolyzes to produce H⁺. Acidic (pH < 7)
Weak Acid + Strong Base The anion from the weak acid hydrolyzes to produce OH⁻. Basic (pH > 7)

Chapter Summary

  • Acid-Base Theories: Acids and bases can be defined in several ways. The Brønsted-Lowry definition (proton donor/acceptor) and the Lewis definition (electron-pair acceptor/donor) are the most common.
  • Conjugate Pairs: When a Brønsted-Lowry acid loses a proton, it forms its conjugate base. When a base gains a proton, it forms its conjugate acid.
  • pH Scale: A logarithmic measure of acidity, where pH < 7 is acidic, pH > 7 is basic, and pH = 7 is neutral.
  • Buffers: Solutions of a weak acid/base and its conjugate that resist changes in pH.
  • Salts: Ionic compounds formed from acid-base neutralization. They can undergo hydrolysis in water to create acidic or basic solutions.
