Solutions, Colloids, and Suspensions

Mixtures are combinations of two or more substances that are not chemically bonded. They can be classified based on the size of the solute particles.

Solubility

Solubility describes the maximum amount of a solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature. The fundamental principle of solubility is summarized by the phrase "like dissolves like."

"Like Dissolves Like"

  • Polar solvents (like water) dissolve polar solutes (like sugar) and ionic solutes (like salt). These are called hydrophilic (water-loving) substances.
  • Nonpolar solvents (like oil or hexane) dissolve nonpolar solutes (like grease). These are called hydrophobic (water-fearing) substances.

Expressing Concentration

Concentration is a measure of the amount of solute present in a given amount of solution or solvent. There are several ways to express it.

Common Concentration Units

  • Molarity (M): Moles of solute per liter of solution.
  • Molality (m): Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. (Useful because it doesn't change with temperature).
  • Mole Fraction (X): The ratio of the moles of one component to the total moles of all components in the solution.
  • Mass Percent (%): (Mass of solute / Mass of solution) Ã— 100%.

Colligative Properties

Colligative properties are physical properties of solutions that depend on the number of dissolved solute particles, not on their chemical identity.

The Four Colligative Properties

  1. Vapor Pressure Lowering: The vapor pressure of a solution is always lower than that of the pure solvent.
  2. Boiling Point Elevation: A solution boils at a higher temperature than the pure solvent.
  3. Freezing Point Depression: A solution freezes at a lower temperature than the pure solvent (this is why salt is put on icy roads).
  4. Osmotic Pressure: The pressure required to prevent the inward flow of solvent across a semipermeable membrane (osmosis).

Raoult's Law mathematically describes vapor pressure lowering, stating that the vapor pressure of a solution is directly proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent.

Colloids

Colloids are mixtures where particles are dispersed throughout a substance but are not dissolved. They have unique properties that distinguish them from true solutions.

The Tyndall Effect

One of the key properties of colloids is the Tyndall effect: the scattering of a beam of light as it passes through. This is why you can see the beams of headlights in fog (a colloid) but not in clear air (a solution).

Chapter Summary

  • Mixtures are classified as solutions, colloids, or suspensions based on particle size.
  • Solubility is governed by the "like dissolves like" principle: polar dissolves polar, nonpolar dissolves nonpolar.
  • Concentration can be expressed in various units, including Molarity (M), Molality (m), and Mass Percent (%).
  • Colligative Properties (vapor pressure lowering, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, osmotic pressure) depend on the concentration of solute particles, not their identity.
  • Colloids are stable mixtures of medium-sized particles that exhibit the Tyndall effect (light scattering).
