Introduction to Chemical Bonding

Why do atoms join together to form molecules? The simple answer is to become more stable. A chemical bond is the attractive force that holds atoms together in a compound. By forming bonds, atoms can achieve a more stable, lower-energy state, often by completing their outer shell of electrons (the octet rule).

This chapter focuses on covalent bonds, where atoms achieve stability by sharing electrons.

Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

Valence Bond Theory explains covalent bond formation as the overlap of atomic orbitals. When two half-filled orbitals from different atoms overlap, the electrons are shared, and a bond is formed. The greater the overlap, the stronger the bond.

Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds

There are two primary ways orbitals can overlap:

Bond Type Description Key Features
Sigma (σ) Bond Formed by the direct, "head-on" overlap of orbitals along the axis connecting the two nuclei. Strongest type of covalent bond. All single bonds are sigma bonds.
Pi (π) Bond Formed by the "sideways" overlap of parallel p-orbitals, above and below the axis of the nuclei. Weaker than sigma bonds. Present in double (1σ, 1π) and triple (1σ, 2π) bonds.

VSEPR Theory & Molecular Shapes

The shape of a molecule is determined by the arrangement of electron pairs around the central atom. The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory provides a simple model to predict these shapes.

The Core Idea of VSEPR

Electron pairs in the valence shell of a central atom repel each other. They will arrange themselves in 3D space to be as far apart as possible, which minimizes repulsion and determines the molecule's geometry.

Important: Both bonding pairs (in covalent bonds) and lone pairs (non-bonding pairs) contribute to this repulsion.

Common Molecular Shapes

Electron Pairs Arrangement Shape Name Bond Angle Example
2 Bonding, 0 Lone Electron pairs are 180° apart Linear 180° CO₂
3 Bonding, 0 Lone Pairs form a flat triangle Trigonal Planar 120° BF₃
4 Bonding, 0 Lone Pairs point to corners of a tetrahedron Tetrahedral 109.5° CH₄
3 Bonding, 1 Lone Based on tetrahedral, but lone pair repels more Trigonal Pyramidal ~107° NH₃
2 Bonding, 2 Lone Based on tetrahedral, with two strong lone pair repulsions Bent (or Angular) ~104.5° H₂O

Hybridization of Orbitals

Hybridization is the concept of mixing atomic orbitals to form a new set of identical hybrid orbitals. This helps explain how atoms like carbon can form four identical single bonds (as in methane) when its valence electrons are in different types of orbitals (s and p).

sp³ Hybridization

One 's' orbital mixes with three 'p' orbitals to form four identical sp³ hybrid orbitals.

  • Arrangement: Tetrahedral
  • Bond Angle: 109.5°
  • Example: Methane (CH₄), Ammonia (NH₃), Water (H₂O)

sp² Hybridization

One 's' orbital mixes with two 'p' orbitals to form three identical sp² hybrid orbitals (one 'p' orbital remains unhybridized to form a π bond).

  • Arrangement: Trigonal Planar
  • Bond Angle: 120°
  • Example: Ethene (C₂H₄), Boron Trifluoride (BF₃)

sp Hybridization

One 's' orbital mixes with one 'p' orbital to form two identical sp hybrid orbitals (two 'p' orbitals remain unhybridized for π bonds).

  • Arrangement: Linear
  • Bond Angle: 180°
  • Example: Ethyne (C₂H₂), Beryllium Chloride (BeCl₂)

Bond Properties

Several properties help us describe and compare covalent bonds.

Bond Energy & Bond Length

  • Bond Energy: The energy required to break one mole of a particular bond. Stronger bonds have higher bond energies.
  • Bond Length: The average distance between the nuclei of two bonded atoms. Stronger bonds are shorter.

General Trend: Triple Bonds > Double Bonds > Single Bonds (in terms of energy and shortness).

Bond Polarity and Dipole Moment

When two different atoms share electrons, the more electronegative atom pulls the electrons closer, creating a slight negative charge (δ-) on that atom and a slight positive charge (δ+) on the other. This separation of charge is called a polar covalent bond or a dipole.

While individual bonds can be polar, the overall molecule might be nonpolar if its shape is symmetrical, causing the dipoles to cancel out (e.g., CO₂ is nonpolar, but H₂O is polar).

Chapter Summary

This chapter explored how atoms share electrons to form stable covalent bonds and how the repulsion between these electron pairs dictates the 3D shape of molecules.

  • Valence Bond Theory: Covalent bonds form from the overlap of atomic orbitals. Head-on overlap creates strong sigma (σ) bonds, while sideways overlap creates weaker pi (π) bonds.
  • VSEPR Theory: Predicts molecular geometry based on the principle that electron pairs (bonding and lone) repel each other and spread out as much as possible.
  • Hybridization: The mixing of atomic orbitals (s, p, d) to form new, identical hybrid orbitals (sp³, sp², sp) that explain observed bond angles and shapes.
  • Bond Properties: Bond strength is measured by bond energy, and bond distance is measured by bond length. Unequal electron sharing due to electronegativity differences leads to polar bonds.
