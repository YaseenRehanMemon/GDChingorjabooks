Energy in Chemical Reactions

Thermochemistry is the study of heat changes that accompany chemical reactions and phase transitions. Every chemical reaction involves an energy change, as energy is required to break bonds in reactants and released when new bonds form in products.

System, Surroundings, and Universe

  • System: The part of the universe we are studying (e.g., the chemicals in a beaker).
  • Surroundings: Everything else in the universe outside the system.
  • Universe = System + Surroundings.

The First Law of Thermodynamics

This is essentially the Law of Conservation of Energy applied to thermodynamic systems. It states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed.

ΔE = q + w

ΔE = Change in the system's internal energy
q = Heat added to the system
w = Work done on the system

Enthalpy (H)

Enthalpy (H) is a measure of the total heat content of a system at constant pressure. We are usually interested in the change in enthalpy (ΔH), which is the heat absorbed or released during a reaction.

Exothermic vs. Endothermic Reactions

ProcessDescriptionSign of ΔHExample
ExothermicThe system releases heat into the surroundings (feels hot).Negative (-ΔH)Combustion of fuel
EndothermicThe system absorbs heat from the surroundings (feels cold).Positive (+ΔH)Melting ice, photosynthesis

Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH°f)

The standard enthalpy of formation is the enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed from its elements in their standard states (usually at 25°C and 1 atm).

Hess's Law

Enthalpy is a state function, meaning the change in enthalpy depends only on the initial and final states, not on the path taken between them.

Hess's Law of Heat Summation

If a reaction is carried out in a series of steps, the ΔH for the overall reaction is equal to the sum of the enthalpy changes for the individual steps.

This law is incredibly useful because it allows us to calculate the enthalpy change for a reaction that is difficult to measure directly by using data from other, easier-to-measure reactions.

The Born-Haber Cycle

The Born-Haber cycle is a specific application of Hess's Law used to calculate the lattice energy of an ionic compound, which cannot be measured directly.

Chapter Summary

  • Thermochemistry is the study of heat changes in chemical reactions.
  • The First Law of Thermodynamics (ΔE = q + w) states that energy is conserved.
  • Enthalpy (ΔH) is the heat absorbed or released in a reaction at constant pressure. Exothermic reactions release heat (-ΔH), while Endothermic reactions absorb heat (+ΔH).
  • Hess's Law states that the total enthalpy change for a reaction is independent of the pathway taken, allowing us to calculate unknown ΔH values by combining known ones.
  • The Standard Enthalpy of Formation (ΔH°f) is a key value used in these calculations.
