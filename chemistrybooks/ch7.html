Reversible Reactions and Dynamic Equilibrium

Most chemical reactions don't just go in one direction. In a reversible reaction, products can react to re-form the original reactants. This is represented by a double arrow (⇌).

A system reaches dynamic equilibrium when the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. At this point, the concentrations of reactants and products stop changing, but the reactions haven't stopped—they are just balanced, like two people walking on an escalator at the same speed it's moving down.

The Equilibrium Constant (Kc)

The Law of Mass Action gives us a way to quantify the equilibrium state. For any reversible reaction, the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations (each raised to the power of its stoichiometric coefficient) is a constant value at a given temperature.

For a reaction: aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ

Where [ ] denotes concentration in mol/L.

What does the value of Kc tell us?

  • Large Kc (> 1): The reaction favors the products. At equilibrium, there will be more products than reactants.
  • Small Kc (< 1): The reaction favors the reactants. At equilibrium, there will be more reactants than products.
  • Kc ≈ 1: There are significant amounts of both reactants and products at equilibrium.

Le Chatelier's Principle: Shifting the Balance

Le Chatelier's Principle is a fundamental concept that helps us predict how a system at equilibrium will respond to a change.

Le Chatelier's Principle

When a stress is applied to a system at equilibrium, the system will shift in a direction that relieves the stress.

Stress Applied How the System Responds (Shifts)
Adding a Reactant Shifts to the right (towards products) to consume the added reactant.
Removing a Product Shifts to the right (towards products) to replace the removed product.
Increasing Pressure (for gases) Shifts to the side with fewer moles of gas to reduce pressure.
Increasing Temperature Shifts in the endothermic direction (the one that absorbs heat) to cool down.

Application: The Haber Process

The industrial production of ammonia (N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃) is a classic example. To maximize the yield of NH₃, the process is run at very high pressure (shifting equilibrium to the right, which has fewer moles of gas) and moderate temperatures (a compromise, since a low temperature favors the product but makes the reaction too slow).

The Solubility Product Constant (Ksp)

Even "insoluble" ionic compounds dissolve to a tiny extent in water, creating an equilibrium between the solid and its dissolved ions.

The Ksp Expression

The solubility product constant (Ksp) is the equilibrium constant for the dissolution of a sparingly soluble salt. It represents the product of the ion concentrations in a saturated solution.

For a salt like AgCl: AgCl(s) ⇌ Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

Ksp = [Ag⁺][Cl⁻]

A smaller Ksp value means the compound is less soluble.

The Common Ion Effect

The solubility of a sparingly soluble salt is decreased when a common ion (an ion that is already in the equilibrium) is added to the solution. This is an application of Le Chatelier's Principle, as adding a product ion shifts the equilibrium to the left, favoring the solid reactant.

Chapter Summary

  • Dynamic Equilibrium: A state in reversible reactions where the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, and net concentrations are constant.
  • Equilibrium Constant (Kc): A ratio of product to reactant concentrations at equilibrium. Its value indicates whether products or reactants are favored.
  • Le Chatelier's Principle: An equilibrium system will shift to counteract any applied stress (changes in concentration, pressure, or temperature).
  • Solubility Product (Ksp): The equilibrium constant for the dissolving of a sparingly soluble ionic compound. The Common Ion Effect describes how adding a common ion reduces the solubility of such a salt.
