Reaction Rates

Chemical kinetics is the study of the speed, or rate, at which chemical reactions occur. Some reactions, like an explosion, are incredibly fast, while others, like the rusting of iron, are very slow. Kinetics helps us understand and control these speeds.

Defining Reaction Rate

The rate of a reaction is measured as the change in the concentration of a reactant or product over a period of time.

Rate = Δ[Concentration] / ΔTime

The Rate Law

The rate law is an equation that mathematically links the reaction rate to the concentrations of the reactants. The exponents in the rate law define the order of the reaction with respect to each reactant and must be determined experimentally.

Collision Theory and Activation Energy

For a reaction to occur, reactant particles must collide. However, not every collision results in a reaction. Collision theory states that for a collision to be effective, two conditions must be met:

Conditions for a Successful Reaction

  1. Sufficient Energy: The colliding particles must have enough kinetic energy to overcome a minimum energy barrier, known as the activation energy (Eₐ).
  2. Correct Orientation: The particles must collide in the correct spatial orientation to allow bonds to break and new bonds to form.

Activation Energy (Eₐ)

Think of activation energy as an "energy hill" that reactants must climb before they can become products. The higher the hill, the more energy is required for a reaction, and the slower the reaction will be.

Factors Affecting Reaction Rate

We can control the speed of a reaction by manipulating several factors that influence the frequency and effectiveness of collisions.

Factor Effect on Rate Why it Works (Based on Collision Theory)
Concentration Increases rate More particles in the same volume leads to more frequent collisions.
Temperature Increases rate Particles move faster, leading to more frequent and more energetic collisions. More particles will have enough energy to overcome the activation energy.
Surface Area (of solids) Increases rate Grinding a solid into a powder increases the area available for collision.
Catalyst Increases rate Provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy.

Catalysis

A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without being consumed in the process. It works by providing a different, "easier" reaction mechanism with a lower activation energy.

Catalysts vs. Reactants

A catalyst participates in the reaction but is regenerated at the end, so it doesn't appear as a product or reactant in the overall balanced equation. It simply makes the journey from reactant to product faster and easier.

Enzymes: Biological Catalysts

Enzymes are highly specialized proteins that act as catalysts in biological systems. They are incredibly efficient and specific, enabling the complex chemical reactions necessary for life to occur at body temperature.

Chapter Summary

  • Chemical Kinetics is the study of reaction rates and the factors that influence them.
  • Collision Theory states that for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (Activation Energy) and the correct orientation.
  • Reaction rates can be increased by:
    • Increasing concentration of reactants.
    • Increasing temperature.
    • Increasing the surface area of solid reactants.
    • Adding a catalyst.
  • A catalyst provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy, speeding up the reaction without being consumed. Enzymes are biological catalysts.
