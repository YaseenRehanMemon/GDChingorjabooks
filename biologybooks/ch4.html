4.1 Introduction to Bioenergetics

Bioenergetics is the study of how energy is captured, converted, and used in biological systems. It follows the laws of thermodynamics, focusing on two key processes: photosynthesis and cellular respiration.

ATP: The Energy Currency of the Cell

Adenosine Triphosphate (ATP) is the primary energy-carrying molecule used to power most cellular activities. Energy is stored in its high-energy phosphate bonds and released when ATP is hydrolyzed to ADP (Adenosine Diphosphate).

4.2 Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which autotrophs (like plants and algae) convert light energy into chemical energy, stored in the bonds of glucose molecules. It occurs in the chloroplasts.

Overall Equation for Photosynthesis:

\[ 6\text{CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow{\text{Light Energy}} \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 + 6\text{O}_2 \]

Stages of Photosynthesis

  1. Light-Dependent Reactions: Occur in the thylakoid membranes of chloroplasts. Light energy is captured by pigments (like chlorophyll), water is split to release oxygen, and the energy is used to produce ATP and NADPH.
  2. Light-Independent Reactions (Calvin Cycle): Occur in the stroma of chloroplasts. The energy from ATP and NADPH is used to "fix" carbon from CO‚ÇÇ into organic molecules, ultimately producing glucose.

4.3 Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose and other organic molecules to produce ATP, releasing energy for cellular work. It can be aerobic (with oxygen) or anaerobic (without oxygen).

4.3.1 Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration is a highly efficient process that occurs in the presence of oxygen, primarily within the mitochondria.

Overall Equation for Aerobic Respiration:

\[ \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 + 6\text{O}_2 \to 6\text{CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{ATP (Energy)} \]

Stages of Aerobic Respiration

  1. Glycolysis: Occurs in the cytoplasm. Glucose (a 6-carbon sugar) is broken down into two molecules of pyruvate (a 3-carbon molecule). This yields a small amount of ATP and NADH.
  2. Pyruvate Oxidation: Pyruvate enters the mitochondrion and is converted into Acetyl-CoA, releasing CO‚ÇÇ and producing NADH.
  3. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle): Occurs in the mitochondrial matrix. Acetyl-CoA is completely oxidized, producing ATP, NADH, FADH‚ÇÇ, and CO‚ÇÇ.
  4. Oxidative Phosphorylation (Electron Transport Chain): Occurs on the inner mitochondrial membrane. The energy from NADH and FADH‚ÇÇ is used to create a proton gradient, which drives the synthesis of a large amount of ATP through a process called chemiosmosis. Oxygen acts as the final electron acceptor, forming water.

4.3.2 Anaerobic Respiration (Fermentation)

When oxygen is not available, cells can produce a small amount of ATP through glycolysis followed by fermentation. The main purpose of fermentation is to regenerate NAD‚Å∫ so that glycolysis can continue.

  • Lactic Acid Fermentation: Pyruvate is converted to lactic acid (e.g., in human muscle cells during strenuous exercise).
  • Alcoholic Fermentation: Pyruvate is converted to ethanol and CO‚ÇÇ (e.g., in yeast).

4.4 Photorespiration

Photorespiration is a wasteful process that occurs in C‚ÇÉ plants when the enzyme RuBisCO binds to O‚ÇÇ instead of CO‚ÇÇ. This happens in hot, dry conditions when stomata close to conserve water, leading to high O‚ÇÇ and low CO‚ÇÇ levels inside the leaf. It consumes ATP and releases CO‚ÇÇ, reducing the efficiency of photosynthesis. C‚ÇÑ and CAM plants have evolved alternative mechanisms to minimize photorespiration.

Chapter Summary

  • Bioenergetics is the study of energy flow in living systems, with ATP as the main energy currency.
  • Photosynthesis uses light energy, water, and CO‚ÇÇ to create glucose and oxygen. It consists of light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle.
  • Cellular Respiration breaks down glucose to produce ATP.
    • Aerobic respiration (with oxygen) involves Glycolysis, Pyruvate Oxidation, the Krebs Cycle, and Oxidative Phosphorylation, yielding a large amount of ATP.
    • Anaerobic respiration (without oxygen) involves Glycolysis followed by Fermentation, yielding a small amount of ATP.
  • Photorespiration is a wasteful process in C‚ÇÉ plants where RuBisCO fixes O‚ÇÇ instead of CO‚ÇÇ, reducing photosynthetic efficiency.
