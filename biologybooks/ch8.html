8.1 Evolutionary Relations in Plants

Plants are multicellular, eukaryotic, photosynthetic autotrophs with cell walls made of cellulose. The evolutionary history of plants is a story of adaptation from aquatic habitats to a terrestrial life, which presented challenges like water conservation and structural support.

Alternation of Generations

All plants exhibit a life cycle called alternation of generations, where a multicellular haploid (n) stage, the gametophyte, alternates with a multicellular diploid (2n) stage, the sporophyte.

  • The gametophyte produces gametes (sperm and egg) by mitosis.
  • The sporophyte produces spores by meiosis.

8.2 Bryophytes (Non-Vascular Plants)

Bryophytes are the simplest land plants and represent an early stage of plant evolution. They lack a specialized vascular system (xylem and phloem) for transporting water and nutrients, which limits their size and keeps them tied to moist environments.

Key Characteristics of Bryophytes

  • Dominant Generation: The gametophyte is the dominant, photosynthetic, and free-living stage of the life cycle.
  • Dependence on Water: Require water for sexual reproduction, as the sperm must swim to reach the egg.
  • Examples: Mosses, Liverworts, and Hornworts.

8.3 Seedless Vascular Plants (Pteridophytes)

Seedless vascular plants were the first to evolve vascular tissues (xylem and phloem), allowing them to grow taller and live in drier habitats than bryophytes. However, they still reproduce using spores and require water for fertilization.

Key Characteristics of Pteridophytes

  • Dominant Generation: The sporophyte is the dominant, larger generation.
  • Vascular Tissue: Possess xylem for water transport and phloem for nutrient transport, providing structural support.
  • Reproduction: Reproduce via spores, not seeds.
  • Examples: Ferns, Club Mosses, and Horsetails.

Evolution of the Leaf

Vascular plants evolved two main types of leaves:

  • Microphylls: Small, single-veined leaves (e.g., in club mosses).
  • Megaphylls: Larger leaves with a complex, branching vascular system (e.g., in ferns and seed plants).

8.4 Seed Plants (Spermatophytes)

The evolution of the seed was a major adaptation that allowed plants to fully conquer terrestrial environments. A seed consists of an embryo, a food supply, and a protective coat.

Seed plants are divided into two major groups: Gymnosperms and Angiosperms.

Gymnosperms

Gymnosperms are plants with "naked seeds," meaning their seeds are not enclosed within a fruit. They are typically cone-bearing plants.

  • Examples: Conifers (pines, firs, spruces), Cycads, Ginkgo.

Angiosperms (Flowering Plants)

Angiosperms are the most diverse and successful group of plants. Their key adaptations are the flower (for efficient reproduction) and the fruit (for seed protection and dispersal).

Key Features of Angiosperms

  • Flowers: Specialized reproductive structures that attract pollinators.
  • Double Fertilization: A unique process where one sperm fertilizes the egg to form the zygote, and a second sperm fuses with two other nuclei to form the endosperm (food supply).
  • Fruits: Mature ovaries that enclose and protect the seeds.

Chapter Summary

  • Plant evolution shows a trend of increasing adaptation to land, including the development of vascular tissue, seeds, flowers, and fruits.
  • Bryophytes are non-vascular plants with a dominant gametophyte generation, requiring water for reproduction.
  • Seedless Vascular Plants (Pteridophytes), like ferns, have a dominant sporophyte and vascular tissue but still reproduce via spores.
  • Seed Plants are the most successful land plants.
    • Gymnosperms have "naked seeds," typically in cones.
    • Angiosperms have flowers and fruits, and are divided into Monocots and Dicots.
  • A key evolutionary trend is the reduction of the gametophyte generation and the increasing dominance of the sporophyte.
