1.1 Introduction to Biological Molecules

All living organisms are composed of chemicals. The study of the chemical composition and reactions within living cells is called biochemistry. The large, complex organic molecules that form the structure and carry out the functions of life are called biomolecules or macromolecules.

Monomers and Polymers

Most macromolecules are polymers, which are long chains made up of repeating smaller units called monomers.

  • Condensation (Dehydration Synthesis): The process of joining monomers together by removing a water molecule.
  • Hydrolysis: The process of breaking polymers apart by adding a water molecule.

The Importance of Water

Water is the most abundant component in any living cell (70-90%). Its unique properties, due to its polarity and ability to form hydrogen bonds, make it the "medium of life":

  • Universal Solvent: Dissolves polar and ionic substances.
  • High Specific Heat: Stabilizes temperature.
  • Cohesion and Adhesion: Allows for transport in plants.

1.2 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the primary source of energy for living organisms. They are made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, typically in a 1:2:1 ratio. Their monomers are called monosaccharides.

TypeDescriptionExamples
MonosaccharidesSimple sugars, the basic building blocks.Glucose, Fructose, Galactose
DisaccharidesTwo monosaccharides joined by a glycosidic linkage.Sucrose (table sugar), Lactose (milk sugar), Maltose
PolysaccharidesLong chains (polymers) of monosaccharides. Used for energy storage and structure.Starch (plant energy storage), Glycogen (animal energy storage), Cellulose (plant cell wall structure)

1.3 Proteins

Proteins are the most diverse and complex macromolecules, performing a vast array of functions in the cell. They are polymers of amino acids.

Amino Acids and Peptide Bonds

There are 20 common amino acids that are the building blocks of all proteins. They are joined together by peptide bonds to form a long chain called a polypeptide. The specific sequence of amino acids determines the protein's unique 3D structure and function.

Levels of Protein Structure

  1. Primary Structure: The unique linear sequence of amino acids.
  2. Secondary Structure: Local folding of the polypeptide chain into coils (alpha-helices) or sheets (beta-pleated sheets), held together by hydrogen bonds.
  3. Tertiary Structure: The overall 3D shape of a single polypeptide, resulting from interactions between the R-groups of the amino acids.
  4. Quaternary Structure: The arrangement of multiple polypeptide chains to form a single functional protein (e.g., hemoglobin).

1.4 Lipids

Lipids are a diverse group of hydrophobic molecules that are insoluble in water. They are crucial for long-term energy storage, insulation, and forming cell membranes.

TypeDescriptionFunction
Fats & Oils (Triglycerides)Composed of glycerol and three fatty acids. Fats are solid at room temp (saturated), while oils are liquid (unsaturated).Long-term energy storage, insulation.
PhospholipidsGlycerol, two fatty acids, and a phosphate group. They have a hydrophilic head and a hydrophobic tail.Primary component of cell membranes.
WaxesLong-chain fatty acids linked to long-chain alcohols.Waterproofing, protective coatings.
SteroidsCharacterized by a four-ring structure.Hormones (e.g., testosterone, estrogen), cholesterol (component of cell membranes).

1.5 Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are macromolecules that store and transmit genetic information. They are polymers of nucleotides.

Components of a Nucleotide

Each nucleotide consists of three parts:

  1. A 5-carbon sugar (deoxyribose in DNA, ribose in RNA).
  2. A phosphate group.
  3. A nitrogenous base (Adenine, Guanine, Cytosine, Thymine in DNA; Uracil replaces Thymine in RNA).

DNA vs. RNA

  • DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid): A double-stranded helix that stores the genetic blueprint for an organism.
  • RNA (Ribonucleic Acid): A single-stranded molecule involved in translating the genetic information from DNA into proteins. The three main types are mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA.

Chapter Summary

  • Living organisms are composed of biomolecules, which are primarily based on carbon.
  • Carbohydrates (sugars, starches, cellulose) provide quick energy and structural support.
  • Proteins (polymers of amino acids) have complex structures and perform most of the work in cells.
  • Lipids (fats, oils, waxes, steroids) are hydrophobic and are used for energy storage, insulation, and membranes.
  • Nucleic Acids (DNA and RNA, polymers of nucleotides) store and transmit genetic information.
