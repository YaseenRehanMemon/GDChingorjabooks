2.1 Introduction to Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up the rate of metabolic reactions in living cells without being consumed in the process. Life as we know it would not be possible without enzymes, as most biochemical reactions would occur too slowly to sustain life.

Key Characteristics of Enzymes

  • Catalytic Nature: They are highly efficient catalysts, increasing reaction rates dramatically.
  • Specificity: Each enzyme typically catalyzes only one specific reaction or a very small group of related reactions.
  • Protein Structure: Most enzymes are globular proteins. (Some RNA molecules, called ribozymes, also act as catalysts).
  • Regulation: Enzyme activity can be controlled by various factors, allowing the cell to regulate its metabolic pathways.

2.2 Enzyme Structure

The function of an enzyme is directly related to its complex three-dimensional structure.

2.2.1 Active Site

The active site is a specific region on the enzyme's surface where the substrate binds and the chemical reaction occurs. It is a small, 3D pocket or groove with a specific shape and charge distribution that is complementary to its substrate.

2.2.2 Co-factors

Some enzymes require a non-protein component, called a co-factor, to be active. The protein part of such an enzyme is called an apoenzyme, and the complete, active enzyme with its co-factor is a holoenzyme.

2.3 Mechanism of Enzyme Action

Enzymes work by lowering the activation energy of a reaction‚Äîthe minimum energy required to start the reaction. This makes it easier for reactants (substrates) to be converted into products.

2.3.1 Models of Enzyme Action

  • Lock and Key Model: Proposed by Emil Fischer, this model suggests the active site has a rigid shape that is perfectly complementary to the substrate, like a lock and its key.
  • Induced Fit Model: A more modern and accepted model proposed by Koshland. It suggests the active site is flexible. The binding of the substrate induces a conformational change in the enzyme, resulting in a tighter, more precise fit.

2.4 Factors Affecting Enzyme Action

The rate of an enzyme-catalyzed reaction is influenced by several factors.

Key Factors

  • Temperature: As temperature increases, the reaction rate increases up to an optimum temperature. Beyond this point, the enzyme begins to denature (lose its shape and function), and the rate rapidly decreases.
  • pH: Each enzyme has an optimum pH at which it is most active. Extreme pH values can cause denaturation.
  • Substrate Concentration: As substrate concentration increases, the reaction rate increases until the enzyme's active sites become saturated with substrate. At this point, the rate plateaus at its maximum velocity (\(V_{max}\)).
  • Enzyme Concentration: If substrate is in excess, the reaction rate is directly proportional to the enzyme concentration.

2.5 Enzyme Inhibition

Enzyme inhibition occurs when a molecule, called an inhibitor, binds to an enzyme and decreases its activity. Inhibition can be reversible or irreversible.

Type of InhibitionDescription
Competitive InhibitionThe inhibitor has a shape similar to the substrate and competes for the same active site. Can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration.
Non-competitive InhibitionThe inhibitor binds to a different site on the enzyme (an allosteric site), changing the shape of the active site and making it less effective.
Feedback InhibitionA metabolic control mechanism where the end product of a pathway acts as an inhibitor for an enzyme earlier in the pathway, preventing overproduction.

Chapter Summary

  • Enzymes are highly specific protein catalysts that speed up biochemical reactions by lowering their activation energy.
  • The active site is the region where the substrate binds. The Induced Fit model describes how the active site can change shape upon substrate binding for a better fit.
  • Some enzymes require non-protein co-factors (activators, coenzymes, prosthetic groups) to function.
  • Enzyme activity is affected by temperature, pH, and the concentrations of both the enzyme and the substrate.
  • Enzyme inhibition (competitive or non-competitive) is the process of reducing enzyme activity, which is a key mechanism for regulating metabolic pathways.
