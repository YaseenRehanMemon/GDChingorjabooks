13.1 Introduction to Immunity

The immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs that work together to defend the body against pathogenic organisms and other foreign invaders. Immunity is the body's ability to resist infection and disease.

Two Branches of the Immune System

  • Innate Immune System: Provides a non-specific, immediate defense. It is the first and second line of defense.
  • Adaptive (Acquired) Immune System: A highly specific defense that develops after exposure to a pathogen. It has memory, allowing for a faster and stronger response to subsequent infections. This is the third line of defense.

13.2 First Line of Defense (Innate Immunity)

The first line of defense consists of physical and biochemical barriers designed to prevent pathogens from entering the body.

  • Physical Barriers:
    • Skin: An intact, multi-layered physical barrier.
    • Mucous Membranes: Line the digestive, respiratory, and urinary tracts, trapping microbes.
    • Ciliated Epithelium: Cilia in the respiratory tract sweep mucus and trapped particles upward.
  • Biochemical Barriers:
    • Saliva, Mucus, Tears: Contain lysozyme, an enzyme that breaks down bacterial cell walls.
    • Stomach Acid (HCl): Creates a highly acidic environment that kills most pathogens.

13.3 Second Line of Defense (Innate Immunity)

If pathogens breach the first line of defense, the second line of non-specific defense is activated.

Key Components of the Second Line

  • Phagocytes: White blood cells like neutrophils and macrophages that engulf and digest pathogens.
  • Natural Killer (NK) Cells: Lymphocytes that can detect and destroy virally infected cells and tumor cells.
  • Inflammatory Response: A localized response to injury or infection characterized by redness, heat, swelling, and pain. It helps to contain the infection and recruit immune cells to the area.
  • Protective Proteins: Includes complement proteins that can lyse pathogens, and interferons that help prevent the spread of viral infections.
  • Fever: An elevated body temperature that can inhibit the growth of some pathogens and enhance immune responses.

13.4 Third Line of Defense (Adaptive Immunity)

The adaptive immune system provides a specific, targeted response to particular pathogens and has the crucial feature of memory.

Recognition of Self vs. Non-self

The adaptive immune system can distinguish between the body's own cells ("self") and foreign invaders ("non-self") by recognizing molecules called antigens. Antigens are typically proteins or polysaccharides on the surface of pathogens.

Key Players in Adaptive Immunity

The main cells of the adaptive immune system are lymphocytes: T cells and B cells.

Cell-Mediated Immunity (CMI)

Primarily involves T lymphocytes (T cells).

  • Helper T Cells (TH): Recognize antigens presented by other immune cells and activate B cells and cytotoxic T cells.
  • Cytotoxic T Cells (TC): Directly attack and kill infected body cells and tumor cells.

Humoral Immunity (Antibody-Mediated Immunity)

Primarily involves B lymphocytes (B cells) and the antibodies they produce.

When a B cell is activated by an antigen (often with help from a TH cell), it differentiates into:

  • Plasma Cells: Secrete large quantities of antibodies into the blood and lymph.
  • Memory Cells: Long-lived cells that provide long-term immunity.

Active vs. Passive Immunity

  • Active Immunity: Develops when your own body produces antibodies in response to an infection (natural) or vaccination (artificial). It is long-lasting.
  • Passive Immunity: Results from receiving antibodies from another source, such as from mother to baby through the placenta (natural) or through an injection of immune serum (artificial). It is temporary.

13.5 Immune System Disorders

  • Allergies: An exaggerated immune response to a normally harmless substance (allergen).
  • Autoimmune Diseases: The immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis).
  • Immunodeficiency: A condition where the immune system's ability to fight infectious disease is compromised or entirely absent (e.g., AIDS, caused by HIV).
  • Transplant Rejection: The immune system recognizes a transplanted organ as foreign and attacks it.

Chapter Summary

  • The immune system is divided into innate (non-specific) and adaptive (specific) branches.
  • First Line of Defense: Physical and chemical barriers like skin and mucous membranes.
  • Second Line of Defense: Non-specific responses including phagocytes, NK cells, inflammation, and fever.
  • Third Line of Defense (Adaptive Immunity): A specific response to antigens involving lymphocytes.
    • Humoral Immunity: B cells produce antibodies.
    • Cell-Mediated Immunity: T cells (Helper and Cytotoxic) manage and carry out direct attacks.
  • Immunity can be active (your body makes antibodies) or passive (you receive antibodies).
  • Immune system disorders include allergies, autoimmune diseases, and immunodeficiencies.
