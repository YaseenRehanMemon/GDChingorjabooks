11.1 Introduction to Holozoic Nutrition

Holozoic nutrition is a type of heterotrophic nutrition characterized by the ingestion of solid or liquid organic food, which is then digested, absorbed, and assimilated. It consists of five main stages:

The 5 Stages of Holozoic Nutrition

  1. Ingestion: The process of taking food into the body.
  2. Digestion: The breakdown of large, complex food molecules into smaller, soluble ones.
  3. Absorption: The uptake of digested food molecules into the cells of the body.
  4. Assimilation: The utilization of absorbed food molecules for energy, growth, and repair.
  5. Egestion: The removal of undigested waste from the body.

11.2 Types of Digestion

Digestion can be classified based on where it occurs and the mechanisms involved.

  • Intracellular Digestion: Digestion occurs inside the cell within a food vacuole (e.g., Amoeba).
  • Extracellular Digestion: Digestion occurs outside the cells, within a specialized digestive tract or cavity (e.g., humans, Planaria).
  • Mechanical Digestion: The physical breakdown of food into smaller pieces (e.g., chewing, churning in the stomach).
  • Chemical Digestion: The chemical breakdown of food molecules by enzymes.

Digestive Systems

  • Sac-like (Incomplete): A digestive system with only one opening that serves as both mouth and anus (e.g., Planaria).
  • Tube-like (Complete): A digestive system with two openings, a mouth for ingestion and an anus for egestion (e.g., humans).

11.3 The Human Digestive System (Alimentary Canal)

The human digestive system is a long, tube-like alimentary canal extending from the mouth to the anus, along with several accessory glands.

Major Organs of the Alimentary Canal

  • Mouth and Oral Cavity: Where ingestion and mechanical digestion (chewing) begin. Salivary amylase starts carbohydrate digestion.
  • Pharynx and Esophagus: Passageways that transport food from the mouth to the stomach via peristalsis.
  • Stomach: A muscular sac that churns food. It secretes hydrochloric acid (HCl) to kill bacteria and pepsin to begin protein digestion. The semi-digested food paste is called chyme.
  • Small Intestine (Duodenum, Jejunum, Ileum): The primary site for chemical digestion and absorption of nutrients. Its large surface area is due to folds, villi, and microvilli.
  • Large Intestine (Colon, Rectum, Anal Canal): Primarily responsible for the reabsorption of water and electrolytes, and the formation and storage of feces.

11.4 Role of Accessory Glands

Accessory glands secrete digestive juices into the alimentary canal.

  • Salivary Glands: Produce saliva, containing mucus for lubrication and amylase for starch digestion.
  • Liver: The largest gland, produces bile, which emulsifies fats (breaks large fat globules into smaller ones). The bile is stored in the gall bladder.
  • Pancreas: Secretes pancreatic juice containing a variety of powerful enzymes (amylase, trypsin, lipase) to digest carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. It also produces bicarbonate to neutralize stomach acid.

11.5 Disorders of the Digestive System

Various conditions can affect the digestive system.

  • Ulcer: A sore in the lining of the stomach or duodenum, often caused by the bacterium H. pylori or overuse of certain medications.
  • Food Poisoning: Illness caused by consuming food contaminated with bacteria, viruses, or toxins.
  • Dyspepsia (Indigestion): Discomfort or pain in the upper abdomen.
  • Obesity: The accumulation of excessive body fat, increasing the risk of many health problems.
  • Anorexia Nervosa & Bulimia Nervosa: Serious psychological eating disorders.

Chapter Summary

  • Holozoic Nutrition involves ingestion, digestion, absorption, assimilation, and egestion.
  • Digestion can be intracellular or extracellular, and involves both mechanical and chemical processes.
  • The Human Alimentary Canal is a complete digestive tract including the mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, and large intestine.
  • Accessory Glands (salivary glands, liver, pancreas) secrete enzymes and other substances like bile to aid in digestion.
  • The Small Intestine is where most chemical digestion and absorption occur, aided by secretions from the pancreas and liver.
  • The Large Intestine's primary role is water reabsorption.
