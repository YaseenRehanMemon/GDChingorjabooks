7.1 Kingdom Protoctista

The Kingdom Protoctista is a diverse and varied group of eukaryotic organisms. It is often considered a "catch-all" kingdom for eukaryotes that are not plants, animals, or fungi. They are mostly unicellular, but some are colonial or simple multicellular organisms.

7.2 Major Groups of Protoctista

Protoctists are broadly categorized based on their similarities to other kingdoms.

Plant-like Protoctists (Algae)

Algae are photosynthetic protoctists, often called the "grass of the water." They can be unicellular, colonial, or multicellular. They are classified based on their photosynthetic pigments.

Example: Ulva (sea lettuce), which exhibits an alternation of generations between a haploid gametophyte and a diploid sporophyte.

Fungi-like Protoctists (Slime Molds and Water Molds)

These organisms are heterotrophic absorbers. Slime molds are amoeboid and move, while water molds (Oomycetes) have filamentous hyphae similar to fungi.

Example: Phytophthora infestans, a water mold that causes late blight of potato.

Animal-like Protoctists (Protozoa)

Protozoa are unicellular, heterotrophic organisms that ingest their food. They are classified based on their method of locomotion.

  • Sarcodina (Rhizopoda): Move using pseudopodia (e.g., Amoeba).
  • Flagellata (Mastigophora): Move using flagella (e.g., Trypanosoma).
  • Ciliata (Ciliophora): Move using cilia (e.g., Paramecium).
  • Sporozoa: Non-motile parasites (e.g., Plasmodium, which causes malaria).

7.3 Kingdom Fungi

Fungi are a large, successful group of eukaryotic organisms that are primarily multicellular (except for yeasts). The study of fungi is called mycology.

7.3.1 General Characteristics

  • Nutrition: Fungi are absorptive heterotrophs. They secrete digestive enzymes into their surroundings and then absorb the resulting nutrients.
  • Structure: The body of a fungus is called a mycelium, which is a network of filamentous threads called hyphae. Hyphae can be septate (with cross-walls) or aseptate (coenocytic).
  • Cell Wall: Fungal cell walls are made of chitin, a strong and flexible polysaccharide.
  • Reproduction: Fungi can reproduce both asexually (through spores, conidia, or budding) and sexually.

Importance of Fungi

  • Decomposers: Fungi are essential for recycling nutrients in ecosystems by breaking down dead organic material.
  • Food and Drink: Used in baking (yeast), brewing, and as food (mushrooms).
  • Medicine: Source of important antibiotics, most famously penicillin.
  • Pathogens: Cause diseases in plants (e.g., rusts, smuts) and animals (e.g., ringworm, athlete's foot).
  • Mutualistic Relationships: Form vital symbiotic relationships like lichens (with algae) and mycorrhizae (with plant roots).

Chapter Summary

  • Kingdom Protoctista: A diverse group of eukaryotes including algae (plant-like), slime/water molds (fungi-like), and protozoa (animal-like).
  • Kingdom Fungi: A major kingdom of absorptive heterotrophs with chitin cell walls and bodies made of hyphae.
  • Fungal Diversity: Major groups include Zygomycota, Ascomycota, Basidiomycota, and Deuteromycota, primarily distinguished by their methods of sexual reproduction.
  • Ecological Roles: Both protoctists and fungi play critical roles as producers, decomposers, pathogens, and symbionts, significantly impacting human life and the environment.
