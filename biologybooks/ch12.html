12.1 Circulatory Systems

In complex multicellular animals, simple diffusion is not sufficient to transport substances over long distances. The circulatory system solves this problem by transporting fluid throughout the body, functionally connecting all cells to the organs responsible for exchange (like the lungs and intestines).

Open vs. Closed Circulatory Systems

  • Open Circulatory System: Blood (called hemolymph) is pumped by a heart into an open body cavity (the hemocoel), where it directly bathes the tissues. Found in arthropods and most mollusks.
  • Closed Circulatory System: Blood is confined within vessels (arteries, veins, capillaries) and is distinct from the interstitial fluid. This allows for more efficient transport and higher blood pressure. Found in annelids, some mollusks, and all vertebrates.

Single vs. Double Circuit Circulation

  • Single Circuit: Blood passes through the heart only once in a complete circuit (e.g., in fish).
  • Double Circuit: Blood is pumped through the heart twice in one circuit. This involves a pulmonary circuit (to the lungs) and a systemic circuit (to the rest of the body). Found in amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.

12.2 The Human Heart

The human heart is a four-chambered muscular pump responsible for circulating blood throughout the body. It is enclosed in a protective sac called the pericardium.

12.2.1 Structure of the Heart

  • Chambers: Two upper, thin-walled atria (right and left) that receive blood, and two lower, thick-walled ventricles (right and left) that pump blood out.
  • Valves: Ensure one-way blood flow. The atrioventricular (AV) valves (tricuspid and bicuspid/mitral) are between atria and ventricles. The semilunar valves are at the exit of the ventricles.

12.2.2 The Cardiac Cycle

The cardiac cycle is the sequence of events during one heartbeat. It consists of two main phases:

  • Systole: The contraction phase of the heart chambers.
  • Diastole: The relaxation phase of the heart chambers.

The "lub-dub" sound of the heartbeat is caused by the closing of the AV valves (lub) and the semilunar valves (dub).

12.2.3 Control of the Heartbeat

The heartbeat is controlled by a specialized conduction system:

  1. The Sinoatrial (SA) node, or the "pacemaker," located in the right atrium, initiates the electrical impulse.
  2. The impulse spreads across the atria, causing them to contract.
  3. The impulse reaches the Atrioventricular (AV) node, where it is briefly delayed.
  4. The impulse then travels down the bundle of His and through the Purkinje fibers, causing the ventricles to contract from the bottom up.

12.2.4 Electrocardiogram (ECG)

An ECG is a recording of the electrical activity of the heart. It shows distinct waves (P, QRS, T) that correspond to the depolarization and repolarization of the atria and ventricles.

12.3 Blood Vessels

Blood travels through a network of vessels that form a closed loop.

VesselFunctionStructure
Arteries & ArteriolesCarry blood away from the heart.Thick, muscular, and elastic walls to withstand high pressure.
CapillariesThe site of exchange between blood and tissues.Extremely thin walls (one cell thick) to allow for efficient diffusion.
Veins & VenulesCarry blood toward the heart.Thinner walls than arteries; contain one-way valves to prevent backflow of blood under low pressure.

12.4 Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is the hydrostatic force of blood exerted on the walls of the vessels. It is highest in the arteries and lowest in the veins.

Systolic and Diastolic Pressure

Blood pressure is measured as two numbers (e.g., 120/80 mmHg):

  • Systolic Pressure (Top Number): The pressure in the arteries during ventricular contraction (systole).
  • Diastolic Pressure (Bottom Number): The pressure in the arteries during ventricular relaxation (diastole).

12.5 The Lymphatic System

The lymphatic system is a network of vessels and organs that returns excess interstitial fluid (called lymph) to the bloodstream. It is a crucial part of the immune system.

12.6 Cardiovascular Disorders

Disorders of the heart and blood vessels are a leading cause of death worldwide.

  • Hypertension: High blood pressure.
  • Atherosclerosis: The hardening and narrowing of arteries due to the buildup of plaque (cholesterol, fats, etc.).
  • Thrombus: A blood clot that forms within a blood vessel.
  • Heart Attack: Damage or death of heart muscle due to a blockage in a coronary artery.

Chapter Summary

  • Circulatory Systems can be open or closed, and have single or double circuits.
  • The Human Heart is a four-chambered pump. The cardiac cycle involves systole (contraction) and diastole (relaxation), controlled by an electrical conduction system starting at the SA node.
  • Blood Vessels: Arteries carry blood away from the heart, veins carry it toward the heart, and capillaries are the site of exchange.
  • Blood Pressure is the force of blood against vessel walls, measured as systolic over diastolic pressure.
  • The Lymphatic System returns excess fluid to the blood and is a key part of the immune system.
  • Cardiovascular Disorders include hypertension and atherosclerosis, which can lead to heart attacks and strokes.
