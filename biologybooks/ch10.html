10.1 Nutrition in Plants

Plants are photosynthetic autotrophs, meaning they produce their own food. In addition to light, carbon dioxide, and water, they require various mineral nutrients from the soil for healthy growth.

Macronutrients vs. Micronutrients

  • Macronutrients: Required in large amounts (e.g., Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium, Calcium, Magnesium, Sulphur).
  • Micronutrients (Trace Elements): Required in small amounts (e.g., Iron, Manganese, Zinc, Copper).

10.1.1 Carnivorous Plants

Some plants, like the Venus flytrap, are carnivorous. They have adapted to live in nitrogen-deficient soils by trapping and digesting insects to supplement their nitrogen supply.

10.3 Transport in Plants

Vascular plants have specialized tissues, xylem and phloem, for long-distance transport.

10.3.1 Water Transport (Xylem)

Water and dissolved minerals are absorbed by the roots and transported upward to the rest of the plant through the xylem. This movement is driven by the Cohesion-Tension Theory:

Cohesion-Tension Theory

  1. Transpiration: Water evaporates from the leaves through stomata, creating a negative pressure potential (tension).
  2. Cohesion: Water molecules stick to each other (due to hydrogen bonds), forming a continuous column in the xylem.
  3. Adhesion: Water molecules stick to the walls of the xylem.
  4. Pulling Force: The tension created by transpiration pulls the entire column of water up from the roots.

10.3.2 Sugar Transport (Phloem)

Sugars produced during photosynthesis are transported from a source (e.g., a mature leaf) to a sink (e.g., a root, fruit, or developing leaf) through the phloem.

Pressure-Flow Hypothesis

This model explains phloem transport:

  1. Loading: Sugar is actively transported into the phloem at the source, making the sap highly concentrated.
  2. Water Entry: Water moves from the xylem into the phloem by osmosis, creating high turgor pressure.
  3. Bulk Flow: This high pressure pushes the sap through the phloem from the source to the sink.
  4. Unloading: Sugar is actively transported out of the phloem at the sink, and water follows by osmosis, returning to the xylem.

10.4 Homeostasis in Plants

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment. Plants achieve this through various adaptations, particularly for water balance (osmoregulation).

  • Hydrophytes: Plants adapted to live in water.
  • Mesophytes: Plants adapted to moderate water conditions.
  • Xerophytes: Plants adapted to arid (dry) conditions, with features like thick cuticles, sunken stomata, and deep roots to conserve water.

10.5 Growth and Responses in Plants

10.5.1 Plant Hormones (Growth Regulators)

Plant growth and development are controlled by chemical messengers called hormones.

HormonePrimary Function(s)
AuxinsPromote cell elongation, apical dominance, root formation.
GibberellinsPromote stem elongation, germination, fruit development.
CytokininsPromote cell division, inhibit senescence (aging).
Abscisic Acid (ABA)Inhibits growth, closes stomata during water stress, promotes dormancy.
Ethene (Ethylene)A gaseous hormone that promotes fruit ripening and abscission (leaf fall).

10.5.2 Growth Responses (Tropisms)

A tropism is a directional growth response to an external stimulus.

  • Phototropism: Growth in response to light.
  • Geotropism (Gravitropism): Growth in response to gravity (shoots grow up, roots grow down).
  • Thigmotropism: Growth in response to touch (e.g., a vine coiling around a support).

10.5.3 Photoperiodism and Vernalization

  • Photoperiodism: A plant's response to the relative lengths of day and night, which often controls flowering. Plants can be classified as long-day, short-day, or day-neutral.
  • Vernalization: The promotion of flowering by exposure to a prolonged period of cold.

Chapter Summary

  • Plant Nutrition: Plants require light, water, CO‚ÇÇ, and mineral nutrients (macronutrients and micronutrients) for growth.
  • Gaseous Exchange: Occurs through stomata, regulated by guard cells to balance gas needs with water conservation.
  • Transport: Water moves up through the xylem via transpiration, cohesion, and adhesion. Sugars move through the phloem from source to sink via the pressure-flow mechanism.
  • Homeostasis: Plants regulate their internal environment, especially water balance, with adaptations categorized as hydrophytic, mesophytic, or xerophytic.
  • Growth & Responses: Regulated by hormones (auxins, gibberellins, etc.) and directional responses to stimuli called tropisms. Flowering can be triggered by day length (photoperiodism) or cold treatment (vernalization).
