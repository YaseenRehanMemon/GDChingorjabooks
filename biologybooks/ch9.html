9.1 Characteristics of Animals

Animals are a diverse group of organisms that share several key characteristics. They are multicellular, eukaryotic, and heterotrophic (ingestive). Most animals are motile at some point in their lives, reproduce sexually, and lack cell walls.

9.2 Criteria for Animal Classification

Animals are classified based on their evolutionary relationships, which are inferred from their morphology, embryological development, and genetic makeup.

Key Classification Criteria

  • Presence of Tissues: Parazoa (e.g., sponges) lack true tissues, while Eumetazoa have them.
  • Body Symmetry: Can be asymmetrical, radially symmetrical (like a pie, e.g., cnidarians), or bilaterally symmetrical (with a left and right side, e.g., humans).
  • Number of Germ Layers: Diploblastic animals have two germ layers (ectoderm and endoderm), while triploblastic animals have a third layer, the mesoderm.
  • Body Cavity (Coelom): Triploblastic animals are classified based on their body cavity:
    • Acoelomates: Lack a body cavity (e.g., flatworms).
    • Pseudocoelomates: Have a body cavity partially lined by mesoderm (e.g., roundworms).
    • Coelomates: Have a true coelom, a body cavity completely lined by mesoderm (e.g., annelids, arthropods, chordates).
  • Embryological Development: Coelomates are further divided into Protostomes (where the blastopore becomes the mouth) and Deuterostomes (where the blastopore becomes the anus).

9.3 Major Invertebrate Phyla

Invertebrates are animals that lack a backbone, representing over 97% of all animal species.

EchinodermataEchinodermsRadially symmetrical as adults, deuterostomes, with a water vascular system and tube feet (e.g., starfish, sea urchins).

9.4 Phylum Chordata

Chordates are deuterostome animals characterized by four key features present at some stage of their development:

Four Hallmark Features of Chordates

  1. A notochord (a flexible dorsal rod).
  2. A dorsal, hollow nerve cord.
  3. Pharyngeal gill slits.
  4. A post-anal tail.

Vertebrates

Vertebrates are a subphylum of chordates where the notochord is replaced by a backbone of vertebrae. This group includes:

  • Jawless Fishes (Agnatha): e.g., lampreys.
  • Cartilaginous Fishes (Chondrichthyes): e.g., sharks, rays.
  • Bony Fishes (Osteichthyes): The most numerous group of vertebrates.
  • Amphibia: First vertebrates on land, still tied to water for reproduction (e.g., frogs, salamanders).
  • Reptilia: Evolved the amniotic egg, allowing for reproduction on land (e.g., snakes, turtles, crocodiles).
  • Aves (Birds): Evolved from reptiles, adapted for flight with feathers, hollow bones, and are endothermic (warm-blooded).
  • Mammalia (Mammals): Have hair and mammary glands, and are endothermic. Includes monotremes (egg-laying), marsupials (pouched), and placentals.

Chapter Summary

  • Animals are multicellular, heterotrophic eukaryotes lacking cell walls.
  • They are classified based on symmetry, number of germ layers, presence of a coelom, and embryological development (protostome vs. deuterostome).
  • Invertebrates are a vast group including phyla like Porifera, Cnidaria, Platyhelminthes, Nematoda, Annelida, Mollusca, Arthropoda, and Echinodermata.
  • Chordates possess a notochord, dorsal nerve cord, pharyngeal slits, and a post-anal tail at some life stage.
  • Vertebrates, a subphylum of chordates, have a backbone and include classes like fishes, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals, each with unique adaptations.
