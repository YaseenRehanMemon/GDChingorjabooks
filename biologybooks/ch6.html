6.1 Taxonomy of Prokaryotes

Prokaryotes are the most primitive and first inhabitants on Earth. They are single-celled organisms that lack a true nucleus and other membrane-bound organelles. Modern classification, based on ribosomal RNA sequences, divides all life into three domains, two of which are prokaryotic.

The Three Domains of Life

  1. Domain Archaea: Prokaryotes often found in extreme environments.
  2. Domain Bacteria: The more common and well-known prokaryotes.
  3. Domain Eukarya: All organisms with eukaryotic cells (protists, fungi, plants, animals).

6.2 Domains Archaea and Bacteria

Domain Archaea

Archaea are known for living in extreme habitats. While they share a prokaryotic cell structure with bacteria, they have unique genetic and metabolic characteristics that are closer to eukaryotes.

  • Thermophiles: Live in hot springs.
  • Halophiles: Live in high-salt environments.
  • Methanogens: Produce methane gas and are found in anaerobic conditions like marshes and animal guts.

Domain Bacteria

Bacteria are incredibly diverse and are found in nearly every habitat on Earth. They play crucial roles as decomposers, pathogens, and symbionts.

Gram Staining

Bacteria are often classified based on their cell wall structure using a technique called Gram staining:

  • Gram-positive bacteria: Have a thick peptidoglycan cell wall and stain purple.
  • Gram-negative bacteria: Have a thin peptidoglycan cell wall and an outer membrane. They stain pink or red.

6.3 Structure of Bacteria

Bacterial cells, while simple, have a well-defined structure.

Key Structural Components

  • Cell Wall: Provides shape and protection, composed of peptidoglycan.
  • Plasma Membrane: Regulates passage of materials.
  • Cytoplasm: Contains the nucleoid, ribosomes, and other components.
  • Nucleoid: A region containing a single, circular chromosome (the bacterial DNA).
  • Ribosomes: Site of protein synthesis (70S in size).
  • Appendages: May include flagella (for motility), pili (for attachment and conjugation), and capsules/slime layers (for protection).
  • Endospores: Some bacteria can form a dormant, highly resistant structure called an endospore to survive harsh conditions.

Shapes of Bacteria

  • Cocci: Spherical (e.g., *Streptococcus*).
  • Bacilli: Rod-shaped (e.g., *E. coli*).
  • Spirilla: Spiral-shaped (e.g., *Helicobacter pylori*).

6.4 Nutrition, Growth, and Reproduction

Modes of Nutrition

  • Autotrophs: Produce their own food.
    • Photoautotrophs: Use light energy (e.g., cyanobacteria).
    • Chemoautotrophs: Use energy from inorganic chemicals.
  • Heterotrophs: Obtain organic molecules from other organisms (e.g., saprotrophs, parasites).

Growth in Bacteria

Bacterial growth refers to an increase in the number of cells in a population, which typically occurs exponentially. The growth curve has four phases: Lag, Log (Exponential), Stationary, and Death.

Reproduction and Genetic Recombination

  • Binary Fission: The primary mode of asexual reproduction, where a cell divides into two identical daughter cells.
  • Genetic Recombination: Bacteria can exchange genetic material through:
    • Conjugation: DNA transfer through a pilus.
    • Transformation: Uptake of DNA from the environment.
    • Transduction: DNA transfer via a bacteriophage.

6.5 Importance of Bacteria

Bacteria are vital to the biosphere and have significant impacts on human life.

Beneficial Roles

  • Decomposers: They are essential for recycling nutrients in ecosystems.
  • Nitrogen Fixation: Convert atmospheric nitrogen into forms usable by plants.
  • Food Production: Used to make yogurt, cheese, and vinegar.
  • Biotechnology: Used in medicine (e.g., producing insulin) and genetic engineering.

Harmful Roles

  • Pathogens: Cause a wide range of diseases in humans, animals, and plants (e.g., cholera, tuberculosis, pneumonia).
  • Food Spoilage: Cause food to decay and become unpalatable.

Chapter Summary

  • Prokaryotes are divided into two domains: Archaea (often extremophiles) and Bacteria (widespread).
  • They lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles but have a cell wall, plasma membrane, ribosomes, and a nucleoid region with a circular chromosome.
  • Bacteria are classified by shape (cocci, bacilli, spirilla) and cell wall structure (Gram-positive/negative).
  • They exhibit diverse modes of nutrition (autotrophic and heterotrophic) and reproduce primarily through binary fission.
  • Genetic diversity is achieved through conjugation, transformation, and transduction.
  • Bacteria play crucial roles as decomposers, pathogens, and in biotechnology.
