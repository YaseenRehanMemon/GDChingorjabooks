3.1 Cell Theory and Techniques

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms. The Cell Theory is a foundational principle of biology, stating that all living things are composed of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, and all cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Techniques in Cell Biology

Studying cells requires specialized techniques:

  • Microscopy: Using light or electron microscopes to magnify cells and their internal structures.
  • Cell Fractionation: A process of breaking cells open and separating their organelles by centrifugation, allowing for the study of individual components.

3.2 Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

All life can be divided into two fundamental types of cells based on their complexity and structure.

FeatureProkaryotic CellEukaryotic Cell
NucleusNo true nucleus; genetic material is in a nucleoid region.True nucleus with a nuclear membrane.
OrganellesNo membrane-bound organelles.Contains membrane-bound organelles (mitochondria, ER, Golgi, etc.).
SizeGenerally smaller and simpler.Generally larger and more complex.
ExamplesBacteria, Archaea.Plants, Animals, Fungi, Protists.

3.3 The Cell Boundary

3.3.1 Plasma Membrane

The plasma membrane is a selectively permeable barrier that surrounds every cell. According to the Fluid Mosaic Model, it is composed of a flexible phospholipid bilayer with various proteins embedded within it.

Functions of the Plasma Membrane

  • Regulates the passage of substances into and out of the cell.
  • Receives signals from the environment.
  • Allows for cell-to-cell communication and identification.

3.3.2 Cell Wall

The cell wall is a rigid outer layer found in plants, fungi, and bacteria, located outside the plasma membrane. It provides structural support, protection, and prevents the cell from bursting in a hypotonic environment.

3.4 Cytoplasm and Organelles

The cytoplasm is the entire content within the plasma membrane, excluding the nucleus. It consists of the gel-like cytosol and various organelles, each with a specific function.

Key Eukaryotic Organelles

  • Nucleus: Contains the cell's genetic material (DNA) and controls all cell activities.
  • Ribosomes: Not membrane-bound; responsible for protein synthesis.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): A network of membranes. The Rough ER (with ribosomes) processes proteins, while the Smooth ER synthesizes lipids and detoxifies substances.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for secretion or delivery to other organelles.
  • Lysosomes: Contain digestive enzymes to break down waste materials, cellular debris, and ingested particles.
  • Mitochondria: The "powerhouses" of the cell; site of cellular respiration and ATP production.
  • Chloroplasts: (In plants and algae) Site of photosynthesis, converting light energy into chemical energy.
  • Vacuole: A large, membrane-bound sac. In plants, the central vacuole stores water and maintains turgor pressure.
  • Cytoskeleton: A network of protein fibers (microfilaments, intermediate filaments, microtubules) that provides structural support, cell shape, and facilitates movement.

Chapter Summary

  • Cells are the fundamental units of life, categorized as either simple prokaryotic cells (no nucleus) or complex eukaryotic cells (with a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles).
  • The plasma membrane, described by the fluid mosaic model, is a selective barrier for all cells. A cell wall provides additional support in plants, fungi, and bacteria.
  • The nucleus controls cell activities, while ribosomes synthesize proteins.
  • The endomembrane system (ER, Golgi, lysosomes) works together to synthesize, modify, package, and transport proteins and lipids.
  • Mitochondria are responsible for cellular respiration and ATP synthesis, while chloroplasts perform photosynthesis.
  • The cytoskeleton provides shape, support, and movement for the cell.
